# Digital Innovation in UK Probation and Prisons: Projects and Lessons

## Notable Digital Projects Supporting Rehabilitation and Desistance

In recent years, the UK’s Ministry of Justice (MoJ) and HM Prison & Probation Service (HMPPS) have launched several digital initiatives to help rehabilitate offenders and reduce re-offending. These projects span literacy and education, remote supervision, communication tools, and support services. Key examples include:

- **Turning Pages Digital (Shannon Trust)** – A new mobile app version of the Shannon Trust’s peer-mentoring literacy programme. Developed with a tech cooperative (Yalla) under the Prison Leavers Project, it allows prison leavers and others in the community to improve reading skills with help from trained mentors【6†L136-L142】【24†L391-L399】. This app expands the charity’s **Turning Pages** reading manuals into a digital tool, filling a literacy skills gap and boosting users’ confidence and employability – factors linked to lower recidivism【24†L391-L399】【6†L118-L125】. Initially trialed with 300 offenders in Kent, Surrey and Sussex, it builds on evidence that prisoners engaged in education are significantly less likely to reoffend【6†L114-L122】.

- **“My Journey” Resettlement App** – A smartphone app developed by Swansea University, Legal Innovation Lab Wales, and the charity Include UK as part of the Prison Leavers Innovation Challenge. It links people leaving custody with community support services (for housing, addiction, mental health, etc.), helping them navigate life after prison【6†L157-L164】. **My Journey** was piloted with 300 prison leavers from HMP Swansea and HMP Parc, aiming to keep individuals “on the straight and narrow” by connecting them to vital services during the risky transition period【6†L157-L164】. Both *Turning Pages Digital* and *My Journey* emerged from a £1.45 million MoJ fund for tech solutions to cut reoffending, within the wider £20 million **Prison Leavers Project**【8†L164-L172】【6†L134-L142】.

- **Remote Check-In and Biometric Monitoring Pilot** – In 2025 the MoJ began piloting an AI-enabled remote supervision tool for probation. Offenders in four regions (South West, North West, East of England, and Kent/Surrey/Sussex) must periodically record short video “selfie” check-ins on their phones【16†L133-L140】【16†L115-L123】. Facial recognition AI verifies their identity from the video and prompts them to answer questions about recent activities or behaviour. The system flags any identity spoofing or concerning answers, triggering an instant alert to probation officers for follow-up【16†L115-L123】. This initiative – part of an £8 million “Plan for Change” tech drive – supplements traditional measures like GPS ankle tags and in-person visits, with the goal of strengthening oversight and compliance between appointments【16†L107-L115】【16†L133-L140】. Officials say it could reduce probation officer workload and prevent incidents by catching issues early, though its use of biometric surveillance has raised civil liberties concerns (e.g. around accuracy, bias and data protection – see **Ethics** below)【13†L102-L110】【16†L119-L127】. The pilot will be evaluated before any wider rollout【13†L93-L100】.

- **MyProbation “PoP” App (Unilink)** – A **Person on Probation** mobile application developed by Unilink (a justice tech vendor known for prison self-service kiosks) and piloted in 2023【12†L23-L32】【12†L42-L50】. The app is designed to make probation supervision more interactive and user-centered. It enables secure two-way communication between probation officers and service users, including the ability to receive or request appointment slots, send quick check-in messages or feedback, and handle routine requests digitally【12†L23-L32】. Users can also access important documents (e.g. appointment letters, licence conditions) and a resource **noticeboard** through the app【12†L42-L50】. By streamlining scheduling (e.g. easy rescheduling with automated reminders) and providing direct messaging, the aim is to improve engagement and give people on probation more agency in managing their rehabilitation【12†L23-L32】【10†L300-L308】. Early user research for the app was grounded in desistance theory – focusing on strengthening relationships and motivation. As one designer noted, probation is most effective “when built around relationships,” so digital tools should augment (not replace) the personal supervisor–supervisee connection【10†L280-L287】.

- **Breaking Free Online** – A **computer-assisted therapy (CAT)** platform targeting substance misuse recovery. Breaking Free provides digital cognitive-behavioural therapy modules for alcohol and drug dependence and is offered in some UK prisons and probation services as a rehabilitative resource【3†L338-L342】. The program helps individuals identify triggers, track their mood and progress, and develop coping skills, all through an interactive online interface【3†L338-L342】. By making therapy accessible on tablets or computers (with appropriate security), it extends support to those who may not engage in face-to-face programs. Users can earn **achievement “badges”** for reaching recovery milestones, which helps reinforce positive behaviour change. Digital therapeutics like Breaking Free complement traditional treatment and have been used as part of addiction programmes in prison settings. (Notably, digital delivery also allows providers to **monitor usage data** and outcomes, feeding into evaluation of what works to sustain recovery.)

- **“Changing Lives” App (Northern Ireland)** – A mobile app created by the Probation Board for Northern Ireland (PBNI) to support people on probation in the community【3†L346-L354】. The **Changing Lives App** serves as a pocket guide to probation, providing handy information on license requirements, mental health and addiction support, and other resettlement resources【3†L346-L354】. It also offers communication features – helping users stay connected to their probation officers and quickly find local services. While developed in NI, it reflects a broader trend in UK probation of using apps to improve client engagement and understanding of their obligations. Clear, user-friendly access to such information (e.g. “when and where you should be” to avoid breaches) can prevent unintentional non-compliance【2†L79-L87】【2†L39-L47】. By empowering service users with knowledge and 24/7 support at their fingertips, the app aims to reduce the risk of re-offending. (This aligns with HMPPS efforts in England/Wales too – for example, research has hypothesized that better explaining licence conditions and schedules to individuals with chaotic lives could avert needless recalls to prison【2†L77-L85】.)

- **Electronic Monitoring Technology** – The continued expansion of **GPS tagging and other monitoring wearables** is also part of the digital innovation landscape. Electronic monitoring has long been used in the UK (e.g. curfew ankle tags), but recent initiatives have broadened its scope. **Sobriety tags** (which monitor alcohol consumption through skin sweat) were introduced nationwide in 2021 to help tackle alcohol-fueled reoffending, and GPS location tags are increasingly used for higher-risk offenders to enforce exclusion zones or track movements in real time. The government’s latest *Sentencing Bill* and *Plan for Change* include the *“biggest ever expansion”* of tagging, with funding for tens of thousands more offenders to be tagged each year in the community【16†L149-L157】. The intent is to deter reoffending and quickly detect breaches of conditions. For example, HMPPS has piloted an “acquisitive crime” project comparing GPS trail data to reported crime locations, to proactively identify suspects wearing tags【15†L23-L27】【15†L33-L37】. While tagging is primarily a control measure, it supports rehabilitation indirectly by enabling initiatives like **home curfew instead of prison** (keeping individuals in the community where rehabilitative work can continue) and by mandating sobriety which can facilitate engagement in treatment. As with other tech, however, ethical use and robust evaluation of outcomes (e.g. proving that GPS data actually reduces reoffending rates) remain critical.

*In addition to the above, other innovative platforms are emerging:* For example, the social enterprise **Beam** has an online crowdfunding platform that helps prison leavers secure training, employment and housing by raising funds for each person’s tailored plan【18†L50-L58】【18†L79-L87】. Partner agencies (like probation services or local councils) can refer individuals via a simple web form, then track each referral’s progress on a dashboard with live updates on jobs and accommodation outcomes【18†L122-L130】. This kind of digital tool joins up support around the service user, while also giving the frontline staff a motivational feedback loop (seeing positive stories of people they helped)【18†L126-L134】. It represents how technology can *“build new networks”* and community support for offenders beyond traditional justice services【18†L85-L94】【18†L99-L107】. 

Overall, these examples illustrate a wave of digital transformation in offender rehabilitation – from educational apps and virtual mentoring, to smartphone check-ins and data-driven supervision. Many originated as **pilots or prototypes** – often co-developed with charities, universities or private vendors – and are being evaluated for wider rollout. Crucially, they complement (rather than replace) in-person probation and prison programs, aiming to *augment* the capacity of staff and engage offenders more effectively in their own journey to desistance.

## Lessons on Data-Sharing, Privacy Ethics, and Evaluation

The rollout of digital tools in prisons and probation has highlighted important lessons in data-sharing, ethics, and the need for rigorous evaluation:

- **Breaking Down Data Silos:**  A recurring challenge has been fragmented and outdated IT systems that don’t “talk” to each other【2†L39-L47】. Probation officers historically spent hours searching across multiple databases or emailing colleagues to get a full picture of an offender’s risks and needs【2†L39-L47】. Improving rehabilitation outcomes requires better information flow between prisons, probation, health services, and partner agencies. Several initiatives focus on creating a **“single view” of an individual** from custody through to community【2†L49-L57】. For instance, *Justice Digital* teams at MoJ are working to integrate case management systems so that data (like risk assessments, licence conditions, or intervention records) follows the person and is accessible in one place【2†L49-L57】. New apps have tried to address this gap on a micro level: the *Socrates* pilot app allowed prison medical providers (Care UK) to upload prisoners’ health records and upcoming community appointments, so that upon release individuals could easily share this info with community doctors【9†L319-L327】. Likewise, probation apps like *MyProbation* or NI’s *Changing Lives* app can serve as **information hubs**, giving service users and staff a shared repository of key documents, schedules and resources【12†L42-L50】【3†L346-L354】. The lesson is that thoughtful data-sharing (with proper security) can save time and “give time back” for meaningful engagement【2†L49-L57】, ensure continuity of care (e.g. consistent mental health treatment from prison to community【9†L319-L327】), and empower offenders with clarity about their responsibilities. However, it requires tackling interoperability and privacy issues upfront – for example, the Shannon Trust’s digital literacy tool had to be securely integrated with their existing CRM so that learner progress data could sync across platforms【28†L117-L126】【28†L123-L131】.

- **Privacy and Ethical Safeguards:**  Digital innovation brings **ethical dilemmas**, particularly around surveillance and consent. Tools that collect personal data or biometrics must balance public safety with individual rights【26†L79-L87】【26†L89-L97】. The AI face-recognition pilot exemplifies this tension: while officials tout it as “catapulting our analogue justice system into a new digital age” of efficient monitoring【16†L123-L131】, critics note the risks of algorithmic bias, misidentification, and unclear data retention policies in such a system【13†L102-L110】. Introducing biometric surveillance in probation raises questions like: How long are biometric data and video recordings stored? Who can access them? What if the AI flags someone in error? Ensuring **transparency, oversight and recourse** (appeal mechanisms) is crucial to maintain fairness and trust【13†L102-L110】. More broadly, probation digitisation must contend with **informed consent and privacy** for service users. Many rehabilitation apps ask individuals to log very personal information (health status, emotions, family ties, etc.), so robust data protection and opt-in usage are essential. HMI Probation’s digital ethics review emphasized that human rights must come first – digital services should be **inclusive, safe, and private by design**【3†L323-L330】. This means incorporating features like anonymized data analysis (where possible), encryption of sensitive data, and giving users control over what information is shared with their probation officer or others. Another ethical aspect is **digital exclusion**: a significant portion of offenders have low digital literacy or limited access to technology【3†L312-L319】. Thus, innovative projects have to ensure they *don’t* widen inequalities. Providing devices or internet access for those who lack them, and training individuals in basic digital skills, are recommended steps【3†L363-L370】【3†L325-L333】. Similarly, systems should be designed accessibly (simple interfaces, support for neurodiverse users, multiple language options, etc.)【28†L55-L63】【28†L73-L81】. In summary, the ethics lesson is to **“design for dignity”** – use tech to empower and support, not to disproportionately surveil or disadvantage. Clear policies around data use, security, and equitable access need to accompany any new digital tool in this space【26†L79-L87】【3†L323-L331】.

- **Continuous Evaluation and Evidence:**  A striking finding from academia is that despite many pilot projects, there remains a **“dearth of evidence”** on what digital tools actually reduce re-offending【14†L132-L139】. In 2019, an HM Inspectorate of Probation rapid evidence assessment on technology in probation concluded it was an “empty review” – virtually no rigorous studies met the criteria for inclusion【14†L101-L110】【14†L132-L139】. This doesn’t mean tech *doesn’t* work, but rather that it hadn’t been properly evaluated yet. The few available studies showed promising intermediate outcomes (like improved attendance or skill gains) but lacked long-term reoffending data【14†L103-L112】. Consequently, each new innovation should build an evaluation component in from the start. Encouragingly, recent pilots are being monitored: for example, the MoJ has stated the biometric check-in trial will be assessed before expansion【13†L93-L100】, and the Prison Leavers Project initiatives (like the apps) are likely subject to ongoing review. Academic partnerships can help – e.g. the *My Journey* app involved a university, and one might expect robust data collection on outcomes like how many users secure housing or jobs. We are also seeing qualitative feedback emerge: the Yalla Cooperative reported initial positive feedback from probation officers and service users using the Turning Pages Digital literacy tool, with users showing improved communication skills and confidence in employability【28†L134-L143】【28†L140-L148】. Going forward, combining such feedback with quantitative measures (reconviction rates, compliance rates, etc.) is vital. Another aspect of evaluation is understanding **what works for whom**. Technology might benefit certain groups (for instance, self-motivated individuals or those with mild needs) but not others, or it might introduce new risks (e.g. if a person in recovery finds a smartphone app triggering or stressful). Continuous user research, as advocated in HMI Probation’s Digital Probation report, is needed to iterate on these tools【3†L329-L336】. The best outcomes likely arise when digital solutions are co-designed with people on probation/prisoners and staff, aligning with desistance principles (personal motivation, pro-social connections, etc.). In short, there is consensus that digital innovation holds promise for rehabilitation, but **robust evidence** must guide its implementation. Building an evidence base (through pilots, academic evaluations, and sharing results widely) will help scale up the interventions that truly make a difference, and modify or discontinue those that don’t【14†L123-L131】【3†L331-L334】. As one probation director put it, we must ask: *“are we doing the right thing for the right people, and are we doing it quickly enough?”*【2†L66-L74】 – continual evaluation is how we answer that in the digital context.

## Opportunities for OTOS’s Digital Mentoring & Recovery-Tracking Model

Given the landscape above, **OTOS’s digital mentoring and recovery-tracking model** could be a valuable addition to the existing ecosystem of rehabilitation technology. OTOS (an acronym for *One-to-One Support*, hypothetically) appears to combine personal mentorship with digital progress tracking – a blend well-suited to current needs. Here are ways it might integrate or complement what’s in place:

- **Augmenting Relationship-Focused Supervision:** A core theme in successful probation tech is that it should *enhance* human relationships, not eliminate them【10†L280-L287】. OTOS’s mentoring component aligns perfectly with this principle. It could pair individuals with trained mentors (volunteers, peers, or professionals) accessible through an app or platform. This would complement the official supervision by providing additional encouragement, advice, and accountability between probation appointments. For instance, while the MyProbation app enables formal officer–client messaging, OTOS could facilitate more informal, supportive check-ins from a mentor who understands the person’s recovery journey. Such positive contact can bolster motivation and well-being, addressing needs that a busy probation officer may not always have time for. Integrating OTOS into the probation service could be as simple as **offering it as an approved intervention**: probation staff could refer eligible participants to the digital mentoring scheme, and with appropriate data-sharing agreements, mentors could update POs on progress or concerns. This creates a *“team around”* the service user, where technology links the mentor, mentee, and officer as needed (much like Beam’s platform links referral partners to client updates)【18†L122-L130】. By building personal trust and confidence through e-mentoring, OTOS can reinforce the advice and goals set by probation, thereby complementing official supervision with a more personal touch.

- **Filling the Aftercare and “Recovery Capital” Gap:** Many existing digital tools focus on specific domains (education, employment, mental health, etc.) or on compliance (e.g. check-ins, tagging). A digital recovery-tracking model like OTOS could fill a notable gap by providing holistic aftercare support, especially for those tackling substance abuse or other behavioural issues linked to reoffending. It could function similarly to a “digital sponsor” or accountability partner. For example, OTOS might allow users to log their daily achievements or struggles (e.g. days sober, meetings attended, triggers experienced) and track recovery metrics over time. These data points – sometimes called **“recovery capital”** – are valuable for showing progress beyond binary outcomes like pass/fail of a drug test. Currently, probation might lack a structured way to record these positives; OTOS could integrate by **sharing progress dashboards** with probation and treatment providers. With user consent, a probation officer could see at a glance the participant’s self-reported recovery journey (e.g. increasing days of abstinence, goals met, mentor notes) and tailor their support accordingly. This complements formal risk monitoring with a strengths-based perspective. It also contributes to data-driven rehabilitation: anonymized recovery data from OTOS could help HMPPS and researchers identify patterns (say, which activities correlate with sustained desistance) feeding into evidence-based practice. Crucially, any integration must respect privacy – perhaps giving service users control over which data to share. But if done ethically, this could enhance **inter-agency collaboration**: for instance, an OTOS app might alert both the mentor and probation officer if a user flags a serious relapse risk, enabling swift intervention (much like the AI check-in flags issues for immediate action【16†L119-L127】, but in a supportive rather than surveillance-oriented way).

- **Complementing Existing Platforms and Programs:** OTOS’s model should be seen as complementary, not competitive, to existing digital initiatives. There are several points of synergy:
  - *With educational and therapeutic apps:* OTOS mentors could encourage use of tools like Breaking Free (for addiction therapy) or Turning Pages (for literacy), and the recovery-tracking function could incorporate milestones from those programs (e.g. completing a therapy module or a book) as achievements. This creates an integrated user experience rather than a scattershot one. In effect, OTOS could act as the **“glue”** that connects various services – a mentor helping the individual navigate the array of support apps and real-world resources available, and a tracker that aggregates their progress in one place.
  - *With probation’s case management:* As HMPPS moves toward unified digital records, OTOS could feed into that “single view”. For instance, an OTOS mentor’s notes or the individual’s self-reported progress could be summarized and attached to the offender’s record in the probation case system (similar to how the Shannon Trust app now logs literacy progress and shares with the charity’s database)【28†L123-L131】. Even a simple two-way sync – where probation officers can see a read-only view of key recovery metrics and mentors can be alerted to significant events (like a change in supervision level or a missed appointment) – would greatly improve coordination. HMI Probation has explicitly recommended greater **collaboration with technology providers and community organisations** to enhance support for people on probation【3†L328-L336】. OTOS embodies this collaborative approach: it’s an outside innovation that can plug into statutory services. By involving probation staff in its design and rollout (co-design workshops, feedback loops), integration will be smoother and more welcomed by practitioners【3†L328-L336】.
  - *Within prison settings:* Although described as digital mentoring for recovery (likely post-release), OTOS could potentially be introduced “through-the-gate”. Many prisons have peer mentor schemes and recovery programmes; a tablet-based version of OTOS could help inmates preparing for release to establish a mentor relationship and begin tracking goals (e.g. enrolling in courses, staying discipline-free, attending AA/NA meetings in prison). Upon release, this record and support continue seamlessly in the community – ensuring continuity that is often lacking at the prison-probation transition.

- **Ensuring Ethical and Effective Deployment:** To maximize its complementarity, OTOS should heed the lessons outlined earlier. Data-sharing must be secure and consent-based, especially given the sensitive nature of recovery information. Mentors should be vetted and trained, and boundaries of the mentor-mentee relationship defined (e.g. what to do if someone discloses risk of harm – likely notify probation). On the positive side, OTOS offers a way to **humanize digital supervision** – balancing the MoJ’s high-tech surveillance initiatives with a supportive, person-centered intervention. This aligns with modern desistance research that highlights hope, human connection, and self-efficacy as keys to leaving crime behind【10†L280-L287】【18†L85-L94】. By integrating those elements through a digital platform, OTOS could help fill gaps that neither purely human services nor purely digital surveillance can address alone.

In conclusion, *OTOS’s digital mentoring & recovery-tracking model* could plug into the UK’s rehabilitation system as a versatile support layer. It would complement existing systems by focusing on the human side of reform – mentorship, motivation, and personal progress – delivered at scale via technology. The timing is ripe: HMPPS is actively seeking “bold ideas” and partnerships to reduce reoffending【16†L123-L131】, and there is growing recognition that **successful rehabilitation requires both toughness and trust**. OTOS can contribute to the trust and support aspect, working alongside the tougher enforcement tools. If integrated thoughtfully (perhaps as part of the next wave of innovation pilots or commissioned services), it stands to enhance the overall digital rehabilitation ecosystem – helping more individuals rebuild their lives and stay crime-free, while providing new data and insights to the system. In the spirit of the current reforms, it represents exactly the kind of collaborative, tech-enabled solution that can *“make our communities safer”* through rehabilitation-focused innovation【6†L123-L131】.
