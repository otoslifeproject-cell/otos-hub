Before the numbers — a person

I can detail and draw this ripple because I was the stone.

Every figure in this document describes a life. I know, because for years it described mine. Adult ADHD I didn't have language for, self-medicated with alcohol because it was the one thing that reliably steadied a brain reaching for regulation. Then the functional use crossed an invisible line into dependence — and the system saw the drink, treated the drink, and never once looked underneath it at the driver.

"Adult ADHD previously complicated with excess alcohol use to self-medicate inadequately managed ADHD symptoms."
— Independent consultant psychiatrist, clinical update letter, April 2026. That single sentence is the entire thesis: the cohort, the mechanism, and the misdirection of treatment.

The ripple below is not a spreadsheet exercise. It is A&E visits my family absorbed, the work that stopped, the people around me who thought I was choosing this. When the right diagnosis finally landed — in the right sequence, with the right medication underneath — the noise narrowed into signal, and the recovery work could finally hold. Medication did not do the recovery work. It made the recovery work reachable.

That is the whole reason OTOS exists. Not to treat anyone — clinical teams do that. But to hold the thread across the doors the way nobody held mine, so the next person's ripple gets caught before it spreads. Treat the driver. Hold the thread. Evidence what happened.

Official figures vs the real cost

The same person. The same revolving door. A different name on the file.

The official figure of £58,000–£65,000/year for a crack or heroin addict covers Tier 1 only — and only the identified addict. It captures nothing from Tier 2 (the family falling apart) or Tier 3 (the binman picking up the litter, the employer's HR team processing the absence, the child's CAMHS referral, the probation caseload, the GDP that was never generated).

A meaningful subgroup may be cycling through addiction because ADHD remains unidentified, untreated or under-treated — the same person, the same revolving door, the same cost chain. But because ADHD is never coded as the root cause, the cost is attributed to the symptom — the addiction — and never to the treatable condition underneath it.

The mechanism · a pipeline problem, not a "bad choices" problem

Block the door upstream, and you increase the harm downstream.

Some NHS areas are already restricting or pausing adult ADHD access under capacity pressure — with the clearest current example being adult Right-to-Choose restrictions for over-25s in Coventry & Warwickshire, alongside wider local pauses and activity caps elsewhere. The wider risk is the same: when adult ADHD access is blocked or rationed upstream, need is not removed; it is stored as future crisis. The consequence is a widening missing adult cohort: people who are never stabilised early, never properly formulated within services, and far more likely to accumulate trauma, exclusion and substance misuse.

1
Permanently undiagnosed — not "waiting", but structurally excluded. The need doesn't disappear.
2
Self-medicating as we speak — alcohol, drugs, compulsions — until they cross the invisible line into addiction. NIA forms from the ADHD self-medication where they were once genuinely stable.
3
Presenting downstream, repeatedly — A&E, CGL, CPFT, 111, crisis MH, safeguarding, housing, police, courts, probation, prison.
4
Treated for the symptom, never the cause — because ADHD is unrecognised — producing churn, relapse and escalating risk.
Over the next decade this produces a visible tsunami effect: costs that look "sudden" to commissioners, but were built slowly, upstream, by a blockage nobody costed at the time. Higher severity at presentation, more comorbidity, more instability, rising re-presentation — because the upstream condition was never addressed.

What OTOS argues — and what it does not claim

OTOS can't stop the wave. It can make the drift visible before crisis.

The point is structural, not clinical. OTOS is not claiming to diagnose or treat NIA. When services are siloed and access is blocked upstream, people drift between doors — and a continuity layer is what keeps the engagement thread alive across those handoffs, makes drift visible early, and provides commissioner-grade receipts of exactly where the pathway breaks.

Where this leaves us

The rings never stop. They just move to budgets that can't see the cause.

The ripple is what one untreated life costs the whole system right now. The tsunami is what happens when we build a decade of those lives on purpose, by blocking the door upstream. Both come back to the same, single, treatable driver — and the same quiet failure to hold the thread between the services that could catch it.

The one line

If adult ADHD diagnosis and treatment is throttled today, the system will pay tomorrow — in addiction, crisis, and cost. We are already paying. The only question is whether we pay upstream, once — or downstream, forever.
