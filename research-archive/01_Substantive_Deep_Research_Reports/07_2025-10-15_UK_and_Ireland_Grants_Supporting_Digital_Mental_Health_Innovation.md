# Funding Sources for Innovation in Mental Health (UK & Ireland)

Innovators in **digital mental health**, including areas like dual-diagnosis (e.g. concurrent ADHD and addiction), can tap into a mix of UK and Irish grants, foundations, and public funds. Below we outline key funding programs – from NHS initiatives to major charities – with their eligibility, typical funding, success measures, and examples of projects supported.

## NHS Innovation Accelerator (NIA)  
- **Eligibility:** Open to healthcare innovators (clinicians, academics, or SMEs) with an evidence-based solution addressing NHS priorities and ready for scaling. Applicants must have a proven innovation (often already piloted in health settings) and support from their organization. Both UK and international applicants are considered (with overseas companies supported to enter the NHS)【13†L68-L76】【13†L123-L131】.  
- **Funding Range:** The NIA is a **three-year fellowship program** rather than a traditional grant – it provides in-kind support (mentorship, networking, evaluation guidance) rather than large direct funding. Each cohort selects ~10–20 Fellows and may offer a small bursary or travel stipend, but the emphasis is on accelerating uptake across the NHS【13†L68-L76】.  
- **Success Metrics:** Success is measured by the **scale and impact** of the innovation in the health system. NIA tracks how widely solutions spread and their benefits to patients and staff – e.g. over **3,300 NHS sites** are now using NIA innovations, and alumni have collectively raised £218 million in external funding【13†L78-L86】. Key metrics include improved patient outcomes, cost savings, and adoption at scale within the NHS.  
- **Example Awardees:** *myHappymind* – a digital mental wellbeing curriculum for schools – was an NIA-supported innovation. It equips teachers and parents to build children’s resilience, and studies showed it cut CAMHS mental health referrals by ~43% in participating schools【10†L145-L153】. Another example is *Sleepio* (digital insomnia therapy), which scaled via NIA and is now used widely in the NHS. NIA has also backed platforms relevant to ADHD and mental health (e.g. digital coaching apps) to spread them nationally.  

## National Institute for Health and Care Research (NIHR)  
- **Eligibility:** NIHR is the UK’s leading health research funder. Its programs typically require a UK-based lead applicant (e.g. an NHS trust, university, small business or charity) often in collaboration. For innovation grants like **NIHR i4i (Invention for Innovation)**, eligible leads include higher-education institutes, NHS or social care providers, SMEs, or non-profits in the UK【19†L1-L9】. Multi-disciplinary teams (clinicians, academics, industry partners) are usually needed, and patient/public involvement is emphasized in proposals.  
- **Funding Range:** **Funding ranges widely** depending on the scheme. NIHR awards can be as small as ~£50k for feasibility studies up to multi-million-pound program grants. For example, NIHR’s i4i Fast Track funding offers ~£50k–£150k for rapid health tech projects【19†L7-L15】, while i4i Product Development Awards have no formal upper limit (often £500k–£1m+ for complex multi-year projects)【19†L1-L9】. Large NIHR programs (e.g. Programme Grants, Collaborations) can fund several millions over 3–5 years.  
- **Success Metrics:** NIHR focuses on **robust evidence and patient benefit**. Funded projects are expected to yield high-quality research (publications, clinical trial results) and practical improvements in healthcare. Key metrics include improved health outcomes, reduction in service pressures, and readiness for NHS adoption. For instance, NIHR’s recent mental health tech awards aimed to **reduce waiting times** for youth ADHD/autism assessments and improve early interventions【16†L223-L232】【16†L259-L267】. Success is seen in measurable impacts like shorter waitlists, better symptom management, or cost-effectiveness, which can support NHS implementation.  
- **Example Awardees:** In 2025 NIHR awarded **17 digital health projects** ~£1.5 million in total to tackle delays in children’s mental health care【16†L215-L224】. These included an AI-driven triage tool (*EnrichMyCare*) to spot early signs of autism/ADHD and route families to the right support, and other apps to connect mental and physical health services【16†L223-L232】【16†L269-L278】. NIHR has also funded trials of non-drug ADHD interventions (e.g. neurofeedback, digital therapies) and addiction treatment research. Such projects demonstrate NIHR’s support for innovation in dual-diagnosis and digital mental health, provided they generate solid evidence of efficacy.

## National Lottery Community Fund (UK)  
- **Eligibility:** The National Lottery Community Fund (NLCF) is the UK’s largest community grant-maker, open to a wide range of **non-profit entities** – including charities, community groups, social enterprises, and local partnerships. Funding is available across England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland【42†L100-L108】. Applications must serve a charitable purpose and typically need to show community involvement and clear social/health benefits. Small grassroots organizations are welcome (indeed, over 60% of NLCF grantees have annual incomes <£100k)【42†L111-L119】.  
- **Funding Range:** **Very broad.** NLCF awards **over £600 million** annually across ~11,000 grants【42†L105-L113】. Grants range from micro grants of **£300** (for small community activities) up to **multi-year investments over £1 million** for strategic initiatives【42†L111-L119】. In practice, the majority of awards are small (£1k–£10k) supporting local projects【42†L111-L119】, but larger grants (hundreds of thousands of pounds over several years) are given to projects with national or significant regional impact.  
- **Success Metrics:** NLCF prioritizes **community impact and wellbeing outcomes**. Success is measured by how projects improve lives and strengthen communities. For mental health-themed funding, the focus is often on prevention, resilience and early intervention – e.g. boosting young people’s emotional wellbeing to prevent serious issues【40†L39-L47】. Grantees are expected to report on outcomes like number of people supported, improvements in mental health/resilience, community engagement, and sustainability of the benefit. NLCF also values **user-led design** (“people in the lead”), expecting projects to involve those with lived experience in shaping solutions【40†L31-L39】.  
- **Example Awardees:** *WaitingWell* is a recent innovation funded by NLCF to support those on long NHS waitlists for neurodiversity assessments. Backed by a **£20,000 National Lottery grant**【21†L38-L47】, it provides free online guidance and mental health support for individuals (and families) awaiting ADHD or autism diagnosis – addressing the isolation and anxiety during the wait【21†L44-L53】. Another example is the **HeadStart program** (a £56m National Lottery initiative) which worked across six regions to improve adolescents’ mental health in schools【42†L135-L143】. Thousands of smaller community grants have also funded local addiction recovery cafés, ADHD youth clubs, and digital mental health peer support apps, demonstrating NLCF’s broad reach in this sector.  

## Wellcome Trust  
- **Eligibility:** The Wellcome Trust is a global charitable foundation that funds **research and innovation in health**. Its mental health funding calls are often open worldwide (excluding a few countries) and encourage **collaborative teams**. Typically, a university or research institute acts as lead applicant, partnering with others – for example, Wellcome’s digital mental health awards require a team that includes academic researchers *and* an organization capable of scaling the intervention (such as a tech company or non-profit)【23†L279-L288】【23†L290-L298】. Lived-experience experts (people with direct experience of the mental health issue) are also expected to be involved in the project design【23†L205-L213】.  
- **Funding Range:** Wellcome’s investments in mental health are substantial. For instance, recent **Mental Health Challenge Awards** have offered **£3–7 million per project over up to 5 years**【23†L259-L267】. Even initial “Discovery” phases often grant a few hundred-thousand pounds to build teams and proofs-of-concept【22†L5-L13】. Beyond UK-focused work, Wellcome funds international consortia and high-risk, high-reward science (with some major programs exceeding $10M). Overall, applicants can expect multi-year support that is considerably larger than typical charity grants – reflecting Wellcome’s commitment to big advances in mental health science.  
- **Success Metrics:** Wellcome is driven by **scientific and societal impact**. In mental health, its goal is to transform understanding and treatment of conditions like anxiety, depression, psychosis (and increasingly, areas like addiction). Key success metrics include generating new evidence (e.g. identifying *“active ingredients”* that make therapies effective【22†L1-L9】), validating interventions through rigorous trials, and ensuring solutions are scalable to reach large populations. Ultimately, a Wellcome-funded project should produce advances publishable in top journals *and* a clear pathway to improving patients’ lives (e.g. a validated digital therapy that can be widely deployed). For digital innovation calls, success might be measured by symptom reduction and functional improvement in target groups (such as demonstrable reductions in anxiety or depression scores through the digital intervention)【23†L205-L213】, and the ability to scale that intervention to thousands of users.  
- **Example Awardees:** Wellcome’s mental health portfolio includes cutting-edge projects. For example, it launched the **“Accelerating Digital Mental Health”** grant in 2022, funding teams to rigorously test tools like VR therapies and smartphone apps for early intervention in youth mental illness【23†L205-L213】. Separately, through its Wellcome Leap initiative, it committed **$50 million to an “Untangling Addiction” program** targeting new approaches to addiction treatment and measurement【43†L1-L8】. This has funded global researchers (including UK teams) to develop novel addiction interventions (e.g. predictive screening for opiate abuse, new digital biomarkers for substance use disorders). Another Wellcome-backed project in 2023 investigated how improving sleep via a digital program could help children with ADHD, receiving ~£2.3m in collaboration with UK universities【14†L19-L27】【14†L33-L41】. Such examples show Wellcome’s focus on high-impact research that can lead to **evidence-based innovations** in ADHD, addiction, and broader mental health.

## Comic Relief (UK)  
- **Eligibility:** Comic Relief is a UK charity funder that runs themed calls for proposals, often targeting **marginalized groups and community-level innovation**. Grants are typically open to UK-registered charities, social enterprises, and sometimes partnerships (including those working overseas in specific regions). Each funding initiative has specific focus – e.g. youth mental health, men’s mental wellbeing, sport-for-mental-health, etc. Applicants must demonstrate deep reach into disadvantaged or underserved communities and innovative approaches to address their needs【29†L175-L184】. Projects led by those with lived experience or that tackle inequalities often fit well. In the mental health realm, Comic Relief has prioritized projects for vulnerable young people, minorities, and people facing multiple challenges (poverty, racism, etc.), aligning with its mission to overcome stigma and barriers to mental wellness.  
- **Funding Range:** Comic Relief typically offers **medium-to-large grants (six-figure sums)**. For example, calls like *“Thriving Not Just Surviving: Young Men’s Mental Health”* or *“Ahead of The Game: Sport and Mental Health”* invited proposals for **£150,000 up to ~£450,000**, for project durations of 3–5 years【29†L169-L177】. Many grants are in the £100k–£300k range over multiple years, allowing organizations to both deliver services and build capacity. In addition, Comic Relief has partnered on tech innovation funds (such as “Tech for Good”) awarding around £50k–£100k for digital prototypes addressing social issues. Overall, their funding is significant enough to scale up community programs, but usually not as large as research council or Wellcome grants.  
- **Success Metrics:** Comic Relief emphasizes **real-world impact and learning**. Success is measured by improvements in beneficiaries’ lives – e.g. reduced distress, improved mental well-being, better access to support for the target group – as well as the project’s contribution to knowledge of what works【29†L161-L169】. Grantees are expected to monitor outcomes (often using qualitative stories and quantitative measures of change in well-being or service uptake). Because Comic Relief often funds innovative or unproven approaches, a key metric is also the **evidence generated**: projects should document effective methodologies that can be shared or replicated. Long-term sustainability and community buy-in (how the work continues post-grant) are also considered signs of success.  
- **Example Awardees:** The **ADHD Foundation** (a leading neurodiversity charity) secured a Comic Relief grant (alongside BBC Children in Need) totaling almost **£250,000 over 3 years**【28†L122-L129】. This enabled the expansion of ADHD support programs for families – scaling up counseling, peer support, and training across the UK. In another initiative, Comic Relief’s *“Thriving Not Just Surviving”* fund supported projects in Northern Ireland and Scotland that helped young men at risk of addiction and mental ill-health through sports and mentorship, with awards of ~£100k each. Under the *“Ahead of the Game”* call, organizations in the UK and Africa used £150k+ grants to fuse **sport and therapy** to boost mental health in at-risk youth【29†L158-L167】【29†L169-L177】. These examples reflect Comic Relief’s focus on creative, community-rooted solutions for mental health and dual-diagnosis challenges.

## SBRI Healthcare (NHS)  
- **Eligibility:** **SBRI Healthcare** is a UK-government funded competition program (run by the AHSNs and Accelerated Access Collaborative) that invites **innovative companies and other entities to solve NHS challenges**. Calls are open to **private companies (including startups/SMEs)**, as well as public or third-sector organisations (NHS teams, universities, charities) – either singly or in consortia【31†L81-L89】. The key requirement is a fit with the specific call topic and the potential for a commercial solution that the NHS could ultimately procure. SBRI operates in phases, so winners of Phase 1 (feasibility) who demonstrate promise can apply to Phase 2 (development). Applicants must be UK-based or have a UK registered entity to contract with, since contracts are awarded for R&D services.  
- **Funding Range:** SBRI provides **100% funded development contracts** (no equity taken). Funding is typically delivered in two phases: **Phase 1** proofs-of-concept at ~£50k–£100k each (over ~6 months), and **Phase 2** full product development/testing awards often around **£500k–£1 million** each (over ~12–18 months). For example, a recent SBRI mental health competition on “work-related mental health” awarded **£1.7 million total across 9 projects**, roughly £190k per project for a 12-month Phase 1 development【31†L67-L75】. Successful Phase 1 teams can then secure larger Phase 2 grants. This staged approach allows high-risk ideas to be explored cheaply and the best to be scaled with more substantial funds.  
- **Success Metrics:** SBRI projects are contracted to deliver specified **milestones and outputs** – success is measured by meeting technical objectives (e.g. prototype built, pilot study data) and demonstrating **feasibility in real-world healthcare**. By the end of a Phase 2, an innovation should have evidence of clinical or economic benefit and be closer to market. The NHS evaluators look for potential health outcomes improvement, cost savings, and scalability. As noted in an NHS statement, SBRI winners are chosen for their potential to make “a big difference to individuals’ health and support the economy…with the NHS able to evolve by adopting the most promising innovations”【31†L109-L118】. In short, success = a working product or service that the NHS could implement, backed by data (technical, clinical, and health-economic).  
- **Example Awardees:** Under SBRI’s 2024 competition for **“digital innovations for people with poor mental health to stay in work”**, nine companies received ~£200k contracts. One is *InsideOut*, an AI-driven mental health chatbot (avatar coach “Remi”) that provides 24/7 support to employees; it was awarded **£196,000** to test delivering cognitive-behavioral therapy and coaching via an AI avatar to reduce depression and insomnia in the workplace【31†L123-L131】. Another is *Tend VR*, which is adapting a VR mindfulness therapy for farmers with anxiety and depression (grant ~£191k)【31†L147-L155】. Past SBRI competitions have funded **addiction recovery tech** (e.g. digital tools for alcohol misuse), **ADHD assessment tools** (such as classroom attention monitoring apps), and numerous mental-health apps – many of which have gone on to NHS procurement or further NIHR trials.

## Innovate UK & Regional Innovation Programs  
- **Eligibility:** **Innovate UK** (the UK’s national innovation agency, part of UKRI) funds **business-led innovation projects**. Calls relevant to digital mental health often fall under specific programs (e.g. “Mindset XR” for extended-reality therapies) or open Smart Grants. Generally, a UK-registered business must lead the project, but collaborations with universities, NHS trusts, or charities are common and encouraged【33†L85-L93】. Some regional accelerators (like DigitalHealth.London or the Scottish **CivTech** program) have their own criteria, often focusing on companies tackling local health priorities and requiring pilot engagement with regional health services. For Ireland, agencies like **Enterprise Ireland** and the **Health Service Executive (HSE)** support digital health startups through accelerators or Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR) challenges (e.g. recent SBIR calls in Ireland on opioid abuse and health apps).  
- **Funding Range:** Innovate UK grants for health tech can range from ~£75k for small feasibility studies up to **£2–3 million for large R&D collaborations**, depending on the competition. A recent example is **Mindset XR (Extended Reality) for Mental Health**, which in 2025 provided **£3.6 million to 17 projects** – averaging around **£200k each** – to be completed in 12–18 months【33†L82-L90】. Innovate UK typically funds a portion of project costs (50–70% for SMEs, higher for research orgs), so projects often have total budgets larger than the grant. Regional programs vary: for instance, CivTech in Scotland offers ~£50k–£250k contracts to develop solutions for public-sector challenges, while **Health Innovation hubs in Wales** may give small seed grants (~£20k) plus support to pilot innovations in Welsh NHS sites.  
- **Success Metrics:** Innovate UK and similar schemes gauge success by **technical innovation, commercial potential, and societal impact**. For digital mental health projects, this means developing a prototype or product that can demonstrably improve mental health outcomes *and* attract follow-on investment or adoption. They look for evidence that a solution can scale commercially (creating UK jobs and growth) and solve a pressing health problem. The **Mindset XR** projects, for example, target a wide range of mental health conditions (from ADHD and autism to PTSD and addiction) with immersive tech【33†L125-L135】, aiming to extend treatment reach (including to remote or underserved areas) and reduce costs for the NHS【33†L137-L142】. Metrics include successful user testing, clinical validation results, user engagement levels, and readiness for uptake by health providers. Many regional initiatives (like accelerators) also use **success stories** – e.g. number of NHS contracts secured by the startup, or health outcomes in a local pilot – as measures of impact.  
- **Example Awardees:** Under Innovate UK’s **Mindset XR programme**, one Northern Ireland-based consortium is developing a VR therapy with a virtual coach for **addiction recovery**, creating interactive simulations of therapy sessions to help people with substance use disorders【33†L153-L160】. In England, another Innovate UK-backed project is using an **augmented-reality game** co-designed with teens and psychologists to help children with severe anxiety attend school (targeting Emotionally Based School Avoidance)【33†L144-L153】. These projects, alongside others addressing ADHD (e.g. an XR solution for improving attention in adolescents) and depression (smart-glasses delivering therapy cues) were all funded in 2025, highlighting the breadth of digital mental health innovation supported. Regionally, *Digital Health London Accelerator* has helped companies like **Big White Wall (Togetherall)** – an online mental health community – to scale within the NHS, and in Ireland, the **HeadStart fund** (co-funded by Z Zurich Foundation and Rethink Ireland) recently awarded grants to youth mental health social enterprises. Such programs ensure that promising digital interventions for dual-diagnosis and mental health can progress from startup ideas to real-world impact.

## Rethink Ireland & Irish Funding Schemes  
- **Eligibility:** **Rethink Ireland** (formerly Social Innovation Fund Ireland) provides grants and support to **social innovations** – typically non-profit organizations or social enterprises in Ireland that address critical social issues like mental health, addiction, or disability. Eligibility criteria include having a proven concept with potential for scale and a clear social impact. Many funds target specific groups (e.g. youth, rural communities, people with disabilities), so an initiative focusing on dual-diagnosis (ADHD and addiction) would fit if it addresses an identified need. Rethink Ireland requires projects to be not-for-profit or have a strong social mission, and often co-funds with government departments or foundations, which means alignment with national strategies (e.g. Ireland’s *Sharing the Vision* mental health policy) can strengthen applications.  
- **Funding Range:** Rethink Ireland runs **themed funds** that typically offer multi-year awards plus non-financial support (like mentoring and training). Grant sizes vary: smaller funds might give **€30k–€60k per project per year**, whereas larger flagship funds give **€100k+ per year for 2–3 years**. Since 2016, Rethink Ireland has invested **€23.1 million in 127 health-related awardees**【46†L91-L99】 – averaging roughly €180k per project, though some received significantly more and some less. For example, the **Youth Mental Health Fund (2018–2021)** supported several youth mental health nonprofits with funding in the hundreds of thousands (spread over 3 years)【46†L162-L169】. Additionally, the Irish government’s Sláintecare Integration Fund (2019) injected €20 million into 122 pilot projects nationwide (some of which were mental health or addiction-focused), typically granting €100k–€200k each to test new models in community care.  
- **Success Metrics:** Irish funders like Rethink Ireland measure success by **social impact and scalability**. They track the number of lives improved and the depth of that improvement – e.g. Rethink Ireland reports that its health fund awardees have **impacted 134,000+ people** through their services so far【46†L91-L99】. Key metrics include outcomes like improved mental health scores, reduced hospital admissions, or increased engagement in treatment, depending on the project. Equally important, however, is an **increase in the awardee’s capacity** – whether the funding helped the organization grow (staff, reach, sustainability) and whether the innovation can scale or be replicated across Ireland. Many funds also require an evaluation report. In the case of government-backed pilots (like Sláintecare), success might be a proven reduction in wait times or better care integration that leads the HSE to mainstream the project after the pilot.  
- **Example Awardees:** Through Rethink Ireland’s funds, numerous Irish mental health innovations have flourished. For instance, *MyMind*, a digital platform providing low-cost online therapy, received support to expand nationwide and now reaches thousands who couldn’t access traditional services. The **HeadStart Fund (2023–2026)** – in partnership with corporate donors – is currently supporting youth mental health projects and will grant up to €300k to scale the best solutions. Earlier, the **Youth Mental Health Fund 2018–2021** backed organizations like *Jigsaw* (which offers early intervention mental health hubs) to extend their programs【46†L162-L169】. On dual diagnosis, while Ireland historically had limited dedicated funding, this is changing: in 2022 the HSE launched a Dual Diagnosis working group and via the **Community Mental Health Fund** (financed by Ireland’s National Lottery), local addiction-and-mental-health recovery programs (such as integrated support groups) have received grants of €5k–€50k through county councils. As Ireland rolls out its **National Mental Health Research Strategy (2024)**, we can expect more grant opportunities specifically targeting innovative approaches to co-occurring addiction and mental health issues.
