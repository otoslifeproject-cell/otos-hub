# Global dual-diagnosis initiatives (ADHD + addiction)

**International research networks:**  The International Collaboration on ADHD and Substance Abuse (ICASA) is a key global group (involving experts like W. van den Brink, A. Schellekens, Z. Demetrovics, etc.) with a stated mission “to substantially decrease … ADHD patients developing an addiction” and to improve detection, diagnosis and treatment of co-occurring ADHD + SUD【81†L14-L17】.  ICASA and its partners run large studies – e.g. the INCAS prospective cohort (12 sites in 9 countries) which is tracking treatment and outcomes in adults with both ADHD and SUD【59†L112-L120】.  An **international consensus** (2021) of 55 experts from 17 countries recommended routine ADHD screening in adolescents treated for SUD (and vice versa), and “first-line” ADHD treatment with long-acting stimulants embedded in psychosocial care【39†L621-L629】.  (This Delphi-based statement highlights the growing adoption of integrated care models.)  

- **Multi-site research:** The INCAS study (2022) spans 9 countries/12 sites under ICASA auspices, enrolling ~580 adults with ADHD+SUD to monitor their clinical course and treatment patterns【59†L112-L120】【81†L14-L17】.  
- **Consensus guidelines:** An international Delphi panel (Ozgen et al. 2021) concluded that adolescent ADHD+SUD should be co-managed: e.g. “routine screening is recommended for ADHD in … substance abuse treatment” and vice versa, and ADHD pharmacotherapy (long-acting stimulants) should be combined with psychosocial therapy【39†L621-L629】. 

**Integrated treatment models:**  Recent clinical trials and reviews emphasize integrated or “holistic” approaches for ADHD+addiction.  For example, a Dutch RCT (van Emmerik-van Oortmerssen et al. 2019) compared **integrated CBT** (addressing both ADHD and SUD) versus standard SUD-focused CBT.  Integrated CBT produced significantly greater ADHD symptom reduction: patients receiving the dual-focused therapy had a larger drop in ADHD scores (ARS) post-treatment (e.g. mean 28.1 vs 31.5)【79†L351-L360】.  In other words, adding ADHD-specific CBT modules (planning/problem-solving, emotion regulation) to addiction therapy gave extra benefit for ADHD symptoms【79†L351-L360】.  A University of Groningen report similarly found that “one in five addicts have ADHD” and that their integrated CBT module outperformed standard treatment on ADHD symptoms【78†L115-L122】.  

- **Psychosocial + medication:**  A 2025 review (Ratka et al.) argues for a **holistic approach** combining both pharmacological and non-pharmacological interventions.  It states that optimal care for co-occurring ADHD and SUD “includes pharmacological and non-pharmacological interventions,” with behavioral therapies and lifestyle modifications as important adjuncts【51†L1620-L1628】.  In practice, experts note that CBT and other therapies can improve executive function and emotional control, while stimulant (or non-stimulant) medications can be safely used (often long-acting forms) and may even reduce substance use over time.  
- **Medication strategies:**  Guidelines now generally endorse treating ADHD even in SUD patients.  The adolescent consensus recommended long-acting stimulants as first-line ADHD meds in co-morbid SUD【39†L621-L629】.  Recent analyses suggest ADHD medications (when managed properly) do not increase SUD risk and may prolong abstinence, while some agents like atomoxetine or bupropion are being explored for low abuse potential【51†L1650-L1658】【51†L1695-L1704】.  

**Key organizations and programs:**  Several specialized clinics and pilot programs have adopted integrated care: for example, the Jellinek (Arkin) addiction clinics in Amsterdam/Netherlands include ADHD screening and adapted therapy (van Emmerik-van Oortmerssen did her PhD at Jellinek on this)【78†L115-L122】.  In **Australia**, the Alcohol and Drug Research Innovation Agenda (ADRIA) has launched a clinical trial (starting 2024) specifically to improve ADHD care among people with substance use issues.  In the **US**, NIH/NIDA is funding large studies (e.g. a 2025 NIDA grant of $3.7M to Indiana University) to clarify how ADHD medication affects substance abuse risk in youth【75†L97-L105】, reflecting growing focus on co-management.  (General dual-diagnosis programs and rehab centers increasingly advertise combined ADHD/addiction treatment tracks, e.g. specialty inpatient units.)  

- **Example initiatives:**  Apart from research trials (Australia/Indiana), some rehab clinics offer dual ADHD/SUD tracks (e.g. Castle Craig Clinic in Scotland explicitly markets “comprehensive… inpatient addiction and ADHD treatment”).  In practice, however, large-scale national programs are still rare.  The **European** approach has so far been led by research consortia (ICASA) and consensus statements rather than mandated programs, though countries like the Netherlands and Sweden have begun integrating ADHD screening in addiction services.  In the **UK**, no standardized ADHD-in-addiction pilot is documented in the literature, but awareness is rising among charities and some NHS addiction services.  

**Research leaders and institutions:**  Numerous academic teams are active on this topic. Budapest) and Cleo Crunelle (Belgium) have collaborated in ICASA studies【81†L14-L17】.  In the US, experts such as Frances Levin (Columbia University/NYSPI) and Timothy Wilens (Harvard/MGH) are prominent for ADHD+addiction research.  Other notable groups include those at University of Groningen (K. van Emmerik-van Oortmerssen’s CBT trials) and international teams publishing in journals like *European Addiction Research*, *Journal of Clinical Psychiatry*, *BMC Psychiatry*, *Journal of Clinical Medicine*, etc.  These cross-disciplinary efforts all point to an emerging paradigm: viewing addiction through a neurodevelopmental/mental-health lens and addressing ADHD as part of SUD treatment【39†L621-L629】【51†L1620-L1628】.
