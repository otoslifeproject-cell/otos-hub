How to getOut The Other Side.

Thank you for asking for more. This brief is short on purpose: a private strategic advisory brief, not a formal investment memorandum and not a full system disclosure. It is about an unidentified, unacknowledged and high-cost cohort the system already sees and already pays for — adults whose addiction, relapse, repeat A&E contact, family breakdown, service drift and lost productivity may be driven by untreated or under-treated ADHD beneath the visible crisis.

The commercial opportunity is not “changing the NHS for good” as a charity argument. The opportunity is that the system is already spending large amounts reactively, repeatedly and badly. If OTOS can help partners identify the cohort earlier, support a clinically governed priority-review route, hold the person through the gap and show measurable reductions in drift, relapse risk and avoidable demand, then the value created becomes a serious licensing, commissioning and scale opportunity.

Dean was one of the cohort. His ADHD was already in the story, but it was not held as the driver. After seven years of waiting, he engineered a private route back to proper ADHD treatment. Once medicated correctly, the addiction work, therapy, recovery education and structure finally started to land. That sequence created OTOS.

The opportunity is not another recovery app. It is a bespoke non-clinical infrastructure layer, independent of patient records, that tests whether this cohort can be identified earlier, routed to the right ADHD clinical door, wrapped in continuity, and measured for engagement, relapse risk, follow-through and avoidable cost. ADHD-driven addiction is the plain-English cohort; NIA — Neurologically Informed Addiction is the proposed category signal; OTOS Continuity™ is the infrastructure that tests whether the missing thread can be held.

We are already paying for this failure — just in the worst possible places. Dean recovered by engineering a route the system should have had: creating the conditions for detox, securing the letter, reaching the psychiatrist, restarting ADHD treatment, then watching the recovery work finally land.

For an investor, that matters because founder-market fit here is unusually deep. The clinical letter
The thesis, inclinical language.

In a consultant psychiatrist's letter addressed by name to the CPFT ADHD Team, one sentence describes the entire cohort.

"Adult ADHD, previously complicated with excess alcohol use to self-medicate inadequately managed ADHD symptoms."Independent consultant psychiatrist — diagnostic letter to the CPFT ADHD Team, April 2026.

That sentence is the cohort. Not ADHD and alcohol use treated as two separate problems — adult ADHD, complicated by self-medication. Treating the alcohol alone left the driver underneath untouched; recognising and treating the ADHD is what finally let the rest of the work hold. The letter is medical correspondence about Dean — not an endorsement of OTOS, and not proof that OTOS works — but it documents that the mechanism is real in a lived pathway.

"Significant and comorbid substance misuse should be managed jointly between general adult and addiction services…"
Royal College of Psychiatrists — ADHD in Adults: Good practice guidance (CR235)

The comparison, item for item

What recovery needs. What ADHD takes away.

Every pound in the cost case on the next page traces back to one mechanism. Addiction recovery asks for the exact capacities unmanaged ADHD strips out — line them up item for item and the treatment model and the disorder point in opposite directions. This is why the cohort relapses through good support: not for want of effort, but because the recovery is built on the one system the ADHD has disabled.

CapacityUnmanaged ADHD impairsAddiction recovery requires
Executive functionDifficulty planning, sequencing and self-regulating; a starved prefrontal cortexDaily planning, structure and the delay of short-term gratification
Focus & attentionChronic distractibility; hard to sustain attention on non-stimulating tasksDeep, continuous focus through long therapy sessions and recovery work
Working memoryForgets strategies, misses appointmentsRetaining tools, tracking triggers, holding routines
Impulse controlReflexive, immediate urgesPausing and resisting cravings in the moment
Emotional regulationDistress arrives fast and overwhelmsTolerating distress without the substance
Routine & follow-throughHard to sustain mundane routinesRepetition that rebuilds new behavioural patterns
Reward & arousalUnder-arousal; pull to immediate rewardA stable baseline; tolerating delayed reward
Neuroplastic learningBlunted adaptation without immediate payoffHealthy re-wiring to replace deep-seated habits

Read it as a model, not a verdict. An explanatory framing drawn from the published account of ADHD and recovery; presentations vary and the alignment is directional. Full capacity-by-capacity comparison and sources in the OTOS Evidence Dossier.
