Economic Justification & ROI in NHS Commissioning

How digital mental-health and addiction tools are justified on a cost-per-user and return-on-investment basis — and what a funding-ready OTOS pilot for Cambridgeshire & Peterborough ICS must build in.

Digital tools are typically justified by comparing the low marginal cost of a digital programme to the high cost of traditional care (therapy sessions, inpatient rehab). Benchmarks for pilots usually show very low cost-per-user (often <£50/year) and ROI factors of 2–5× through reduced staff time, medication or acute episodes.

A NIHR-funded trial of the Drink Less app reported reduced consumption and projected “hundreds of millions of pounds” in NHS savings over 20 years if widely scaled. In designing a pilot, OTOS should plan to capture such metrics — cost per user, GP/therapy time saved, avoided hospitalisations — as key outcomes.

Evaluation frameworks & evidence standards

NHS commissioners require digital tools to meet national standards. NICE's Digital Technology Assessment Criteria (DTAC) is mandatory — the baseline “entry ticket” for procurement, covering safety, data protection, cybersecurity, interoperability, usability and accessibility.

The NICE Evidence Standards Framework (ESF) defines the level of clinical evidence needed by risk/function. A self- or coach-guided app typically falls under ESF Tier C or D — it must show credible effectiveness (often via RCT or robust real-world study) and economic value. For mental-health / adult ADHD tools, NICE expects validated outcomes (PHQ-9/GAD-7, AUDIT/DAST, ADHD symptom scales) and a favourable cost-effectiveness profile.

The 2025 NHS ADHD Taskforce urged that “ADHD services need to be digitised” and recommended NICE “prioritise rapid assessment of digital products.” OTOS should align its pilot with NICE ESF levels and prepare evidence in NICE Technology Adoption Briefing formats.

Procurement pathways (pilot → rollout)

  • NHS Innovation Accelerator (NIA) & national programmes. Successful innovations join the NIA for NHS-wide visibility (Wysa entered in 2023; its published ROI case was likely instrumental).
  • Academic / NIHR pilots. Partnering with NIHR/universities funds rigorous evaluation. Drink Less was NIHR-backed and published in The Lancet — the kind of evidence NHS trusts look for.
  • ICS & Trust procurements. Regional ICBs commission via local innovation or transformation budgets. Digital products are often placed through frameworks like G-Cloud (Digital Marketplace) so ICSs can “call off” contracts quickly.
  • Regional Innovation Hubs & AHSNs. Health-tech hubs (e.g. Health Innovation East) connect startups with the NHS; NIHR i4i and NHS Innovation grants may fund research that dovetails with pilots.

SMEs should note the NHS push toward value-based evaluation (new guidance launching 2026) — life-cycle savings matter as much as upfront fees.

Case studies of NHS digital tools

ToolEvidence & adoption
Sleepio (Big Health)Digital CBT for insomnia. Built evidence via multiple RCTs; NICE recommended it as cost-saving (2022). Scotland funded it nationally (2021); commissioned in English ICBs (Frimley, BOB) by mid-2024. Shows the timeline: build evidence, then engage early-adopter regions.
WysaAI chatbot for anxiety/depression. Entered NIA 2023. Low cost (£5.90/user) and projected waiting-list reduction. Business case emphasises therapist time saved by handling mild/moderate cases digitally.
SilverCloud (Amwell)Online CBT used across IAPT (~700k referrals over a decade). Reports ~90% lower delivery cost vs face-to-face; therapists report ~1.5h vs 4h per patient.
Drink LessNIHR-funded RCT (n=5,602) showed modest drinking reduction and modelled large NHS savings if scaled — an example of pilot evidence supporting commissioning.
KoothFree online mental-health service for young people. Economic model estimated ~£236 saving per user via reduced GP visits and medication. Commissioned by many UK localities.

Common themes: low per-user cost vs high-cost alternatives, validated clinical outcomes, and early real-world utilisation data. For context, inpatient addiction rehab can cost ~£14,000 for a 28-day course (~£495/day) while many digital solutions cost <£100/user.

Designing a funding-ready pilot for Cambridgeshire & Peterborough ICS

  • Partner with stakeholders. Engage CPICS mental-health and addiction leads and local ADHD specialists to co-design the pilot around ICS priorities; work with digital/transformation teams to access innovation funding.
  • Define outcomes clearly. Use validated measures (ADHD Rating Scale, AUDIT, PHQ-9/GAD-7) plus service metrics (referrals, GP appointments, medication). Set a 12–18 month timeframe with interim milestones — e.g. 200 patients over 6–12 months, measuring symptom change and service use.
  • Economic modelling from Day 1. Project costs and savings under best-case and conservative scenarios. Cambridge has ~5,775 adults needing alcohol treatment (4,505 untreated) — model population impact. Tie ADHD intervention to avoidable outcomes; cite the Taskforce's ~£17bn avoidable ADHD-related cost estimate.
  • NICE / DTAC preparation. Run a DTAC self-assessment early; ensure data security and interoperability; decide which ESF tier applies (likely Tier C for a hybrid coached intervention).
  • Implementation plan. Outline delivery (GP referral, IAPT, addiction services), align with Cambridgeshire's digital strategy, and train supporting clinicians/coaches.

Even a £100–200 digital intervention looks attractive against thousands in alternative care. OTOS should gather baseline costs and aim to undercut them through prevention and early intervention.

Preparing for procurement & scale-up (12–18 months)

  • Develop a business case. Summarise pilot data (clinical improvement, cost per user, extrapolated savings) into an NHS-style case aligned with CPICS strategy and local need.
  • Engage procurement early. Connect with CPICS procurement officers and digital leads; propose contracting via G-Cloud or a local digital formulary; prepare DTAC form, evidence packs and clinical-governance info in advance.
  • Innovation funding. Watch for ICS transformation funds, NHS England digital-service funding and national programmes (NHSX Test Beds, CPICS Innovation Challenge) to bridge pilot-to-scale.
  • Foster champions. Identify clinician and patient champions (a lead psychiatrist or IAPT manager) to advocate internally — NHS adoption often depends on them.
