# Economic Impact of Untreated Dual Diagnosis (ADHD + Addiction) – Executive Summary

## Key Findings

- **High Cost to Health & Public Services:** Adults with untreated ADHD (especially with co-occurring addiction or mental illness) incur significantly higher costs to healthcare, social services, and the justice system. In the UK, an adult with ADHD costs **~£17,000 more per year** in public sector expenses than a non-ADHD peer【33†L703-L711】. This contributes to an **estimated £17 billion annual cost** to the UK economy from not treating ADHD (via higher health, social care, and criminal justice costs, plus lost productivity)【2†L49-L57】【33†L703-L711】. Similarly, U.S. data show about **$14,000 in excess cost per adult with ADHD annually**, totaling **$122.8 billion** nationally【24†L193-L201】. Australian research found a **per-person cost of ~US$15,700** per year (2018–19), with total social/economic costs around **A$20.4 billion** annually【10†L340-L348】【9†L535-L543】. Canadian experts note that while specific national figures are lacking, **ADHD likely costs Canada billions** each year in avoidable expenses, comparable to U.S. levels【24†L195-L203】. In Ireland, no exact data exist, but stakeholders observe that **separate mental health and addiction services result in higher overall costs** (due to repeated relapses and inefficiencies) compared to an integrated dual-diagnosis approach【16†L14-L22】.

- **Strain on Emergency, Social Care, and Justice Sectors:** Untreated dual diagnosis drives frequent crises. For example, individuals with unaddressed ADHD are more likely to make **repeated A&E (emergency) visits, require social supports, and encounter the justice system**【1†L311-L319】【2†L37-L45】. **25% of the UK prison population has ADHD**, with unmanaged symptoms contributing to impulsive offending and higher reoffending rates【3†L11-L19】. This represents a huge cost to criminal justice and social services. Untreated ADHD is also linked to higher rates of substance misuse, homelessness, family breakdown, and other social care issues【3†L47-L54】 – all of which escalate public spending. In Ireland, it was estimated back in 2008 that keeping people with severe dual diagnosis in prison cost about **€200 million per year**, illustrating the heavy justice system burden【16†L25-L33】 (and costs have likely risen since).

- **Cost of Early Intervention & Integrated Treatment:** Providing early, integrated treatment for ADHD and co-occurring conditions has upfront costs but greatly reduces long-term expenditures. **Effective early intervention** (e.g. timely ADHD diagnosis, medication, therapy, and concurrent addiction treatment) can prevent the downstream costs of crises. Standard ADHD treatments are **highly cost-effective** – studies show that medication and psychosocial interventions for ADHD **yield better outcomes at lower overall cost than no treatment** in the UK【33†L718-L724】. In fact, treating ADHD can **mitigate or avoid costly outcomes**: evidence links ADHD treatment (especially stimulant medication) to **lower risks of future substance abuse, depression, injuries/accidents, long-term unemployment and criminality**【33†L734-L742】【24†L211-L219】. For instance, treating children with ADHD early (before puberty) significantly **reduces the likelihood of later developing a substance use disorder**【24†L217-L224】 – a clear example of early intervention saving costs on addiction services down the line. The **annual cost of ADHD medication or therapy per patient** is relatively small (hundreds to a few thousand dollars/pounds) compared to the tens of thousands in costs that an unmanaged dual-diagnosis patient can accumulate in health, social, and justice systems each year.

- **Return on Investment (ROI) for Integrated Programs:** There is strong evidence that money spent on integrated dual-diagnosis services yields substantial returns in savings to public systems. A **UK analysis by Turning Point and the London School of Economics** found that **for every £1 invested in an integrated dual diagnosis (mental health + addiction) program, about £4.07 is saved** in reduced costs elsewhere【16†L30-L38】. These savings come from outcomes like higher employment, reduced crime, fewer hospital admissions, and more stable housing【16†L32-L39】. A recent UK briefing projected that investing in ADHD diagnosis/treatment could return **£4 for every £1 within 3 years, and up to £16 per £1 over a decade**【3†L30-L38】. In real terms, treating around 15,000 patients per year could save roughly **£493 million in 3 years and £1.9 billion in 10 years** through reductions in healthcare usage, welfare dependency, and justice costs【3†L30-L37】. Case studies further illustrate ROI: a pilot integrated support program in one UK locality tracked a high-needs individual and saw their annual service costs (hospital, police, courts, etc.) drop by **£140,000** after receiving proper dual-diagnosis care【16†L37-L40】. In short, integrated community care not only improves patient outcomes but also **pays for itself** by cutting expensive crisis interventions.

- **Digital and Community-Based Innovations:**  Leveraging **digital health tools and community-based programs** for dual diagnosis care can amplify cost savings. Digital interventions (telemedicine, online therapy platforms, apps for mental health/ADHD support) can dramatically **lower delivery costs** while improving access. For example, one digital mental health program reported a **91% reduction in care delivery costs** compared to traditional in-person therapy【26†L103-L111】. Scalable online interventions (like internet-based CBT for ADHD or co-occurring anxiety) have been shown to reduce overall medical utilization by ~15%, including shorter hospital stays【26†L103-L111】. These tools extend reach and provide earlier help, potentially preventing costly crises. Community-based programs tailored to dual diagnosis also show positive economic impacts. In forensic settings, **assertive community treatment for people with mental illness and addiction** (instead of standard incarceration or hospitalization) has yielded net savings on the order of tens of thousands of dollars **per patient per year**, by averting jail time and emergency hospitalizations (as seen in U.S. program evaluations【16†L25-L33】【16†L37-L40】). Overall, embracing digital therapeutics and strengthening community dual-diagnosis services can further **increase the ROI** by efficiently managing conditions and preventing relapse, thereby reducing expensive acute care and justice system encounters.

**Bottom Line:** Failing to treat ADHD with co-occurring addiction/mental health issues comes at a very high price to society – often **tens of thousands per person each year** in medical emergencies, social support, lost productivity, and crime-related costs. Conversely, **investing in early and integrated dual-diagnosis treatment is cost-effective and yields substantial savings**. Multi-country evidence (UK, Ireland, US, Australia, Canada) from 2020–2025 consistently shows that every dollar/pound spent on comprehensive ADHD and addiction care returns several-fold in benefits: healthier and safer individuals, and **significantly lower burdens on healthcare, social care, and justice systems**【16†L30-L39】【3†L30-L38】. In economic terms, proactive treatment of dual diagnosis isn’t just an ethical imperative – **it more than pays for itself** in reduced future costs and improved societal outcomes【16†L32-L40】【24†L211-L219】.
