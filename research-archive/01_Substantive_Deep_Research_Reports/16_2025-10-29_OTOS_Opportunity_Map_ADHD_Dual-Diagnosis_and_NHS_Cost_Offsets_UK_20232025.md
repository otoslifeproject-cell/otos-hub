## 2. Executive Summary – Dual-Diagnosis Landscape & OTOS Opportunity

**Overview:** The UK faces a *critical gap* in addressing co-occurring ADHD, mental health, and addiction (“dual diagnosis”). Long NHS waiting lists for ADHD assessments, high addiction relapse and criminal re-offending rates, and fragmented services all drive up costs and harm outcomes. **OTOS (One Treatment One Solution)** is an integrated digital approach targeting these dual-diagnosis cases. This summary outlines current challenges and how OTOS can improve outcomes and generate substantial savings, aligned with NHS England, NIHR, MoJ, ONS, and the independent ADHD Taskforce findings.

**ADHD Waiting Times Crisis:** ADHD services are overwhelmed, with many adults waiting years for diagnosis and support. In England, some Integrated Care Boards report **median waits over 2 years**, and the worst-case waits exceed **10 years**【4†L694-L702】【13†L1265-L1273】. For example, **Herefordshire & Worcestershire** patients have waited ~550 weeks (~10.5 years) while **Dorset** managed ~12 weeks【13†L1265-L1273】. Wales and Northern Ireland show similar backlogs: Hywel Dda (Wales) averages ~3 years【32†L1292-L1300】; Belfast (NI) projects **8-year waits**【33†L1355-L1362】. Such “inhumane” delays (as described by clinicians) deny timely care, exacerbating risks of self-harm, unemployment, and crime【8†L201-L209】. The **ADHD Taskforce** warns these backlogs will escalate unless new cost-effective solutions are implemented【2†L19-L27】【2†L31-L39】.

**Dual-Diagnosis Prevalence:** A significant portion of individuals struggle with both mental ill-health and substance misuse. UK studies show **20–37%** of mental health patients in care have co-existing drug/alcohol issues, and conversely **6–15%** of those in addiction treatment have severe mental illness【34†L334-L336】. In deprived communities this overlap is even higher (one local audit found ~70% of community drug users had a mental health condition)【26†L25-L29】. **Dual-diagnosis adults** are often “frequent flyers” across NHS and justice systems, yet historically services have worked in silos. The result is costly repeat emergency visits, failed treatments, and revolving-door incarceration. ***Insight:** Integrating care for dual diagnoses is no longer optional – it SHOULD be standard (multisector consensus) to break the cycle.*

**Relapse and Re-offending – The Cost of Failure:** Current systems see unacceptably high failure rates. In addiction services, **40–60% of patients relapse** within one year【17†L31-L37】, undoing progress and incurring more healthcare costs. In criminal justice, the Ministry of Justice reports a **27% overall re-offending rate** within a year for adults【49†L75-L81】 – and nearly **half of short-sentence prisoners (≈48%) are reconvicted within a year**【49†L73-L81】. Many of these individuals have unmet mental health or ADHD needs contributing to their cycles of relapse and crime【8†L201-L209】【4†L733-L741】. These outcomes represent human tragedies and **massive waste:** Re-offending alone costs taxpayers about **£18 billion annually** in England & Wales【49†L73-L81】, and substance misuse costs (drugs £19bn, alcohol £25bn) per year are similarly staggering【42†L7-L15】【42†L11-L17】. ***Insight:** Every relapse or re-offence that OTOS prevents COULD save tens of thousands of pounds – and more importantly, lives and livelihoods.*

**NHS Spending and Fragmentation:** The NHS and social services are pouring money into downstream consequences instead of upstream solutions. Mental health spending in England has risen to **£15.6bn (planned 2025–26)【40†L15-L23】**, about 8–9% of the NHS budget, yet much of this is absorbed by expensive crisis care and institutional costs. For instance, each prison place costs ~**£52k per year**【49†L55-L63】, far above what community support or digital interventions would cost. Similarly, probation supervision costs ~£6k per person/year【49†L41-L49】 – still a fraction of prison costs, highlighting the value of community-based approaches. **However, dual-diagnosis care remains under-resourced**: funding for addiction treatment was cut by ~£1bn over the last decade【43†L121-L129】, and no dedicated budget exists for integrated dual-diagnosis pathways. The result is costs being shifted rather than reduced – for example, untreated ADHD leads to higher A&E, welfare, and criminal justice expenditures, estimated at **£17k extra per adult per year** (in one study)【4†L703-L711】. ***Insight:** By reallocating a portion of spend from reactive to preventive (e.g. digital assessment, early intervention), the NHS COULD achieve far better value for money.*

**The OTOS Solution – Integration and Early Intervention:** OTOS offers a joined-up, digitally enabled care pathway for individuals with co-occurring neurodevelopmental and substance use disorders. It builds on evidence that **timely diagnosis and integrated support pay off**. For example, when children with ADHD get earlier care, later costs in education and crime drop measurably【4†L709-L717】. Conversely, failing to intervene means higher costs across sectors – as the ADHD Taskforce notes, “impacts… seen across education, work, justice… many costs are avoidable with appropriate early support”【1†L182-L190】. OTOS *integrates* ADHD treatment, psychological support, and addiction services into one package (hence “One Solution”), leveraging digital tools to scale efficiently.

- 🟩 **DO IT – Digital Diagnostics & Triage:** *Quick win:* Expand use of digital ADHD assessments and screening tools across all regions. Already **75% of NHS trusts** use the computerised QbTest to aid ADHD diagnosis【47†L1-L4】, speeding up assessments by >40%【4†L783-L790】. This has yielded **9–39% cost reductions** in pilot sites and a **£3.37:£1 return on investment**【46†L225-L234】【46†L229-L238】. OTOS can immediately plug into this digital workflow – e.g. adding an addiction risk screening alongside QbTest – to triage dual-diagnosis cases faster. This **invest-to-save** approach is strongly recommended by NHS England and NICE【2†L31-L39】【46†L233-L240】. By fast-tracking high-risk patients to treatment, we avoid wasted spend on crises (A&E visits, police callouts, etc.) and reduce waiting lists.

- 🟩 **DO IT – Integrated Dual-Dx Care Pathways:** Break the silos between mental health and addiction services. Where implemented, integrated “co-occurring conditions” teams show better engagement and outcomes【11†L1107-L1115】【11†L1099-L1107】. OTOS should be adopted as a unified pathway *within ICSs and Health Boards*, so that once a patient is identified with dual needs, they receive one coordinated care plan (rather than bouncing between services). This directly addresses the gap noted in all four nations: “postcode lottery” access and no single service responsible for dual diagnosis【11†L1099-L1107】【21†L23-L31】. The potential payoff is large – fewer no-shows, more sustained recoveries, and reduced reoffending. **This approach is a “DO IT now”** because the data shows dual-diagnosis patients are driving disproportionate costs under the status quo【34†L318-L326】【34†L320-L328】.

- 🟨 **SHOULD – Focus on Transition Age Youth:** A notable opportunity is to target late-teens and young adults (17–25), where unaddressed ADHD often evolves into criminal justice involvement or addiction. Children’s services report thousands of youths waiting for neurodiversity assessments【32†L1298-L1306】, and only **1 in 6** diagnosed ADHD kids transition successfully to adult services【4†L688-L696】 – a dangerous drop-off. We SHOULD extend OTOS to youth justice and CAMHS-to-adult service transitions. For example, youth with ADHD or autism leaving care or entering the justice system could be fast-tracked into an OTOS program that continues support into adulthood. This preventative focus aligns with the Taskforce’s call to “prevent adverse impacts and costly outcomes in the future” by intervening early【1†L151-L160】【1†L168-L176】. It will help “turn off the tap” of future dual-diagnosis clients by catching them before addiction takes hold.

- 🟨 **SHOULD – Regional “Opportunity Map” Pilots:** The data identifies certain *high-need* regions (e.g. **North East England** for substance misuse, **Belfast** for ADHD waits, etc.) as ideal pilot sites. Regions like Yorkshire/Humber (highest drug use increase)【36†L349-L358】 or cities like Glasgow (massive referral surge for ADHD)【32†L1317-L1325】 could yield high impact. We SHOULD secure cross-agency agreements in a few such areas to implement OTOS and closely measure outcomes (e.g. reduction in wait times, relapse, A&E visits, arrests). These pilots can build the economic case with local data, complementing national evidence. NIHR and devolved health departments likely would support such innovation trials, given that 75% of surveyed commissioners already recognize the need for better data and digital solutions in ADHD services【2†L53-L58】【2†L59-L67】.

- 🟦 **COULD – Long-Term Savings & Scaling:** If proven, OTOS could be scaled UK-wide, delivering multi-year savings. Mental ill-health costs the English economy **£300bn/year** (all societal costs)【43†L135-L143】【43†L157-L164】 – effectively “a pandemic every year” in economic impact. Even modest gains from OTOS (for instance, a 10% improvement in outcomes for dual-diagnosis patients) *could save ~£3–4 billion annually* when factoring health, crime and productivity gains (author’s analysis using cost benchmarks【4†L703-L711】【49†L73-L81】). This far outweighs the implementation costs. We **could** reinvest these savings into further preventive services (housing, employment support for those in recovery, etc.), creating a virtuous cycle. In the long run, widespread OTOS adoption might also inform **policy changes** – e.g. establishing a national target for ADHD waiting times (currently absent)【8†L185-L194】, or including ADHD in the 18-week NHS standard, which it is currently excluded from【8†L163-L172】【8†L181-L189】.

**Key Takeaway:** The confluence of ADHD and addiction represents a *major opportunity for impact*. By adopting OTOS – an integrated, digital, “one-stop” dual-diagnosis solution – the NHS and partners can **simultaneously improve patient outcomes and achieve substantial cost savings**. This aligns with the “prevention and digitisation” priority in the NHS 10-Year Plan【1†L119-L127】【1†L131-L139】 and addresses what the Royal College of Psychiatrists calls an urgent need for *“joined-up solutions for people living with ADHD”* and co-occurring conditions【10†L1200-L1208】【10†L1212-L1216】. We have the data, we have early pilots proving effectiveness – now it’s time to act. In summary, **investing in OTOS is a DO IT** ✅: it will pay off through lower waiting times, higher recovery rates, reduced reoffending, and freed-up resources to be used elsewhere【46†L225-L234】【46†L229-L238】. The NHS’s own independent report implores that we **“address the growing backlogs… to avoid wasted expenditure on the adverse outcomes of untreated ADHD… an invest to save model.”**【2†L31-L39】 OTOS embodies this invest-to-save principle for dual diagnosis, offering a path to measurable social and economic returns while transforming lives.
