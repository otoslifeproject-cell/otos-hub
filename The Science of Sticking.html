The science of sticking

Why recovery has to be practised into the brain.

And why some brains need help to practise it. Medication does not do the recovery work. It creates the conditions where the recovery work can finally land. Continuity keeps the repetition alive long enough for it to stick.

Neuroplasticity, plainly

Neurons that fire together, wire together.

Recovery is not a decision. It is a learning process — and learning, in the brain, is physical. Every repeated behaviour strengthens a pattern of neurons; repeat it enough and the pattern becomes efficient, then automatic, then simply who the person is. New behaviour is built the way a path is worn across a field: not in one crossing, but in many.

Three things are non-negotiable for that path to form: repetition — one rehearsal changes almost nothing; time between repetitions — consolidation happens in the gaps, during rest and sleep; and a brain in a state where it can encode the repetition at all. That third condition is the whole story for the people this document is about.

Why the ADHD brain finds sticking harder

A signalling problem, not a motivation problem.

Behaviour change asks for exactly the capacities that unmanaged ADHD impairs: attention, working memory, impulse control, emotional regulation, routine, delayed reward, and repeated behavioural learning.

In ADHD, differences in dopamine and noradrenaline function affect the circuits the brain uses to self-direct — the prefrontal systems that hold a goal in mind, filter distraction, wait for a later reward, and return to a task tomorrow. The practical consequences map directly onto the requirements for "sticking":

  • Working memory is leaky — the strategy learned on Monday isn't reliably there at the moment of temptation on Wednesday.
  • Reward is steeply discounted over time — a benefit weeks away exerts far less pull than relief available right now.
  • Under-arousal drives a search for stimulation — and the fastest, most reliable regulator available is often the most harmful one.
  • Repetition is fragile — each rehearsal depends on the same executive machinery that is running under-powered.
So the new neural pattern keeps starting to form and then failing to consolidate — not because the person isn't trying, but because the biological conditions for consolidation aren't in place. The field never gets crossed the same way twice in a row, so the path never wears in.

The cruel mismatch

The treatment model and the disorder point in opposite directions.

For a defined subgroup, alcohol is not, at first, misuse — it is regulation: focus, relief, calm, social access, a temporary correction of baseline. Over time that functional use crosses an invisible line into dependence. Then the system asks the person to recover using attention, memory, routine and delayed reward — precisely the capacities that were impaired to begin with, and are now doubly impaired. The work was never wrong. The brain could not yet use it.

This ADHD-driven pattern into dependence is the pattern NIA (Neurologically Informed Addiction) proposes and names — a founder-proposed working category, not a recognised diagnosis.

What changes the equation

Medication opens the window. Repetition builds the wall.

If the barrier to consolidation is neurochemical, two things have to happen — in order, and together. First, medication can open the window: appropriately prescribed ADHD medication can improve attention, behavioural control and follow-through, restoring enough signalling that a rehearsal can actually be encoded. Second, the skills still have to be built by repetition — this is what CBT, relapse-prevention and recovery education are: structured repetition of the notice–pause–choose–repeat sequence until it consolidates into habit. Neither alone is the intervention.

NICE supports the combination. NICE recommends medication for adults whose ADHD symptoms remain significantly impairing, and recommends considering non-pharmacological treatment in combination with medication where adults have benefited from medication but remain impaired — which may include a full course of CBT. Medication reduces the core barrier; structured psychological work builds the skills on top.

The clinical signal (use carefully) — in adult ADHD studies reviewed by NICE, medication-plus-CBT showed stronger responder outcomes than medication alone or CBT-plus-placebo in selected adult groups:

The part everyone underestimates

The window opens — and then closes on an empty room.

Consolidation needs repetition across days, not inside a weekly hour. But the gaps are exactly where support disappears — between appointments, across a waiting list, through a missed call, a bad week. For the ADHD brain — leaky working memory, weak future-reward pull — the unrehearsed behavioural pattern is the first thing lost. A skill taught and then left unrepeated for two weeks does not wire in.

This is where continuity matters: Surface — micro check-ins and early drift signals. Hold — appointment prompts and transition receipts. Reinforce — recovery-literacy repetition between sessions. Evidence — consent-led signals so the intervention can be tested, improved, continued or stopped.

Neurons that fire together wire together — but only if they keep firing. Continuity is what keeps them firing across the days when no one is in the room.

The NIA mechanism, step by step

The brain has to be ready to learn.

1Unmanaged ADHD — low regulation, poor inhibition, under-arousal, impulsivity, emotional dysregulation.
2Alcohol as regulation — focus, relief, calm, social access, temporary baseline correction.
3Dependence — tolerance, withdrawal, reward-system capture, a deeper executive-function deficit.
4Recovery mismatch — the system asks the person to recover using attention, memory, routine and delayed reward: precisely the capacities most impaired.
5Medication-supported learning window — ADHD treatment can improve attention, behavioural control and follow-through, so rehearsal can finally be encoded.
6Continuity layer — check-ins, reminders, waiting-well learning, partner nudges, recovery-literacy repetition and relapse-risk visibility keep the new behavioural pattern warm long enough for behaviour to stick.
The brain has to be ready to learn. The person has to stay engaged long enough for learning to become behaviour. The system has to hold the thread.

The position, stated safely

Not that medication cures addiction. Something narrower, stronger, testable.

"For a defined subgroup, alcohol dependence may be driven by untreated or inadequately treated ADHD. Once the ADHD is clinically stabilised, CBT, recovery education, therapy, routine-building and behavioural reinforcement may become usable in a way they were not before. Continuity infrastructure is what keeps that learning window open between services."
Caveats that travel with this document

Neuroplasticity, Hebbian learning and habit consolidation are established neuroscience, described here in plain terms; the ADHD-addiction pattern (NIA) is a founder-proposed working category, not a recognised diagnosis. Medication effects are described as can / may — clinical decisions belong to clinical teams. The responder figures are selected adult ADHD studies as reviewed by NICE — engagement signals, not alcohol-recovery rates, and not an outcome promise. OTOS is non-clinical: it does not diagnose, prescribe, treat, deliver therapy or provide crisis care. It surfaces continuity signals; clinical teams make clinical decisions. Founder experience is design evidence, not universal proof.
