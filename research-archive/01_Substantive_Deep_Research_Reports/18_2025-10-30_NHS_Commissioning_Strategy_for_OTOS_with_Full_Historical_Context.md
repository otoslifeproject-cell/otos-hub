# OTOS NHS Commissioning Strategy and Pilot Guidance

Commissioning in the NHS is a structured cycle of assessing needs, planning services, purchasing care, and monitoring outcomes **to achieve the best health results**【46†L37-L44】. In practice, integrated care boards (ICBs) now hold commissioning authority under NHS England, aligning local service design with the national Long Term Plan【46†L37-L44】【46†L47-L49】.  Any proposal like OTOS must therefore clearly address local needs and national priorities – for example by showing how the innovation improves defined patient outcomes and system goals set out in the NHS 10-Year Plan.

- **Focus on outcomes:** Modern NHS guidance stresses long-term patient outcomes over upfront cost【21†L119-L127】.  The DHSC’s recent value-based procurement guidelines explicitly call for an “invest to save” approach where technologies are chosen based on effectiveness and **long-term impact on patients and costs**【21†L119-L127】【39†L36-L43】. OTOS must frame its value in these terms (e.g. “improved tinnitus outcomes” or “reduced referral burden”), not just initial expense.
- **Alignment with strategy:** NHS commissioning is evolving under the NHS Long Term Plan. Commissioners expect innovations to link to strategic goals (e.g. reducing waiting times, improving mental well-being)【46†L47-L49】【39†L42-L50】. The OTOS pilot should therefore define metrics that map onto those goals (for instance, reduced hearing-health-related demand or better mental health scores) and reference the Long Term Plan ambitions in its narrative.
- **Outcome-based contracting:** The NHS is also piloting outcome- or value-based contracts, where payment is tied to achieving agreed clinical or service outcomes【21†L119-L127】. OTOS should prepare to negotiate contracts or pilot agreements that include milestones (e.g. X% of patients reporting symptom reduction) or savings-sharing, reflecting this shift towards performance-based commissioning.

## Evidence and Evaluation Frameworks

New interventions in the NHS must meet rigorous evidence standards. For digital health solutions like OTOS, NICE’s **Evidence Standards Framework (ESF)** is a key reference. The ESF categorizes digital technologies by complexity and risk, and specifies the level of clinical and economic evidence required at each tier【37†L156-L164】. In practice, this means OTOS should classify its solution (e.g. an app delivering therapeutic content) and collect evidence accordingly. 

- **Use NICE’s ESF:** Developers are encouraged to use the ESF user guide and tools. NICE even provides a *budget-impact estimation tool* for digital health, to quantify costs/benefits relative to current practice【37†L158-L164】. OTOS should utilize such tools to calculate its potential return on investment (ROI) and cost savings. This will help meet NICE’s expectation that commissioners understand the financial impacts of a new digital intervention.
- **NICE HealthTech evaluation:** NICE is expanding its appraisal process to treat devices and digital tools with the same rigor as drugs【39†L36-L43】. Technologies that meet NICE standards may receive strong NHS-wide recommendations for adoption. OTOS should track NICE’s emerging healthtech standards and, if feasible, seek a formal NICE assessment. Framing OTOS’s evidence in NICE terms (e.g. specifying clinical pathways, patient populations) will align it with these national decision frameworks.
- **DTAC compliance:** On the technical/regulatory side, NHS England’s **Digital Technology Assessment Criteria (DTAC)** set the required safety, security, interoperability, data protection and usability standards【43†L49-L57】【43†L139-L147】. OTOS must ensure its product meets or exceeds these criteria (for example, clinical safety and data privacy standards) before or during procurement. The DTAC is used by NHS buyers at procurement time, so being “DTAC-ready” will smooth the approval process【43†L49-L57】.
- **Local evaluation pathways:** In addition to NICE/DTAC, OTOS pilots should adopt robust local evaluation frameworks. The NHS Evaluation Toolkit recommends **health economic modeling** appropriate to the scale of the study【29†L143-L152】. For small-scale pilots, a cost–benefit or cost–utility analysis may suffice, whereas a larger pilot (covering many sites or 12+ months) should plan a **budget-impact model** to project system-wide ROI【29†L143-L152】. This modeling will be essential evidence for NHS decision-makers.

## Economic Evaluation and Modelling

To convince commissioners, OTOS must demonstrate value for money. The NHS encourages formal economic evaluation of new services【29†L143-L152】. Key approaches include cost-benefit, cost-effectiveness and budget-impact analyses, each highlighting value from different angles【31†L332-L341】【31†L347-L356】. 

- **Cost–Benefit Analysis (CBA):** Quantifies all benefits and costs in monetary terms, yielding an ROI. For example, comparing the cost of OTOS against savings from fewer appointments or medications【31†L332-L341】.
- **Cost–Effectiveness/Utility (CEA/CUA):** Measures cost per unit outcome, such as cost per point improvement on a tinnitus severity scale, or cost per quality-adjusted life-year (QALY) gained【31†L339-L347】. NICE traditionally uses cost per QALY, so estimating an incremental cost per QALY versus usual care can be persuasive for NHS bodies.
- **Budget Impact Model (BIM):** Projects the net budget change for the NHS if OTOS is adopted. The NHS Evaluation Guide notes that for large-scale roll-outs, a BIM “is more suitable” as it highlights the potential ROI to commissioners based on their population and assumptions【29†L143-L152】. OTOS should build a BIM showing, for example, how national adoption would change expenditures (e.g. fewer specialist visits, shorter waiting lists)【29†L143-L152】.
- **Pilot data collection:** Early pilots must plan to collect usage, outcomes, and cost data. The NHS Evaluation Toolkit advises establishing baseline data and consistent collection across sites【29†L163-L172】. OTOS’s pilot protocol should specify data on service use, symptom scores, quality of life, and any resource savings (e.g. clinician time freed), so these can feed into the economic analysis.
- **ROI and business case:** Ultimately, the goal is a clear business case for NHS adoption. This means packaging outcomes into value (patient benefit) and money (cost savings). As one NHS guide puts it: “by purchasing the most effective technology, not just the cheapest, we’ll improve patient care, cut waiting times and drive long-term cost savings”【21†L119-L127】. OTOS’s materials should mirror this language, emphasizing how its clinical effectiveness translates into system efficiency.

## Procurement Pathways and Contracting

Understanding NHS procurement rules is vital. For healthcare services, the Light Touch Regime of the Public Contracts Regulations typically applies【33†L124-L131】. Key points for OTOS are:

- **Threshold and advertisement:** Any clinical service contract above the (current) threshold (~£589k as of 2017) must be published in the Official Journal of the EU (OJEU) and Contracts Finder, with a transparent procurement process【33†L124-L131】. If OTOS expects to contract with an ICB or NHS England, the consortium should budget for a full tender unless an exemption applies.
- **Single-provider negotiations:** If a market assessment shows only OTOS can deliver the service, a commissioner can directly negotiate without advertising【33†L136-L144】. Similarly, if only OTOS responds to a small tender, the commissioner can assess its capability and negotiate the contract directly【33†L145-L154】. OTOS should therefore be prepared to demonstrate uniqueness (e.g. patented methodology, clinical evidence) so that it qualifies for this exception if needed.
- **Market engagement via PIN:** Commissioners often use a Prior Information Notice (PIN) to engage the market early【35†L162-L169】. A PIN describes the future service needed and invites providers to co-develop ideas. OTOS should respond proactively to any PIN related to tinnitus or mental health support services. If feasible, OTOS could even work with a commissioner to draft a PIN, shaping the spec in its favor. Importantly, a PIN does **not** commit the commissioner to award a contract, but it is a transparent first step【35†L162-L169】.
- **Competitive process:** If multiple providers are interested or identified, a competitive tender (with published award criteria) must be run【33†L149-L154】. OTOS should prepare strong bid documentation (including the evidence and economic case above) that scores highly on clinical impact, patient benefit, and value criteria. Using clear NHS language (“meets the specified outcomes”, “cost per patient”) will help commissioners evaluate objectively.
- **Procurement vehicles:** In parallel, OTOS might explore existing NHS procurement frameworks. For example, **Digital Health Innovation Hubs**, NHS Supply Chain or the London Procurement Partnership now support digital and MedTech procurement【21†L135-L143】. The new DHSC guidelines envisage partnering with NHS Supply Chain and LPP to roll out value-based procurement across trusts【21†L135-L143】. Engaging with these bodies early could smooth access (e.g. being on a pre-approved supplier list for innovations).
- **Standards compliance:** As noted, any NHS procurement of digital tools will check DTAC compliance【43†L49-L57】 and may require DTAC certification. Therefore, OTOS should compile DTAC evidence (safety cases, security reports, etc.) and have it ready to present with proposals【43†L49-L57】. This ensures OTOS is procurement-ready for NHS buyers.
