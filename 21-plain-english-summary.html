Academic Funding Pack — 21

In plain English: ADHD, addiction, and the gap between them

A lay summary for anyone who isn't a specialist — a PPI group, an ethics-adjacent reader, or an academic contact outside psychiatry. No jargon, no statistics to defend — but no softening the subject either.

PurposeTo explain OTOS Continuity™ in ordinary language, without technical or clinical terms, so anyone can understand what's being proposed and ask informed questions.
Reading timeUnder 3 minutes
Written forNon-specialist readers, PPI contributors, ethics-adjacent audiences
01

The problem, in plain terms

Undiagnosed or unsupported ADHD is one of the most common routes into addiction. Many adults in addiction treatment turn out to have ADHD nobody has picked up — and living with unmanaged ADHD makes it much harder to stay engaged with an addiction service, a recovery programme, or ordinary daily routine.

So the same person is often moving between an ADHD assessment, addiction treatment, and recovery or wellbeing support — three separate services that don't automatically tell each other what's happening.

If that person goes quiet — misses an appointment, stops replying, drifts away — often nobody notices straight away, because no single service is watching the whole picture. By the time it's noticed, it can already be a crisis: a relapse, an A&E admission, or worse.

02

What we want to try

We want to test a simple idea: a way of gently keeping track of whether someone at the ADHD–addiction–recovery interface is still connected to the help they've agreed to — without creating a new clinical service, and without anyone having to log in to yet another system.

In practice, this could mean a text message check-in, a record of whether someone turned up to their ADHD assessment or addiction appointment, or a note that one service has safely handed someone to another. Nothing about it changes what the existing ADHD, addiction or recovery services do — it just makes the gaps between them visible.

03

What it is not

This is not a diagnosis tool, a treatment, or a therapy. It does not prescribe or change medication. It is not an emergency or crisis service — if someone is in crisis, existing emergency routes still apply exactly as they do now. It does not create one shared medical record — every service keeps its own records exactly as it does today.

04

Who would be involved

If this goes ahead as a small test (a "pre-pilot"), it would involve around 50 adults at the ADHD–addiction interface over about 12 weeks, working alongside NHS ADHD teams, addiction and recovery services (e.g. CGL/CRS), and alcohol liaison and wellbeing partners who already provide care — not instead of them. Nobody would be enrolled without agreeing to take part first.

05

What it would be like for someone taking part

  • They would be asked, clearly, whether they want to take part — and could say no, or stop at any time.
  • They might get an occasional check-in message.
  • If they went quiet for a while, a real person on their existing care team — not an algorithm acting alone — would be prompted to check in.
  • Their actual care (appointments, treatment, support) would carry on exactly as it does now.
06

Data and privacy, in plain terms

Only the minimum needed to know "are they still connected" would be recorded — things like whether a message was sent, replied to, or went quiet, and whether a handoff between services happened. This is not the same as someone's medical or treatment record, and it would never replace it. People would be asked for their consent before any of this is collected, and could withdraw it.

07

Why this exists

This idea comes from one person's own experience: undiagnosed ADHD that he self-medicated with alcohol for over thirty years, without realising why. When he was finally assessed, a long wait for NHS ADHD treatment meant he stayed inside addiction services that could only treat what they could see — not the ADHD underneath it. He ended up having to find, and pay for, his own way out.

A psychiatrist's letter written after that private reassessment now sits on file with the NHS team that had him waiting. It's one person's case, not proof this works for everyone — but it's the reason this is being tested properly, rather than left as one man's story.

08

What happens next

Right now, nothing has been tested yet. We're at the stage of finding the right academic partner to help design a small, careful test of this idea — one that would be reviewed and approved through the normal research and ethics processes before anyone takes part.

If you only read one line

We want to test whether a simple, consent-based way of noticing when someone at the ADHD–addiction interface goes quiet — reviewed by real people, not acted on by a computer alone — helps them stay connected to the ADHD and addiction support they've already agreed to. Nothing else changes.
