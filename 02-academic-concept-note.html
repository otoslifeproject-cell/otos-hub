Academic Concept Note

Testing a non-clinical continuity layer for adults at the ADHD–addiction–recovery interface. A short orientation for a prospective academic partner, before any application is drafted.

Intended readerA prospective PI or clinical academic
LengthOne sitting, no appendices
01

The problem

Unmanaged or undiagnosed ADHD is a well-documented route into addiction: people self-medicate an untreated neurological condition, often with alcohol, long before anyone identifies ADHD as the underlying driver. Once someone is in addiction treatment, an ADHD assessment waiting list can trap them there — the root cause goes unaddressed while the addiction service treats the symptom it can see.

Where a pathway spans an ADHD assessment, an addiction service and a recovery or wellbeing service, the connective tissue between them (did the referral land, did the person show up, did anything happen in the silence between appointments) is often invisible until it has already failed — a relapse, a repeat A&E presentation, or someone forced to escalate their own crisis to be taken seriously by any one of the three.

This model is founder-originated: shaped directly by one person's documented experience of unmanaged ADHD self-medicated into addiction, an ADHD assessment wait he stayed inside addiction services for, and a private route out he had to engineer himself. See 06 — Evidence Anchors for how that case is used as evidence, and its limits.

02

What ADHD takes away, against what services assume

The mismatch is specific, not general. Addiction and recovery services are largely built on the assumption that a person can self-initiate and sustain engagement between contacts. Unmanaged ADHD removes exactly that capacity — which is why the highest-risk window is not the appointment itself, but the silence either side of it.

What unmanaged ADHD takes awayWhat the service assumes / needs
Working memory for dates, times, next stepsRemembering and attending scheduled appointments unprompted
Task initiation / executive functionSelf-starting the next step without being asked
A felt sense of "next week" as realSustained motivation across a multi-week programme
Tolerance for repetitive, unstructured formatsSustained engagement with repetitive group or step-based formats (e.g. 12-step, weekly groups)
Dopamine / emotional self-regulationSelf-soothing without a substitute stimulant or behaviour
Independent follow-through on take-home tasksCompleting between-session work unsupervised

This is the gap OTOS Continuity™ is built to sit inside — not by replacing what an addiction, recovery or wellbeing service does, but by carrying the small, consistent reinforcement (a check-in, a reminder, a nudge toward a step already agreed with the person's own care team) that unmanaged ADHD makes hard to sustain alone. The mechanism is intervening earlier, in the gap itself, rather than waiting for the next scheduled contact to notice someone has drifted.

03

What OTOS Continuity™ is

A non-clinical Continuity Management Layer.

OTOS creates an agreed, non-clinical Continuity Thread — a visible record of the user journey built from consent, prompts, replies, silence, handoff receipts, attendance signals, drift signals and human-review events. It is one person, one thread, with separate service records and authorised partner views. It is not a shared clinical record and not an EPR.

OTOS does not diagnose, prescribe, treat, provide therapy, provide crisis care or make safeguarding decisions. Clinical teams retain full responsibility for assessment, diagnosis, treatment, medication and monitoring throughout. See 05 — Governance Boundary Note for the full statement.

04

Why this is a research question, not just a product

Whether a non-clinical continuity layer measurably changes attendance, drop-off and re-engagement in real pathways — and how — is an open empirical question. It sits inside implementation science, digital mental health and health services research, and touches directly on Wellcome's interest in mechanisms of engagement and "waiting well" in mental-health systems.

05

What exists today

A working, non-clinical model and a proposed Pre-Pilot / First-50 feasibility design (see 04). No efficacy claims are made from this stage — it is feasibility and acceptability evidence, generated to support a properly designed, independently led pilot.

06
