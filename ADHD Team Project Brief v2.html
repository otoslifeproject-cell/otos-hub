The clinical anchor

The diagnosis is the thesis, in clinical language.

In April 2026 I received a diagnostic letter from an independent GMC-registered consultant psychiatrist, addressed to you, the CPFT Adult ADHD Team. In one sentence it names the exact pattern your assessments are built to catch:

From the diagnostic letter · Psymplicity · April 2026
“Adult ADHD, previously complicated with excess alcohol use to self-medicate inadequately managed ADHD symptoms.”

Independent consultant psychiatrist · addressed to the CPFT Adult ADHD Team.

Please note the order of that sentence carefully.

Not “ADHD plus alcohol use disorder, treated separately.”

Not “alcohol use disorder, with ADHD as comorbidity.”

The diagnosis, in the order that matters: Adult ADHD — complicated by self-medication. In my case, the order matters: the ADHD was the driver; the drinking was the visible symptom.

The order of operations is everything — and NICE and the Royal College both advise that co-morbid substance misuse should be jointly managed, with general adult and addiction services aware of each other’s plans. This is precisely the cohort we want to help you identify early — where your assessment has the most to gain. My full letter accompanies this brief by email.

This letter is about my care — not proof that OTOS works. It is proof of something only your team can act on: the driver underneath this kind of addiction is real, documented and, once assessed, clinically addressable.

The comparison, item for item

What recovery needs, ADHD has already taken away.

You are likely aware that this cohort relapses repeatedly, even through good support, and I'm sure you carry the frustration of watching it. Here is why it happens. Line up what successful addiction recovery requires against what unmanaged ADHD impairs — not loosely, item for item. The treatment model and the disorder are pointed in opposite directions.

CapacityUnmanaged ADHD impairsAddiction recovery requires
Executive functionDifficulty planning, sequencing and self-regulating; a starved prefrontal cortexDaily planning, structure and the delay of short-term gratification
Focus & attentionChronic distractibility; hard to sustain attention on non-stimulating tasksDeep, continuous focus through long therapy sessions and recovery work
Working memoryForgets coping strategies, misses appointments, loses the threadRetaining new tools, tracking triggers, holding daily routines
Impulse controlReflexive, immediate urges; acting before the pausePausing, reflecting and resisting cravings in the moment
Emotional regulationDysregulation; distress arrives fast and overwhelmsTolerating distress and riding out craving without the substance
Routine & follow-throughHard to initiate and sustain mundane, unrewarding routinesRepetition and routine that physically rebuild new pathways
Reward & arousalUnder-arousal and a pull toward immediate rewardA stable baseline — tolerating boredom and delayed reward
Neuroplastic learningBlunted adaptation; resists routines without immediate payoffRapid, healthy re-wiring to replace deep-seated habits

 Read it as a model, not a verdict. The mapping is an explanatory framing drawn from the published account of ADHD and recovery; presentations vary and the alignment is directional. It is offered to show why this cohort struggles with standard recovery — not to diagnose any individual.

The science of sticking. NICE recommends medication and structured CBT in combination — and in adult studies, medication-plus-CBT outperforms either alone. The medication doesn't do the recovery work; it creates the conditions where it can finally land. Full evidence in the OTOS Evidence Dossier.

Why the wait is the problem

Still on your review list since 2020 — and I lived what that wait costs.

This is not a criticism of your team — it is the clearest case for why catching ADHD earlier matters so much.

My ADHD has been on a review list since 2020 — while I crossed the invisible line into physical and mental dependency on alcohol. I cycled through A&E, addiction services and mental-health teams and the crisis loop continued. I am still waiting for my medication review today. I survived the sharpest version of what that wait costs.

After a year of showing commitment to CGL, I was funded for a three-month residential rehab — one I chose for its reputation and its official dual-diagnosis status, certain it would finally address my ADHD. Instead, on arrival — among remarkable people doing real care — my ADHD medication (methylphenidate) was removed because it was a stimulant. The underlying condition was switched off by the treatment system itself. I came out with no correct medication, and no self-medication left to reach for — and for the first time in my life I felt the full, raw force of unmedicated ADHD with nothing to quiet it. The relapse that followed was not a treatment failure. It was a system-generated, ADHD-driven relapse — exactly the kind your assessment, made early, is built to prevent.

No part of the system thought to connect the loop — exactly the thread OTOS is designed to hold, with your team's assessment at the centre of it.

The chaos was never a character defect. It was an untreated brain doing its best to cope.

When ADHD is left waiting and services run on rigid criteria, addiction turns up and people do desperate things to qualify for help. That is a design problem, not a moral failing and it is the gap - the space that OTOS is built to cover. The most honest line in my whole story was said by the alcohol liaison nurse at Addenbrooke's on my third and final, engineered detox: “You're right. There's nowhere else for me to send you. You've done CGL and all the mental health services. If you get anywhere with your idea, I'd appreciate being able to signpost more support". That nurse ensured my discharge letter had exactly the right wording for me to finally get to the correct medication outside of the NHS. She didn't have to but she knew it was the ADHD too.

You are the answer in this story — the team best placed to catch the missed driver and switch the trajectory. What is missing is an agreed early route in, and a thread that survives the handoff back out. Once someone is on that thread, a partner like CGL sees only a continuity token — never clinical notes — enough to know the ADHD is being addressed, that agreed active-waiting support is underway where approved, or that micro check-ins are tracking connection, mood or drift. Light-touch reinforcement helps new behavioural patterns form — and stops the person vanishing the moment they leave your room.

One team of services

One person, held across the whole team.

No single service can hold this cohort alone — and none should have to. Your assessment, CGL and CRS’s addiction work, RCE Wellbeing’s lifestyle courses, the mental-health teams, the therapy room — each does something the others can’t. OTOS replaces none of it. It holds the thread between every part, so the person stays connected, educated and visible as they move from your room to CGL, over to RCE Wellbeing and back.  Alcohol Liaison at Addenbrooke's may be able to contribute as a touchpoint during the intervention test. If the test works and a future pilot is governed, the bedside could become a major upstream intervention point — beneficial to every other service and cost centre downstream. That is future hypothesis, not a current service claim. The point is that every service stays strong at what it does best. The patient stops falling through the gaps between them. If they touch Alcohol Liaison, we know. If the active-waiting support is reached, that can be receipted. If the learning is holding, that can be measured. A KPI-level, commissioner-grade learning report can show what happened across the cohort. OTOS turns the gap into opportunity, the risk area into evidence, and that is the win — not one service doing more, but the whole team of services finally working as one around a single person.

Meeting in the middle

How the thread works — and where you come in.

OTOS Continuity is a non-clinical layer that sits around the person — not between your records. It does not diagnose, prescribe, treat or replace anyone, and it does not access, merge or write to any EPR. OTOS avoids dependency on full record integration. It would still be consent-led, IG-reviewed and governed, but it does not require services to merge records or write into one another’s EPRs. Rather than wiring services together, it carries a single consent-led continuity token around the individual. Each service interacts only with the person and with the token — never with each other’s notes. So CGL, your team and RCE can each see that follow-through is happening, and that the ADHD is being addressed, without ever seeing inside one another’s systems. Not joining up the services — holding the person across them.
