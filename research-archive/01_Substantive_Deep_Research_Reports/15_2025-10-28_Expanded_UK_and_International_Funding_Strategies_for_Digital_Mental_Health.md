## Executive Summary  
Five major UK funders — **NIHR**, **Nesta**, **Wellcome Trust**, **Comic Relief** (with CAST), and the **National Lottery Community Fund (NLCF)** — exhibit remarkably consistent success patterns in digital mental health awards (2020–2025). Despite each having a unique mission and remit, successful funding applications across these programs tend to share common characteristics, while proposals often fail for a familiar set of reasons. Below we summarize the key ingredients of winning projects and frequent pitfalls that lead to rejection:

- **Winning projects combine:**  
  - **Rigorous yet proportionate evaluation plans**, with realistic recruitment and retention targets. Funders expect applicants to demonstrate that the proposed research or service can recruit participants at the needed scale and measure clear outcomes without an overengineered design【6†L228-L236】. For example, NIHR often requires feasibility data and “recruitment maths” (calculations showing how each site will enroll enough patients) to justify a full trial, along with well-defined endpoints for success【6†L228-L236】.  
  - **Lived-experience participation in governance and design**, backed by dedicated budgets. Involvement of people with lived experience (patients, service users or community members) is built into successful projects from the start, not as an afterthought. Many funders now insist on evidence of co-design and even include **public co-applicants** on grants (with appropriate compensation) to ensure the project is grounded in real-world experience【12†L146-L154】. NLCF, for instance, asks how communities and people with direct experience will shape and lead the work【25†L257-L265】【34†L107-L115】. Wellcome and NIHR similarly expect **“lived experience experts”** or patient representatives to be part of the team and decision-making【4†L205-L213】【34†L109-L117】.  
  - **Named delivery partners at application stage**, demonstrating ready routes to implement and scale. Strong applications identify key partners such as NHS sites, local authorities, or voluntary/community sector (VCSE) organizations *before* funding is awarded. Having NHS Trusts or local charities on board (often with letters of support or as co-applicants) signals that the project can be executed in real settings【4†L205-L213】. For research-focused funders like NIHR, listing NHS network support (e.g. NIHR Clinical Research Network sites) is a positive signal. For innovation funders like Nesta or Comic Relief, having a tech partner or a service delivery charity involved from the start shows the team has the capacity to build and deploy the solution【33†L231-L239】.  
  - **Sustainability and reuse plans beyond the grant period.** Nearly all funders now look for a vision of what happens after their money runs out. Successful proposals include plans for long-term sustainability – whether through integration into health services, follow-on investment, revenue generation, or handoff to a partner – to avoid a funding “cliff edge.” They also often commit to openness and reuse of the solution. For example, Comic Relief’s Tech for Good programme favors projects that **“have a clear sustainability plan”** and **“are shareable with other parts of the non-profit sector”**, rather than one-off builds that vanish after the grant【33†L197-L202】. Similarly, NLCF expects applicants to consider the legacy of a project (how it will continue benefiting the community and be maintained in the long run)【34†L165-L173】.

- **Common reasons proposals are rejected include:**  
  - *Over-optimistic recruitment or weak feasibility.* A frequent downfall, especially in clinical research proposals, is claiming you will recruit large numbers of participants without evidence or a concrete plan. NIHR panels often flag “**weak recruitment**” planning as a reason for rejection – since poor recruitment is the top reason trials fail in practice【6†L228-L236】. Applications must show an understanding of the target population, recruitment rates, and retention strategies (e.g. past pilot data or letters from sites confirming access to patients).  
  - *Vague comparators or outcomes.* Funders look for well-defined outcome measures and (where applicable) a clear comparator or control condition. If a proposal cannot answer “Compared to what?” or precisely what success looks like, it will likely be scored down. For instance, an RCT proposal needs a clear control group or standard-of-care comparator; an innovation project needs specific metrics of impact. Ambiguity here signals the team hasn’t fully thought through how to prove effectiveness.  
  - *Under-priced or under-resourced delivery.* Many rejected budgets are unrealistically low for the proposed scope of work. **Under-costing** a project (e.g. not accounting for staff time, technology maintenance, recruiting costs, or evaluation) might seem frugal, but reviewers know it leads to failure or corner-cutting. Funders would rather see a higher but justified budget than a low figure that cannot deliver. Adequate resourcing for evaluation and public involvement is especially critical – some programs provide guidance on costing these appropriately【11†L1-L8】.  
  - *Tokenistic Patient and Public Involvement/Engagement (PPIE).** Applications that include PPIE only superficially – for example, a lone token patient representative with no clear role or a last-minute survey of users – are viewed critically. Genuine co-production and governance roles for people with lived experience are now the norm in winning bids【34†L107-L115】【34†L117-L125】. Funders can tell when public involvement is an afterthought versus meaningfully embedded (such as a community advisory panel or co-design workshops with budget allocated).  
  - *“Cliff-edge” sustainability (no plan beyond the grant).* If a project is likely to cease as soon as funding ends, or requires indefinite grant support, funders worry about wasted potential. Proposals should address how the innovation or service will continue or scale up after the grant: Who will fund or adopt it? Will it be integrated into existing systems? Lack of a credible continuation plan (“cliff-edge” ending) is a red flag. Reviewers often ask, *“What is the exit or next stage if this is successful?”* A strong application might, for example, name a partner (like an NHS trust, company or commissioner) ready to pick it up, or detail a revenue model or future grant opportunities to pursue.

In summary, across these funders, the **“success formula”** is a balanced blend of evidence and innovation: demonstrate that your idea works (or will be rigorously tested), involve the right people and partners in a meaningful way, and plan for both impact and longevity. Below we delve into each funder’s specific focus and expectations, and then highlight strategies to secure funding efficiently.

## Funders and Their Funding Criteria in Digital Mental Health  

Understanding the nuances of each major funder’s priorities can help tailor an application for maximum success. The table below outlines, for each funder, what they tend to fund, the evidence they expect, positive signals in partnerships/governance, and frequent pitfalls to avoid:

### NIHR (National Institute for Health and Care Research)  
- **What they fund:** NIHR backs applied health and care research, with an emphasis on pragmatic clinical trials and “what works” evaluations. In digital mental health, NIHR funding often comes through programs like *Research for Patient Benefit*, *Health Technology Assessment*, or *Invention for Innovation (i4i)* – focusing on interventions that can be tested in real-world settings to improve care. Projects typically involve evaluating an app, digital therapy, or service model within the NHS or community care.  
- **Evidence expectations:** NIHR expects a clear path from feasibility to definitive trial. A strong proposal shows that initial feasibility work has been or will be done, and uses that to justify the next, larger study【6†L228-L236】. Applicants should provide **“recruitment maths”** – e.g. X clinics recruiting Y patients per month – to demonstrate the targeted sample size is achievable【6†L228-L236】. Outcomes (endpoints) must be well-defined and clinically relevant (e.g. validated symptom scales, health economic measures). Essentially, NIHR is looking for robust scientific method: a plausible hypothesis, a trial design with appropriate power calculations or rationale, and a plan to reach that recruitment goal. Feasibility studies are often welcomed (and sometimes required) before a full trial, but they must have a *clear trajectory* to patient benefit and a larger evaluation down the line【6†L232-L239】.  
- **Partners & governance signals:** Because NIHR funds work in health services, having **NHS partners** is crucial. Successful bids usually list specific NHS sites or Clinical Research Network clusters that will host the research, often with *letters of support from clinicians or service directors*. This signals that the study can access patients and resources. NIHR also strongly encourages involving patients/the public in research design – e.g. including **paid public co-applicants** on the grant and a well-described PPIE strategy【12†L146-L154】. An application that names a patient advocacy group or a few individuals with lived experience as part of the project team (with defined roles and budget) demonstrates this commitment. Additionally, endorsements like support from an NIHR Applied Research Collaboration (ARC) or local health authority can help.  
- **Frequent pitfalls:** The **most common pitfall is a weak recruitment plan or an unrealistic assumption about participant flow**【6†L228-L236】. NIHR committees often reject otherwise interesting studies if they doubt the team can enroll enough eligible participants in the timeframe. Another pitfall is having **vague comparators or endpoints** – for instance, proposing to test a digital tool without specifying what it will be compared against (standard care, a waiting list, etc.) or using a nebulous outcome like “improve wellbeing” without quantifiable measures. Insufficient **PPIE** is also criticized; if the proposal just says “we will consult patients” with no detail, it will be seen as tokenistic. **Under-costing** is a related risk: NIHR reviewers know what things cost (e.g. NHS staff time, software development, trial management) and will flag budgets that seem too low to do a quality study. Finally, proposals that appear **too exploratory or misaligned** with NIHR’s mandate (for example, very early-stage tool development without any evaluation, or research without a clear pathway to practice) may be deemed out of scope and get turned down.

### Nesta  
- **What they fund:** Nesta is an innovation foundation that invests in mission-aligned health innovations, often through specific challenge programs or impact investment funds. In the 2020–2025 period, Nesta’s health-related funding has focused on areas like mental health, healthy aging, and public health, backing projects that use technology or new models to achieve better outcomes at scale. Unlike NIHR, Nesta is less about rigorous trials and more about **scaling up promising innovations** – they might fund a digital mental health solution to reach more users or build evidence, including supporting social enterprises or startups (sometimes equity investments, other times grants).  
- **Evidence expectations:** Nesta pioneered a **Standards of Evidence** framework, requiring applicants to declare their current evidence level and how they will move up to the next level【17†L208-L218】【17†L214-L218】. This ranges from **Level 1 (theory and observational evidence)** up to **Level 5 (replicated RCT evidence)【17†L208-L216】**. A Nesta application must openly state, for example, “We currently have evidence at Level 2 (some positive outcomes in a pilot) and aim to achieve Level 3 by conducting a controlled trial”【17†L208-L216】. This clarity helps Nesta ensure the project’s evaluation plans are *proportionate*: early-stage innovations aren’t expected to have RCT proof yet, but they *are* expected to have a plan for gathering more rigorous evidence as they grow【17†L214-L218】. Over-claiming impact without evidence is a big no-no – Nesta reviewers are wary of grand claims not backed by data. Instead, a credible theory of change plus initial data and a *roadmap to strengthen evidence* is the winning formula.  
- **Partners & governance signals:** Nesta looks for signs that an innovation can **scale and sustain impact**. This often means a strong team with both subject matter and business/technical expertise, and any partnerships that will help reach more people. For instance, a mental health app might bolster its case by partnering with an NHS trust, a school network, or a corporate employer to reach users. Having a **route-to-market or scale strategy** is key – whether through the public sector, B2C channels, or integration into existing platforms. A good Nesta proposal will mention how it fits into the broader system and any support for expansion (incubator backing, follow-on investors, etc.). Additionally, Nesta-funded projects typically include a **MEL (Monitoring, Evaluation, Learning) plan** to continually measure impact; showing that you have an evaluator or learning partner can strengthen the bid. Governance-wise, while not as formal as NIHR’s PPIE, involving end-users in design and having advisors with lived experience or industry insight is seen as a plus in demonstrating the solution is user-centered and viable.  
- **Frequent pitfalls:** One pitfall is **lack of an evidence-growth plan** – if an applicant cannot articulate how they will use Nesta’s funding to demonstrably increase their evidence level (for example, from anecdotal success to a formal study), it will likely be declined. **Over-claiming outcomes** is another: saying “this app will revolutionize therapy for everyone” without proof will raise skepticism. Reviewers prefer modest, credible claims with data to back them. Also, **no clear path to scale** will hurt a proposal; if it seems like a nice pilot that will remain small, Nesta might not prioritize it. They want to see that the innovation, if proven, can reach tens of thousands of people (and that the team has thought about the business model or partnerships to do so). Finally, not aligning with Nesta’s specific thematic priorities is a common reason for rejection – a project might be innovative but if it doesn’t fit the particular call or mission area Nesta is focusing on (e.g. if Nesta’s call was for youth anxiety solutions and your project is for general wellbeing with no youth focus), it risks being off-topic and not funded.

### Wellcome Trust  
- **What they fund:** Wellcome Trust is a large research charity, and in recent years it has made **mental health science** one of its strategic priorities【3†L37-L40】. Notably, Wellcome has been interested in identifying **“active ingredients”** of effective mental health interventions – essentially the mechanisms or core components that drive positive change. In the digital mental health arena, Wellcome launched funding calls to **evaluate scalable digital interventions**, especially those targeting anxiety, depression, and psychosis in young people【3†L1-L9】【4†L205-L213】. They fund multi-disciplinary research teams to both test the efficacy of digital tools and advance scientific understanding of *how* and *why* they work. Wellcome grants in this space tend to be sizable (often multi-year, multi-million awards) aimed at projects that can ultimately scale globally, rather than small local pilots.  
- **Evidence expectations:** Wellcome expects **rigorous, hypothesis-driven research**. A proposal to Wellcome must have a strong scientific rationale, often centred on an “active ingredient” or theoretical mechanism. For example, instead of just “we will roll out a mindfulness app,” a Wellcome-style project might ask “does improving daily sleep patterns (the active ingredient) via a digital app reduce adolescent anxiety, and through what biological or psychological pathways?” Thus, **mechanism-driven hypotheses** and plans to test them (e.g. mediators, biomarkers, etc.) are important. At the same time, Wellcome is looking for interventions that are **scalable** if effective – so they expect teams to have at least a **minimum viable product (MVP) and some feasibility data** before scaling up【4†L205-L213】. Indeed, a recent Wellcome digital mental health call specified that teams *“must have… a minimum viable product and feasibility data”* prior to seeking full trial funding【4†L205-L213】. So, while they fund rigorous trials, they are not usually funding raw prototyping from scratch – some initial evidence should exist. They also encourage innovative methodologies, from experimental psychology to data science, as long as the approach will yield solid evidence on outcomes and mechanisms.  
- **Partners & governance signals:** A unique feature of Wellcome’s approach is the requirement for **multi-disciplinary teams** and **diverse partnerships**. They often want to see collaborations between academia and those who can implement at scale. The **ideal team** might include clinical researchers, data scientists, designers, an industry or NGO partner that can deliver the digital intervention at scale, and **people with lived experience** of the mental health conditions being addressed【4†L205-L213】【4†L288-L298】. In fact, Wellcome explicitly stated that teams *“must have… an organisation which can take the intervention to scale…and lived experience experts”* as co-applicants【4†L205-L213】. This means a strong application could have, say, a tech company or large charity on board to eventually disseminate the product, and community representatives guiding the work. Governance structures like advisory boards with young people or patients, ethics oversight, and integration of user feedback in the trial design all signal a thoughtful project. Wellcome is also global in outlook – even for UK-based projects, showing awareness of how the findings could apply to other contexts (or involving international partners) can be a plus.  
- **Frequent pitfalls:** The **biggest pitfall is misalignment with Wellcome’s priorities**. If an application does not clearly fit the specific call (e.g. it targets an age group or disorder outside the remit, or doesn’t focus on the mechanistic science they’re after), it will be triaged out. Another common issue is **insufficient preliminary work** – a proposal that sounds like it’s still at ideation stage (“we have an idea for an app and need to develop it and then test it”) would be too early for Wellcome’s later-stage awards, which expect an MVP and pilot data. **Tokenistic involvement** of lived experience is also a risk; given Wellcome’s emphasis on genuine participation, an application that lacks concrete roles for people with lived experience (or just has a perfunctory letter of support from a patient group with no integration into the project) will seem weak next to others that fully embrace co-production. Additionally, Wellcome reviewers look for **scientific rigor**, so a proposal with a sloppy design, vague analysis plan, or lack of consideration of risks and biases (e.g. digital ethics, data privacy, representative sampling) may be rejected even if the idea is exciting. Essentially, a successful Wellcome bid in digital mental health must hit a sweet spot of *innovative but evidence-grounded*, and *ambitious but credible*. Projects that are too broad or lack focus (“we will do a bit of everything to see what works”) often falter, whereas those with a clear scientific question and the team to answer it tend to succeed.

### Comic Relief / CAST (Tech for Good)  
- **What they fund:** Comic Relief, in partnership with **CAST** (the Centre for Acceleration of Social Technology), ran the **Tech for Good** funding programmes through 2016–2022 (often co-funded by Paul Hamlyn Foundation). These grants support UK non-profits to plan, build, and launch new digital products or services that tackle social issues (including mental health). The emphasis is on *user-led design* – funding charities to develop tech that meets the needs of the people they serve. Typically, grants were around £50k–£70k over 9–12 months to build a working product (like a mental health support app, a platform connecting users to services, etc.), with a strong cohort-based support model (grantees received not just money but mentorship in service design and digital development)【19†L65-L73】【19†L99-L107】. By 2020, the programme introduced an **“Explore”** phase offering even smaller grants (~£5k) purely for scoping and prototyping ideas before a full build【19†L95-L103】【19†L109-L117】 – reflecting the philosophy that early-stage discovery is crucial. In summary, Comic Relief/CAST fund the journey from concept to prototype to launch for tech-for-good solutions, ensuring organizations build the right thing in the right way.  
- **Evidence expectations:** This funding stream does not demand academic-style evidence or RCTs, but it does expect to see **evidence of user research and prototyping** to justify the solution. A strong Tech for Good application will demonstrate that the organization has identified a specific user need through engagement with the target group (interviews, workshops, surveys)【20†L69-L78】【20†L81-L90】, and perhaps tested some assumptions with a minimal prototype. In fact, one of the selection criteria was that projects should be *“focused on specific user needs in their design and delivery”*【33†L197-L202】. Additionally, applicants should show they have considered existing technology and solutions – i.e., they’re not reinventing the wheel without reason. Using **existing tools and lean experimentation** is encouraged (for example, using off-the-shelf platforms to mock up a service before coding from scratch)【20†L99-L107】【20†L113-L122】. While formal evaluation isn’t required at this build stage, the funders expect teams to gather user feedback and iterate. They also look for a **sustainability plan in the context of a digital product** – meaning plans for maintenance, updates, and long-term funding or revenue to keep the service running. This was explicitly stated as a requirement: projects must *“have a clear sustainability plan”* beyond the initial build【33†L197-L202】.  
- **Partners & governance signals:** Tech for Good recognizes that many charities need tech expertise to execute their idea. Thus, having a **tech partner** (such as a digital agency or developer) on board was effectively a requirement for grantees unless the charity had in-house developers【33†L231-L239】. An application would be much stronger if it named a specific agency or skilled volunteer team ready to build the solution, showing the project is feasible within the short grant period. Another important aspect is **openness and collaboration**. The program fostered a cohort mentality – funded teams were expected to share learnings with each other and the sector【33†L203-L211】【33†L213-L220】. A positive signal in governance is a plan for openness: for instance, open-sourcing the code or sharing project insights publicly via the Tech for Good Hub. Also, involving end-users in governance (e.g. a steering group of people with lived experience of the issue) is a plus, as it demonstrates the solution will stay user-centered. Lastly, **VCSE delivery partners** can add value: if a digital tool is meant to be deployed by frontline charities, having one or two such organizations committed to pilot it with their beneficiaries can strengthen the case. For example, a mental health chatbot for teens could partner with a youth club or school for testing.  
- **Frequent pitfalls:** A common pitfall is an **over-ambitious scope** given the resources and timeframe. Some applicants propose very complex platforms or multiple features that cannot realistically be built and tested in 9 months with £50k. The funders prefer a well-defined, focused MVP over a grand plan that is likely to derail. Another issue is **low digital maturity** of the team – if the organization has no prior experience with digital projects and hasn’t secured the right expertise, the assessors may doubt their ability to deliver (this is why securing a tech partner early is so crucial). **Poor user involvement** is also a reason for rejection: proposals that seem to be pushing technology for its own sake, without clear user research or user testing plans, don’t fare well. For instance, coming in with a pre-decided solution (like “we will build an AI chatbot because AI is cool”) without evidence that users want or need it would be viewed skeptically. Additionally, not addressing **data protection and safeguarding** considerations when dealing with vulnerable groups (e.g. mental health service users) can sink an application – the funders want to see that the team has thought about user privacy, consent, and safety in using the digital service. Finally, **lack of sustainability thinking** – if it’s not clear how the project will be maintained post-grant, or who will own and support it long-term, that will be a negative (hence the emphasis on sustainability and hand-off plans in the criteria).

### National Lottery Community Fund (NLCF)  
- **What they fund:** The NLCF is the UK’s largest community funder, supporting thousands of projects for community wellbeing, inclusion, and prevention. In the context of health innovation (including mental health), NLCF tends to fund programs that are **community-led and preventative** – for example, projects that build resilience, social support, or early intervention at the grassroots level. Unlike the other funders discussed, NLCF is less about tech or research per se and more about **outcomes for communities**. That said, many digital mental health projects have been funded through schemes like *Reaching Communities* or *Partnerships* funds, especially if they aim to reach underserved groups or address inequalities. NLCF’s approach from 2020–2025, especially under its *“It Starts With Community”* strategy, emphasizes tackling disadvantage, empowering communities, and scaling what works locally【34†L58-L66】【34†L74-L82】. They will fund operational delivery (staff, activities) and capacity building, and they are open to funding services that use technology as a tool, provided the community need and benefit is clear. Importantly, NLCF funds can support projects at scale (grants can be quite large over multiple years), but also small pilots – it spans a wide range. The key is alignment with community outcomes and engagement, rather than innovation for innovation’s sake.  
- **Evidence expectations:** NLCF asks for **clear outcomes** and a plan to measure them that is *proportionate* to the project size【25†L201-L209】【25†L227-L236】. They do not require academic evaluations or extensive metrics for smaller community projects, but they do expect you to think through how you will know if your project is making a difference. In the application, you must describe the changes you aim to bring about (e.g. reduced loneliness, improved mental wellbeing scores, increased service access for a certain group) and how you’ll capture those outcomes (could be surveys, case studies, simple before/after assessments). NLCF explicitly allows part of the grant to be used for evaluation, advising that the effort should be appropriate to the scale of the project【24†L5-L13】. Inclusion and diversity of participants is also a part of the “evidence” – meaning you should plan to reach those in need and track who benefits. **Inclusion plans** (how you will engage people facing poverty, discrimination, etc.) are scrutinized【25†L229-L238】【25†L251-L260】. Another aspect is safeguarding: if working with vulnerable populations (children, those with mental illness), you should mention training or policies to ensure safety and ethical practice. While not “evidence” in the academic sense, these are assurances that bolster an application’s credibility. Overall, NLCF looks for a **theory of change** – a logical link between the activities funded and the outcomes for people – rather than proof of concept or research data. Prior success or learning can be cited (e.g. “in our previous small pilot, 60% of participants reported improved wellbeing”), but new groups can also be funded if the need is compelling and the plan sensible.  
- **Partners & governance signals:** Collaboration is often a requirement for larger NLCF grants, especially the *Partnerships* program【24†L3-L7】. Strong applications demonstrate that **local partners (VCSE organizations, local health services, schools, etc.)** are actively involved in both design and delivery. NLCF wants to see *genuine* partnership, which includes sharing power and resources, not just a token partner added to look good【34†L123-L131】. One signal of a robust partnership is a history of working together or a clear plan for coordination【25†L293-L300】. Simply listing partners is not enough – the role of each should be explained (e.g. one charity will host a support group while another provides clinical expertise, and both have defined responsibilities)【34†L129-L137】. **Local “anchor” organizations** (like a well-established community center or charity in the target community) are viewed favorably as leads or co-leads, since they lend trust and continuity. Governance-wise, **community involvement in decision-making** is a big theme: NLCF loves to see that the people who the project is for have a say in running it. This could be via a steering committee of beneficiaries, community volunteers in leadership roles, or even constituting user committees that help direct the project【25†L257-L265】【34†L109-L117】. Additionally, having the right policies (e.g. a safeguarding policy, diversity and inclusion policy) and mentioning them shows professionalism. For mental health projects, partnering with healthcare providers for referrals or guidance (like a local NHS mental health team or Mind charity) can strengthen the application by showing it’s well-connected and not working in isolation.  
- **Frequent pitfalls:** One pitfall is a **lack of genuine community engagement** – applications that appear to be designed *for* a community by outsiders, rather than *with* the community, tend to score poorly. If there’s no evidence of consulting or involving the target users (for example, designing a mental health app for a community without any input from those community members), NLCF may doubt its appropriateness. Another related issue is **weak co-design**: using buzzwords about empowerment but not actually showing how people influence the project. NLCF now stresses **community leadership over token consultation**【34†L107-L115】 – so a pitfall would be to treat the community as passive recipients. **Fragile partnerships** are also a risk: if the project heavily relies on collaboration but the partners are not clearly committed or aligned, it could fall apart. Applications that list many partners with vague roles, or brand-new partnerships with no explanation of how they will work, raise concerns. It’s better to have a few solid, well-described partnerships than a long list of organizations with unclear involvement. **Overstretching the project’s scope** (trying to address too many different outcomes or groups without focus) can make funders worry that you won’t deliver any of them well. Clarity and focus are important – for instance, it’s a pitfall to say “we will improve mental health for everyone in our city through 10 different activities” instead of a focused plan for a specific group. Lastly, **sustainability and legacy** need to be thought through: while NLCF understands not all community projects continue indefinitely, they do look for some consideration of what happens after the funding. If an application makes no mention of the future – e.g. training volunteers to continue activities, seeking eventual local authority support, or plans to embed into existing services – it might be viewed as short-sighted. Given NLCF’s emphasis on lasting impact【34†L165-L173】【34†L175-L183】, ignoring this could hurt the proposal’s chances.

## Strategies for Securing Funding Quickly and Effectively  
To maximize the chances of success – and to do so efficiently – applicants should consider the following strategies, distilled from the patterns above:

- **Choose the right funder (and scheme) for your project’s stage and purpose.** One of the quickest ways to get a “yes” is to apply where the fit is strongest. For example, if you have a *research-heavy project with preliminary data*, NIHR or Wellcome Trust might be appropriate. If you have an *early-stage digital idea and need to build a prototype*, a Tech for Good grant (Comic Relief/CAST) or Nesta innovation program would be a better fit than an NIHR trial grant. Align your proposal with the funder’s mission: e.g. NLCF for community-driven work tackling inequalities【34†L58-L66】【34†L74-L82】, Nesta for scalable social innovations, NIHR for clinical efficacy in health services, Wellcome for mechanism-focused mental health science, etc. Applying to the wrong type of funder will waste time – pay close attention to each call’s scope and eligibility.  

- **Study and speak the funder’s language.** Tailor your application using the frameworks and keywords the funder expects. If applying to Nesta, explicitly reference their **Standards of Evidence** and identify your current level【17†L208-L216】. For NIHR, use terms like “feasibility study”, “powered trial”, “PPIE” and ensure your proposal reads like a scientific protocol. With Wellcome, frame your research questions around **mechanisms** and **innovation** in mental health science (show that you understand their focus on “active ingredients” and transformative potential). For NLCF, emphasize community empowerment, partnership, and legacy, echoing their strategy’s focus on addressing disadvantage and enabling people to shape services【34†L58-L66】【34†L107-L115】. Using a funder’s own terminology (correctly) demonstrates alignment and that you’ve done your homework, which reviewers appreciate.

- **Front-load critical feasibility work (even if small scale) to strengthen your case.** If you can demonstrate **quick wins or early validation** before you apply, do it. This could mean piloting a workshop with 10 users and including their feedback, building a rough demo of your app to show proof-of-concept, or securing a letter of interest from an implementation partner. Such steps don’t necessarily require significant funding – they can often be done via volunteer effort, small seed grants, or collaborations. Having this in hand allows you to confidently answer questions about demand, usability, or recruitment. For instance, showing that “in a 1-month trial, 30 teens used our app and we retained 80% at 4 weeks” gives NIHR or Nesta tangible evidence that you can recruit and engage users. It speeds up the funder’s decision-making by reducing their uncertainty.

- **Engage partners and beneficiaries **early** and document those relationships.** Don’t wait until after funding to bring in the NHS site, charity, or user group you need; involve them in the project design now. Not only will this improve your project (through their input), but it also means you can **name them in the application and include support letters**, which is a fast-track to reviewer confidence. A proposal that says “We have partnered with X Mental Health Trust, Y University, and Z Youth Group who will each host and support the project” immediately stands out versus one saying “we will look for partners later.” Ensure you have written commitments (even non-binding MOUs) that you can cite. Likewise, involve people with lived experience in co-design early (through focus groups or as co-authors of the proposal) – then clearly state how they shaped the plan. This proactive approach addresses many common questions upfront and shows you’re ready to hit the ground running.

- **Plan for sustainability from the start, and articulate it clearly.** To get funded quickly, convince funders that their investment won’t be a flash in the pan. Even if long-term funding is uncertain, present a plausible path: e.g. integration into an NHS service line, adoption by commissioners, a subscription model, future grant phases, or capacity-building of volunteers to continue the work. For a digital product, maybe the plan is to open-source it and seek community support, or to license it to healthcare providers. For a community intervention, perhaps local authorities or donors could take over funding if it shows success. Having letters or quotes from such future stakeholders (“If this pilot demonstrates positive outcomes, our CCG will consider commissioning it”) can be golden. The key is to show that you have thought about *“What next?”* in concrete terms. This not only improves your chances of funding (by meeting a key criterion), but it often accelerates the project’s impact timeline – because you’re already networking and paving the way for scale-up as you apply.

- **Learn from previous successful applications or case studies.** Many funders publish summaries of projects they’ve funded or even the lay summaries of proposals. Reviewing those can provide quick insight into what a winning application looks like. For example, NIHR and Wellcome list funded projects on their websites – look at ones in digital mental health to see how they framed their aims and methods. Nesta often writes blog articles about their funded innovations and why they’re excited about them; these can reveal the angles that resonated (e.g. a strong evidence base coupled with a clear growth plan). Comic Relief’s Tech for Good documentation and blogs highlight lessons (such as the importance of user testing and not over-scoping)【19†L75-L84】【19†L95-L103】. By reflecting the qualities of successful projects and avoiding known pitfalls, you effectively shortcut the learning curve. It can also be valuable to reach out to past grantees for tips – many are surprisingly willing to share their experience, which might alert you to funder-specific preferences or unwritten rules.

- **Polish the basics and address potential red flags proactively.** Lastly, ensure your application is *clear, concise, and complete*. Reviewers often have many applications to read – a well-structured proposal with headings, bullet points (where allowed), and plain language explanations will score points for readability. Use the headings or sections the funder’s application form provides to guide your narrative logically (don’t bury the lede; make sure the main idea and its importance are evident from the start). Include an appropriate budget breakdown with justifications to show you’ve done realistic planning. Address common concerns before they are asked: if there is a risk of recruitment shortfall, mention a mitigation strategy; if using technology, mention data security; if working with minors, confirm safeguarding measures. By preempting these issues, you instill confidence and avoid back-and-forth that can delay decisions. Essentially, make the reviewer’s job easy – clearly demonstrate **who, what, why, how, how much, and what next** – and your chances of a quick, positive funding decision will be much improved.

**Conclusion:** Securing health innovation funding in the UK’s mental health sector is highly competitive, but also very achievable with the right preparation. All five major funders discussed have a shared vision of impactful, evidence-informed, and sustainable digital mental health solutions – and they want to fund good projects. By understanding their criteria and learning from the success patterns of 2020–2025, applicants can craft proposals that hit the mark. In practice, this means blending the rigor of research with the creativity of innovation, centering the people who matter (patients, users, communities), and planning not just for a grant but for a journey. Armed with these insights and strategies, you can approach your next funding bid with confidence and a significantly higher chance of success. Good luck!
