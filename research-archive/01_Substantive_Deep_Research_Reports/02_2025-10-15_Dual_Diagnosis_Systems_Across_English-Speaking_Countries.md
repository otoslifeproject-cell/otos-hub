# Comparative Analysis of Dual Diagnosis in English-Speaking Health Systems

Dual diagnosis – the co-occurrence of mental health disorders and substance use disorders – is **common across English-speaking healthcare systems**. In many settings it is now treated as the *expectation, not the exception*, with roughly half of individuals with a serious mental illness having a substance misuse issue (and vice versa)【58†L30-L38】【31†L79-L87】. Below we compare how various systems address dual diagnosis in terms of policy models, service pathways, data/outcomes, and funding. We also explore cross-border arrangements (e.g. Dutch patients in UK rehabs) and highlight lessons and gaps that a program like OTOS can apply.

## NHS England (England)

**Policy & Models:** England’s approach is guided by a **“no wrong door”** philosophy and the principle that caring for co-occurring conditions is *“everyone’s job”*【7†L1373-L1381】. National guidance emphasizes *integrated care* delivered by mainstream services rather than segregated “dual diagnosis” units【6†L1319-L1327】. In fact, NICE and Public Health England discourage standalone dual-diagnosis teams; instead, all mental health and addiction services are expected to be competent in treating co-occurring disorders【6†L1319-L1327】. Trauma-informed, recovery-oriented care has been embedded in best-practice guidelines (recognizing that trauma often underlies both addiction and mental illness)【60†L13-L17】. Many mental health trusts now include substance use specialists or dual-diagnosis practitioners within their teams, and addiction services are encouraged to screen for and address common mental health issues, to ensure no patient is bounced away due to their dual needs【7†L1373-L1381】【66†L831-L840】. 

**Service Pathways:** Despite the policy vision, implementation on the ground has been challenging. Historically, mental health and substance misuse services in England developed **separately** – commissioned by different bodies – which led to fragmentation and patients *“falling between the cracks”*【56†L67-L75】【56†L49-L58】. For years, people with dual diagnosis could face a *revolving door*: mental health services might refuse care until the substance use was addressed, while addiction services might deny treatment if a person’s primary problems were mental health–related【56†L71-L75】【66†L831-L840】. This exclusionary cycle (often based on the false assumption that a patient must achieve abstinence before mental health treatment) is exactly what the “no wrong door” policy aims to eliminate【66†L831-L840】【66†L841-L848】. Modern service pathways therefore stress **concurrent treatment** – e.g. a single care plan with a lead care coordinator – and assertive engagement to keep dual-diagnosis patients connected to care. England’s Increasing Access to Psychological Therapies (IAPT) program and community mental health services are being made more accessible to people with substance use disorders, and specialist initiatives (like “dual diagnosis anonymous” peer groups and outreach teams) have emerged in some areas【10†L21-L25】. However, a 2020 review noted there is still *“no agreed model at scale”* and variable practice, meaning quality of dual diagnosis care can depend on local commitment and clinical leadership【66†L841-L848】.

**Data & Outcomes:** England tracks addiction treatment outcomes via the National Drug Treatment Monitoring System (NDTMS) and mental health outcomes via the Mental Health Services Dataset (MHSDS). Co-occurring conditions are now flagged in these systems【64†L53-L59】. Commissioners use tools like the Treatment Outcome Profile (TOP) for substance misuse and HoNOS for mental health to measure **“distance travelled”** – i.e. improvement in substance use days, psychological well-being, etc., from intake to follow-up【26†L1029-L1037】. These outcome data help evaluate “recovery capital” gains (like stable housing or improved daily functioning) alongside clinical outcomes. Nonetheless, there is recognition that outcomes for dual-diagnosis patients historically lag behind single-disorder patients, due to higher relapse and dropout rates. Reducing waiting times is an ongoing priority: while mental health services aim for access within weeks under NHS standards, in practice dual diagnosis patients can face long waits if they need multiple services sequentially. Recent policy documents call for joint monitoring of waiting times and **“no wrong door”** entry points so that a person is rapidly routed to the right help even if they present to the “wrong” service【66†L841-L848】.

**Funding & Commissioning:** A notable challenge (and difference from the rest of the UK) is England’s **split commissioning** of services. NHS England (via regional Integrated Care Systems) funds mental health trusts, while local authorities (through the public health grant) commission the majority of community alcohol and drug services【56†L67-L75】. This split has historically led to siloed budgets and even “criteria games” – e.g. some addiction services were commissioned to focus only on substance issues and would exclude those with severe mental illness, and vice versa【56†L71-L75】. The government has begun to address this by injecting targeted funds (e.g. £421 million from 2022–2025 for drug treatment improvements) and encouraging joint commissioning between NHS and councils【55†L9-L13】【56†L67-L75】. In some areas, integrated care partnerships are pooling budgets or creating dual-diagnosis posts jointly funded by mental health and addiction services. Still, **funding cuts** over the past decade hit addiction services hard (a ~25% spending reduction in many localities), meaning capacity for specialist dual support is limited【56†L87-L95】【56†L108-L111】. Commissioners are now urged to reverse this “disinvestment” and ensure that contracts incentivize collaborative care. Overall, England has strong policy guidance on paper and some innovative pilots, but gaps remain in consistent delivery – making it a cautionary tale of **“mind the gap”** between strategy and practice.

## NHS Scotland (Scotland)

**Policy & Models:** Scotland’s health system is more structurally integrated – unified regional health boards are responsible for both mental health and addiction services – yet it too has faced dual diagnosis challenges. A 2022 Scottish Government *“The Way Ahead”* review explicitly rejected separate dual-diagnosis teams, noting one such team had **“failed”** in a major city【24†L1037-L1045】. Instead, Scotland embraces a **mainstreaming model**: every mental health and substance use service must collaborate so that people get help for both issues under one roof【24†L1037-L1045】. The mantra is similar to elsewhere: *“everybody’s job and no wrong door.”* The Way Ahead report and a new National Mental Health & Substance Use **protocol** (launched in 2023) call for clearer shared care pathways and responsibilities across services【24†L1037-L1045】【66†L818-L827】. In practical terms, many Scottish health boards use **integrated multidisciplinary teams** or shared care plans. For example, an “Option 8: Integrated mental health and substance use team” model has been piloted, featuring a single referral point, joint assessment by addiction and psychiatry staff, and involvement of third-sector (voluntary) peer support workers【24†L1037-L1045】. Trauma-informed care is emphasized as well – Scottish services acknowledge the high prevalence of trauma and adverse childhood experiences among dual diagnosis patients, and training in trauma-informed practice has been widespread in recent years.

**Service Pathways:** Scotland’s approach benefits from fewer structural silos – mental health and addiction fall under the NHS and are often co-located or closely linked within Health & Social Care Partnerships. This integration means, in theory, fewer people are refused service. Indeed, official policy states that *no patient should be told they cannot be treated because of co-occurring substance use*【66†L831-L840】. However, in practice, gaps have persisted. Past audits found many patients still experienced “ping-pong” between services or had to navigate separate mental health and Alcohol & Drug Partnership systems. The new protocol addresses this by standardizing pathways: for instance, anyone entering addiction treatment should be routinely screened for mental health needs (and vice versa)【51†L7-L14】. Joint care plans are created for complex cases, and some boards have “dual diagnosis nurses” acting as liaisons. To reduce crisis cycling, Scotland has also invested in community crisis teams and assertive outreach specifically for individuals with both psychosis and substance misuse, as they are frequent emergency presenters. A major cultural shift is underway – away from a historical focus on prescribing methadone alone for opioid addiction, toward providing psychological and social support concurrently for those with mental illness. *(Notably, a decade ago it was highlighted that residential rehab beds in Scotland often went underutilized by locals while Dutch patients filled them – see Cross-Border section – due to an over-reliance on maintenance approaches【16†L149-L158】【16†L159-L168】. Scotland has since been reevaluating the balance of community vs. residential care to better serve dual-diagnosis clients.)*

**Data & Outcomes:** Scotland monitors co-occurring cases through national datasets (e.g. Scottish Drug Misuse Database and Scottish Morbidity Records for mental health). A rapid evidence review in 2022 underscored the need for better data-sharing between mental health and addiction services【66†L823-L832】. One positive development is the use of **“Recovery Outcome” tools by Alcohol & Drug Partnerships**, which track improvements in substance use, health, and social functioning. These tools act as *distance-traveled measures* – for example, recording reduction in substance use days or improvements in mental well-being scores over time. Waiting times for both psychological therapy and addiction treatment are publicly reported (Scotland had targets, e.g. 90% starting substance treatment within 3 weeks). Dual diagnosis patients sometimes struggle to meet these targets if services aren’t coordinated. To address this, some areas created *fast-track routes*: if a patient with severe mental illness needs rehab, the referral is treated with high priority, and vice versa for an addicted person needing psychiatric review. Outcome-wise, integrated approaches in Scotland have shown promise. For instance, one study found that adding a dual-diagnosis specialist to community mental health teams improved engagement and reduced relapse rates in that cohort【19†L17-L21】. Still, the government acknowledges more systematic outcomes tracking is required, and a **2025 national audit** of dual diagnosis services is planned to benchmark performance across regions【63†L644-L653】【63†L646-L655】.

**Funding & Commissioning:** NHS Scotland funds most treatment directly through its health boards, in contrast to England’s split system. This means mental health and addiction funding come from the same source, facilitating joint initiatives. Local Alcohol and Drug Partnerships (ADPs) do coordinate additional funding (some of which comes via the Scottish Government’s Substance Use budgets), and these ADPs include health board and council representatives. Social care (including supported housing or social support) is run by local authorities, so coordination with NHS (for clinical care) is crucial【65†L1-L4】. Generally, Scotland’s funding has prioritized harm reduction and community care. Residential rehabilitation was historically limited – around **300 beds total across Scotland in 2007**, of which *approximately 10% were occupied by Dutch patients* funded from abroad【16†L159-L167】【16†L165-L173】. Recently, there’s renewed funding to expand rehab and integrated services (the 2021 Scottish Drug Deaths Taskforce recommended increasing residential treatment access, and the government committed additional funds). Scotland also invests in **training**: the National Trauma Training Programme and Scotland’s Drug Death Taskforce both provided resources to upskill the workforce in dealing with complex co-morbidity. In summary, Scotland’s integrated governance and recent policy focus give it a solid framework; the challenge remains turning adequate funding and training into uniformly excellent front-line practice.

## NHS Wales (Wales)

**Policy & Models:** Wales has been proactive in setting a national framework for co-occurring conditions. Since 2015, the **Service Framework for the Treatment of People with Co-occurring Mental Health and Substance Misuse Problems** has guided NHS Wales and partners【28†L7-L15】【28†L61-L69】. It explicitly demands an **integrated approach**: mental health and substance misuse services must work in tandem, with clear joint protocols and shared care pathways【28†L61-L69】. All Local Health Boards in Wales (which manage both mental health and addiction services) are required to implement this framework alongside local authorities. The framework lays out core principles similar to elsewhere: co-occurring disorders should be *“core business”* for both sets of services; no patient should be refused due to dual needs; and services should be person-centered, holistic, and trauma-informed. Wales also emphasizes **competency-based training** – staff across both sectors must be trained to recognize and respond to dual diagnosis【26†L1023-L1031】. Initiatives like the “Dual Diagnosis Practitioner” roles and joint training workshops have been rolled out in several Welsh regions to build a shared skillset. Notably, **“prudent healthcare”** (a Welsh policy ethos) is applied here: only by prudent, collaborative use of resources – e.g. avoiding duplicate assessments, treating the whole person – can the system effectively help those with complex needs【28†L29-L37】【28†L31-L39】.

**Service Pathways:** In practical terms, Wales uses a **“comprehensive, staged approach to recovery”** for dual diagnosis patients【26†L1071-L1078】【26†L1079-L1081】. This means services provide outreach, engagement, and motivational interventions first (to bring people into care), then coordinated clinical treatment addressing both mental health and substance use, followed by aftercare to sustain recovery. Each Health Board area has Local Mental Health Partnership Boards and Area Planning Boards for Substance Misuse working together – these bodies must each have a lead accountable for implementing the co-occurring conditions framework【26†L1038-L1046】【26†L1039-L1047】. An individual with, say, ADHD, alcohol dependency, and depression in Wales might have their care managed via a joint Care and Treatment Plan: the Community Mental Health Team and the Community Drug & Alcohol Team develop one integrated plan with the person. The Welsh framework also stresses **safeguarding and risk management** for dual diagnosis cases (recognizing higher risks of self-harm, violence, or neglect in this group)【26†L1071-L1078】. There are efforts to smooth referral processes – for example, some areas use a single referral form for both mental health and substance services. “No wrong door” in Wales means if someone presents at either service, they will be assessed for both and *“fast-tracked”* or jointly managed rather than referred on or turned away. Challenges remain, particularly in rural areas where specialist staff may be few and one service ends up trying to cover gaps of the other. However, the mandated collaboration at the corporate level has driven improvements. Front-line teams report better communication and fewer patients lost to follow-up compared to a decade ago, although consistent delivery still varies by locale.

**Data & Outcomes:** Wales has been developing its information infrastructure to support dual diagnosis care. The framework called for **shared IT systems and data** so that outcomes and activity could be effectively captured across agencies【26†L1029-L1037】. The country introduced the *Wales Integrated In-depth Substance Misuse Assessment Tool (WIISMAT)* – a comprehensive assessment that covers mental health as well, aimed at ensuring thorough evaluation of co-occurring needs【28†L71-L78】. Outcome measurement includes collecting *aggregated data* on key indicators: e.g. improvements in mental health symptoms (often via **HoNOS** or PHQ/GAD scales), substance use reduction (via TOP or similar), and “softer” outcomes like housing status or self-reported well-being【26†L1029-L1037】. The framework specifically mentions using **patient self-assessment tools** (like the “Service User Outcome Lens”) to capture the person’s view of their progress【26†L1029-L1037】. In terms of waiting times, Wales historically monitored two sets: a 28-day referral-to-treatment target for substance misuse services and targets for mental health (including 28 days for a first outpatient mental health appointment). The expectation is that dual diagnosis clients meet both targets – meaning the system must coordinate to prevent people languishing. On outcomes, Welsh services have reported some positive trends: for instance, among those with dual diagnosis engaged in treatment, there have been improvements in reported mental health stability and reductions in substance use frequency. However, formal evaluation noted difficulty in attributing outcomes to the framework without better data linkage. To address this, Wales is rolling out a single integrated data collection for all substance misuse and mental health contacts, which should enable more robust tracking of dual-diagnosis patient outcomes in the future.

**Funding & Commissioning:** Wales has **unified Local Health Boards (LHBs)** that fund and deliver both mental health and addiction services, with oversight from the Welsh Government. This integrated structure helps avoid the split-funding issue seen in England【65†L1-L4】. Each LHB, in partnership with its local authorities, receives dedicated funding for substance misuse via Area Planning Boards. The Service Framework requires that these partnerships have a named executive lead to ensure co-occurring issues are prioritized【26†L1038-L1046】. Funding is directed toward joint initiatives: for example, some APBs allocate a portion of their substance misuse budget to fund mental health liaison posts in drug/alcohol clinics. Additionally, Wales has taken advantage of UK-wide funding (like the Barnett formula consequentials of England’s drug strategy investment) to bolster dual diagnosis projects – e.g. additional residential rehab capacity with integrated mental health support. One ongoing challenge is workforce: despite funding, recruiting enough psychiatrists with addiction expertise or addiction counselors with mental health expertise is difficult (a challenge across the UK). Wales has tried to mitigate this through training rather than creating new specialist teams. Overall, the commissioning in Wales explicitly ties mental health and addiction together under the same performance and reporting framework, which has been key to making dual diagnosis a shared responsibility. Future funding plans (as per the new Substance Misuse Delivery Plan and Mental Health Strategy) include ring-fencing resources for co-occurring conditions, such as money for pilot *“multi-disciplinary hubs”* where psychiatric nurses and addiction specialists jointly see patients. This integrated funding strategy is a strength of the Welsh system, though ensuring funds are used effectively at ground level remains an area for continuous oversight.

## Health & Social Care (HSC) Northern Ireland

**Policy & Models:** Northern Ireland’s smaller, integrated system (five regional Health and Social Care Trusts) also recognizes dual diagnosis as a significant issue. Mental health and addiction services are largely commissioned and provided within the NHS/HSC structure, which in theory simplifies integration【66†L814-L821】. NI’s approach has been influenced by both UK and ROI policies. The regional **“Dual Diagnosis Steering Group”** set up years ago produced guidelines promoting collaborative care and clear referral pathways. The principle of **“no wrong door”** is endorsed: anyone presenting with co-occurring issues should be welcomed by whichever service they enter. In practice, NI has implemented models like **“stepped care”** for co-occurring disorders – for example, mild-to-moderate cases might be managed in primary care with support from addiction liaison nurses, whereas severe cases (e.g. psychosis with substance dependence) are managed by secondary mental health services with addiction specialists embedded. There are also some specialist dual diagnosis initiatives, such as the Belfast Trust’s Dual Diagnosis team which consults on complex cases trust-wide. The ethos is similar to elsewhere: integrated, person-centered, and trauma-informed. Given Northern Ireland’s particular history, trauma-informed care is especially crucial; many service users have trauma related to the Troubles or other adversities, compounding their mental health and addiction struggles. Hence, HSC services emphasize creating psychologically safe, empathetic environments for dual diagnosis patients.

**Service Pathways:** Service pathways in NI involve close cooperation between mental health teams and community addiction teams (often jointly located in community mental health centers). For example, if an individual with bipolar disorder and alcohol misuse comes to a mental health clinic, they will receive mental health treatment and also be connected to an addiction keyworker – the two professionals coordinate care. Similarly, addiction services routinely screen for mental health issues and can directly refer to psychiatric services without sending the person back to a GP. Northern Ireland has **Crisis Resolution/Home Treatment Teams** that include addiction expertise, acknowledging that many mental health crises have a substance-related component. Access to inpatient detox or rehab with concurrent psychiatric care is somewhat limited (there are few dedicated dual diagnosis inpatient units), but mental health inpatient units will manage withdrawal with input from addiction specialists when needed. Overall, NI’s small size has allowed a somewhat personalized approach – professionals across disciplines often know each other and can case-conference easily. Still, gaps exist, particularly for young people (CAMHS and youth addiction services coordination) and for those with personality disorders plus substance use, who can have difficulty finding a service “home.” The HSC has identified these gaps and is working on a new **managed care network** for co-occurring conditions to ensure more consistent pathways across all trusts. 

**Data & Outcomes:** Northern Ireland participates in the UK-wide data monitoring (e.g. it reports to the Public Health Agency on substance misuse treatment outcomes) but does not yet have as unified a database as England’s NDTMS. Each Trust keeps records of dual diagnosis patients, and there have been periodic audits. One audit found that a high percentage of mental health service users had evidence of substance misuse in their history, but only a minority had a formal “dual diagnosis care plan” documented – prompting efforts to improve documentation and outcome tracking. In terms of outcome measures, NI uses similar tools: CORE-OM or HONOS for mental health, and treatment outcome profiles for addiction. The concept of *“distance travelled”* is often captured qualitatively here – e.g. via recovery care plans that note progress in stability, relationships, etc. Waiting times have been strained by resource limitations; mental health services in NI have seen rising demand and some dual diagnosis patients end up waiting for counselling or specialist therapy. To mitigate this, trusts have used interim measures like **peer support groups** or NGO partnership programs (like those run by charities such as MindWise or Addiction NI) to engage individuals while they await formal treatment slots. Outcomes for those who do get integrated care are encouraging – service reports suggest reduced A&E attendances and improved engagement. However, the system acknowledges it needs better **joined-up data** to truly measure impact; a plan to introduce a single assessment and outcome framework for dual diagnosis is being explored as part of a new Mental Health Strategy 2021-2031 action plan.

**Funding & Commissioning:** In NI, the Department of Health centrally allocates funding to Health and Social Care Trusts, which are responsible for both mental health and addiction services (as well as social services). This integrated commissioning structure means dual diagnosis can be tackled within one organizational budget. Trusts typically have a mental health budget that includes addiction services (or closely aligned budgets). One challenge, however, is that funding has been tight; Northern Ireland has historically underfunded mental health relative to need (it has higher rates of mental illness post-conflict, but lower per-capita spending than other UK regions). Within these constraints, trusts have had to prioritize, and sometimes addiction services suffered cuts, indirectly impacting dual diagnosis care. To fill gaps, NI relies on some **cross-sector partnerships**. For instance, the Public Health Agency funds community addiction programmes and some early intervention services (like Targeted Lifeskills for youth) which help on prevention side. There have also been EU-funded initiatives addressing dual diagnosis (before Brexit) that injected some project money (e.g. for training). Commissioning decisions in NI increasingly involve service user input and rights-based approaches, following the new Mental Health Action Plan. For dual diagnosis, this means future funding is likely to be directed at integrated community teams and possibly a pilot of a **secure co-occurring disorders unit** (particularly for offenders with dual diagnosis, as recommended by criminal justice reviews). In summary, Northern Ireland’s unified commissioning gives it a structural advantage, but limited overall funding and capacity have been hurdles. The focus is now on smarter use of funds – combining mental health and addiction efforts so that every pound spent serves both purposes when applicable.

## HSE Ireland (Republic of Ireland)

**Policy & Models:** The Republic of Ireland has recently made significant strides in dual diagnosis policy. In May 2023, the Health Service Executive (HSE) launched its first **National Clinical Programme for Dual Diagnosis**, complete with a formal *Model of Care*【31†L74-L82】【31†L95-L104】. This model establishes integrated care pathways for people with co-occurring mental health disorder and substance use disorder. It calls for a **“clear and integrated”** approach: mental health and addiction services working jointly rather than in silos【30†L9-L17】【31†L99-L107】. Notably, Ireland’s model explicitly uses the term *“no wrong door”*: at the service intake level, no one should be turned away – if one service isn’t equipped, it must guide the person to the appropriate service without bouncing them out【33†L1-L9】. The model envisions **dual diagnosis as a tertiary service** – that is, a specialized support layer that backs up primary mental health teams, CAMHS, inpatient units, and community addiction services【31†L95-L101】. In practical terms, this means new **Regional Dual Diagnosis Teams** are being set up to consult and co-manage complex cases across Ireland. These teams are multidisciplinary (psychiatrists, addiction counselors, psychologists, peer support, etc.) and are under the joint governance of the HSE Mental Health and HSE Addiction services【31†L101-L109】【31†L109-L116】. Trauma-informed care and harm reduction principles are strongly emphasized, as Ireland’s mental health policy *Sharing the Vision (2020)* recognized the need for compassionate, integrated responses for those with dual needs【31†L75-L83】. 

**Service Pathways:** Historically, Ireland’s mental health and addiction services were quite separate – people often found it difficult to have their dual needs met, with anecdotes of being passed between psychiatric services and addiction units. The new Model of Care is directly addressing this by formalizing pathways. For example, under the model, if an individual with schizophrenia and alcohol dependence presents to either service, a **joint care plan** must be developed. The local Community Mental Health Team (CMHT) retains primary responsibility for the patient (since severe mental illness is present), but now a substance misuse clinician can be embedded or consulted to provide addiction interventions concurrently【31†L95-L104】【31†L101-L109】. Conversely, someone with primary addiction and moderate mental health issues would be managed in addiction services with support from mental health professionals. The model also integrates other sectors: liaison psychiatry (for hospital cases), maternity services (recognizing perinatal substance misuse), and NGOs. Ireland is implementing this first in three pilot regions (CHO 3, 4, and 9) as of 2023【67†L175-L179】, with plans to scale up nationwide. Early on, the program is focusing on upskilling staff – e.g. training CMHT members in addiction screening and training addiction staff in basics of mental health pharmacotherapy. **Referral and eligibility** rules are being rewritten: no longer can services use substance use as an exclusion criterion. Instead, eligibility is based on clinical need, and referrals are accepted even if the person is not yet abstinent (they will receive motivational support alongside other treatments). This is a big cultural change in Ireland’s system. Already, some **innovations** have appeared: “dual diagnosis nurse” roles in general hospitals to link patients to community follow-up, and specialized therapy groups (like DBT groups adapted for people with substance use). Over the next few years, Ireland will be watching outcomes from these integrated pathways to refine the approach.

**Data & Outcomes:** As part of the new model, Ireland is developing metrics to monitor dual diagnosis outcomes. Traditionally, mental health outcomes (e.g. symptom scales, hospital readmission rates) and addiction outcomes (e.g. treatment completion, substance use reduction) were tracked separately. The dual diagnosis programme aims to create a unified outcomes framework. For instance, one key measure will be engagement: **percentage of dual diagnosis patients who remain in treatment** for a minimum duration, since dropout has been a major problem historically. Other measures include waiting times from referral to first appointment with a dual diagnosis service, reduction in acute crises (like Emergency Department visits), and patient-reported outcome measures. The HSE has noted that up to **50% of those attending mental health services have a comorbid SUD**【31†L81-L84】, and a similarly high proportion in addiction services have mental health issues. These figures underscore why better outcomes in this population could significantly improve overall service performance. The Model of Care document itself references international evidence: it cites that integrated treatment leads to better retention and clinical outcomes, though it acknowledges more robust data collection is needed【34†L1160-L1168】【34†L1178-L1186】. “Distance travelled” for Irish services will likely be captured in elements like improvement in psychiatric stability (e.g. fewer hallucinations AND fewer drinking days), improved “recovery capital” (stable housing, employment, relationships), and satisfaction surveys. Given this is a new initiative, a formal evaluation framework is built-in – the National Office plans to track outcomes at the pilot sites and publish results. Ireland’s approach to data is also collaborative: they plan to involve service users in defining what outcomes matter (for example, feeling of hope, or self-rated improvement), rather than only using clinician-rated scales.

**Funding & Commissioning:** Funding for dual diagnosis in Ireland is being injected as new development money within both the mental health and addiction budgets. The HSE mental health division and the National Drugs Strategy (under the Department of Health) have jointly sponsored the programme. In 2023, additional posts (consultants, clinical nurse specialists, psychologists, etc.) were funded in the pilot CHOs specifically for dual diagnosis teams. Ireland’s healthcare is tax-funded with a mix of public and some private provision; both mental health and addiction services are primarily public and free at point of use, though addiction services also involve NGOs (often funded by HSE grants). The commissioning here involves **interagency collaboration** – for example, the HSE funds the clinical teams, while some residential programs (like Coolmine Therapeutic Community or St. John of God’s mental health services) partner to provide integrated residential care when needed. The government also looks at European funds or pilot grants for specific projects (e.g. an EU social innovations fund had previously supported a dual diagnosis homeless outreach project in Dublin). Importantly, the new program received backing because of recognition of cost savings: untreated dual diagnosis cases often cycle through costly acute services (ER, police, inpatient). By investing in integrated care, Ireland expects reduced downstream costs. Commissioning is thus oriented towards *“spend now to save later.”* As part of implementation, clear governance has been set: the HSE’s Chief Clinical Officer is overseeing it, and there is cross-departmental support (Justice, Health, etc., especially since a high-level task force on mental health in the justice system also recommended dual diagnosis services)【67†L119-L128】【67†L143-L150】. While still early, the Irish approach represents a **system-wide commitment** to fund and sustain integrated dual diagnosis care, ending the previous scenario where, as one Irish clinician put it, *“too often the patient was sent from Billy to Jack with neither service taking ownership.”* Now, both services share ownership, backed by proper funding and national policy mandate.

## Australia (Selected States)

**Policy & Models:** In Australia, responsibility for health is largely at the state/territory level, but there has been a consistent national trend toward integrating mental health and addiction services for co-occurring disorders (often called *dual diagnosis* or *co-morbidity*). Over the past two decades, Australian policymakers explicitly adopted **No Wrong Door** approaches. For example, the state of Victoria’s *Dual Diagnosis Initiative (VDDI)*, established in 2002, was built on a *“no wrong door” policy with an integrated person-centered service response*【34†L1179-L1187】. This model (the Nexus program in Melbourne) became a reference point nationally. The ethos across Australia is that dual diagnosis is **“everyone’s core business”** in mental health and in alcohol and other drug (AOD) services【57†L35-L38】【58†L79-L87】. Many states created dedicated dual diagnosis *capability projects* – teams or workers whose role was to improve integration. For instance, New South Wales embedded “dual diagnosis specialists” in each Local Health District, Queensland developed dual diagnosis clinical practice guidelines and training, and Victoria and Tasmania implemented policies that any mental health service must treat co-occurring AOD issues as part of its mandate【57†L21-L24】. Trauma-informed care is a strong element in Australian models as well, since research indicated high rates of childhood trauma among those with dual diagnosis. Culturally safe care is emphasized too, especially for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples who experience co-occurring challenges often in context of intergenerational trauma – services have been urged to integrate culturally informed healing practices into dual diagnosis treatment. Overall, Australia’s model can be summarized as **integrated treatment as best practice**: treat both issues simultaneously with a multidisciplinary approach【58†L79-L87】.

**Service Pathways:** The typical pathway in Australia has shifted from sequential to concurrent treatment. Historically, a person might have been told “detox first, then we’ll address your depression,” but now the standard is to attempt treating both together or at least in close coordination【58†L83-L87】. Most public mental health services in Australia will not turn someone away for substance use – instead, they either manage it internally or involve an AOD service. Similarly, public AOD services screen for mental health and either provide basic mental health support (many have on-site psychologists) or fast-track referrals to community mental health. Many regions use an **“integrated care plan”** model where the client, their mental health case manager, and their AOD counselor all meet and agree on a single plan. To facilitate this, some states have co-location of services: e.g. combined “community mental health and drug & alcohol clinics” in certain rural areas. Another innovation is the use of **consultation-liaison dual diagnosis services** – specialized teams (like Victoria’s Nexus, or Queensland’s QuEDS for eating disorders and substance use) that clinicians can call on for complex cases. For patients with severe conditions (e.g. psychosis plus heavy methamphetamine use), assertive outreach and residential rehabilitation are options. Some states developed **specialized dual diagnosis residential programs** (for example, Odyssey House in Victoria runs programs for clients with mental illness, staffed with mental health nurses). Australia also places importance on involving **peer support** and family in pathways – peer workers with lived experience of mental health and addiction are integrated into many services to engage clients and model recovery. Despite progress, challenges remain in the consistency of pathways: a 2019 literature scan noted variability, with some acute psychiatric units still discharging patients if they relapse on substances. However, overall the trend is firmly toward integrated pathways and better continuity between mental health and AOD systems【34†L1160-L1168】【34†L1178-L1186】.

**Data & Outcomes:** Australian states collect a wealth of data on mental health and AOD services, but linking them to evaluate dual diagnosis outcomes has been a work in progress. On the mental health side, the National Outcomes and Casemix Collection (NOCC) includes routine outcome measures (like the Kessler Psychological Distress Scale, HoNOS, etc.), and AOD services often use the Australian Treatment Outcomes Profile (ATOP). Increasingly, services are using these in tandem for dual diagnosis clients. For instance, an individual in a dual diagnosis program might have their mental health symptom scores and substance use days measured at baseline and follow-up to gauge improvement. Research indicates integrated treatment yields better outcomes: one Australian review cited by Ireland’s Model of Care found that integrated approaches were associated with **better engagement and functional outcomes** for people with co-morbid severe mental illness and substance misuse【34†L1160-L1168】【34†L1170-L1178】. However, quantifying this is complex. Victoria’s dual diagnosis initiative reported qualitative outcomes such as improved clinician skills and more seamless patient experiences, but measuring patient-level outcomes remains challenging due to data silos. The federal government’s reports (e.g. the 2019 Productivity Commission report on mental health) pointed out the need to reduce gaps in data to know if co-morbid clients are doing better. Some specific figures: it’s estimated that around **comorbidity prevalence** is very high – the Alcohol and Drug Foundation notes ~50% of people with mental illness have a substance issue and vice versa【58†L30-L38】 – which implies that system-wide metrics like hospitalization rates, incarceration rates, and mortality for mental health patients are heavily influenced by how well co-occurring SUDs are managed. There have been positive case studies: for example, a dual diagnosis community program in Western Australia reported a significant drop in psychiatric hospital bed-days for its clients after a year of integrated care. Australia is also adopting patient-reported outcome measures (PROMs) to capture the “distance travelled” in personal recovery terms. Tools like the **Outcome Star** (adapted to an Australian context) are used in some NGOs to measure soft outcomes (like improved self-care or social connections) for dual diagnosis clients. These complement the more clinical measures to give a full picture of progress.

**Funding & Commissioning:** Funding for dual diagnosis efforts in Australia comes through both state/territory health budgets and Commonwealth (federal) initiatives. Public mental health services and public AOD services are funded by state health departments. Over the years, states have specifically earmarked funds for dual diagnosis training and positions – e.g. employing dual diagnosis coordinators in health services. The Commonwealth has also funded national projects (through the Department of Health or the former National Drug Strategy) to develop resources and research on comorbidity. Commissioning in recent times often involves **joint planning**: state mental health plans and drug/alcohol plans are increasingly linked. For example, Queensland’s Mental Health, Alcohol and Other Drugs Branch oversees both portfolios, enabling unified strategy. There is also a significant role for the **NGO sector**. Many community-based services for dual diagnosis (like residential rehabs, therapeutic communities, supportive housing programs) are run by NGOs with government funding. Often these contracts encourage partnerships with health services – for instance, an NGO rehab must have formal arrangements with a mental health clinic for psychiatric input. The “pie” of funding in Australia is thus spread among public hospitals, community health, and NGOs. An interesting dynamic is the role of **private healthcare and insurance**: While most severe cases rely on the public system, some private hospitals (covered by private insurance) have started dual diagnosis inpatient programs, catering to those who have insurance or can pay – e.g. specialized units that treat addiction and mental illness together in a hospital setting. However, these remain relatively few, and the public sector shoulders most of the burden. In terms of splitting the pie, one key lesson from Australia is that investing in integration (training staff, hiring dual-skilled clinicians) is considered cost-effective. By the late 2010s, all states had recognized that fragmented care leads to higher overall costs (through repeated relapses, crisis care, etc.), so funding is being structured to incentivize integration. For instance, some funding agreements require services to meet certain dual diagnosis capability criteria (like staff having completed specific training) to receive full funding. In summary, Australia uses a mix of targeted funding and broad commissioning expectations to drive dual diagnosis care, with a view that the *sum is greater than the parts* – only by coordinating the mental health and AOD funding streams can outcomes truly improve.

## New Zealand (Aotearoa New Zealand)

**Policy & Models:** New Zealand has been a leader in formally addressing co-existing mental health and addiction problems (often termed **“CEP”** – Co-Existing Problems). As early as 2010, the NZ Ministry of Health released *Te Ariari o te Oranga* and its companion document *Service Delivery for People with Co-existing Mental Health and Addiction Problems: Integrated Solutions*, which laid out a comprehensive framework for integrated care【39†L53-L62】【39†L63-L70】. The philosophy is succinctly captured by the phrase: **“any door is the right door.”** This means whichever service (mental health or addiction) a person turns to, they will receive support for all of their needs, or be seamlessly connected to additional support, without exclusion【39†L22-L30】. New Zealand’s approach is grounded in a **wellbeing orientation** that is person-centered and whānau (family) inclusive. Te Pou (the NZ mental health workforce development center) developed *Te Whare o Tiki*, a knowledge and skills framework for co-existing problems, which aligns with key principles like Engagement, Integrated Care, and Cultural Considerations【39†L37-L45】. Culturally, services strive to incorporate Māori models of health (such as Te Whare Tapa Whā) recognizing that a holistic balance is needed for recovery. Trauma-informed care is also emphasized given the prevalence of trauma among this population. In terms of service design, NZ does not have separate “dual diagnosis units” per se; instead, it has required **all mental health and addiction services to become CEP-capable**. The Mental Health and Addiction Inquiry (2018) and the subsequent new national strategy (*He Ara Oranga*) reinforced that co-existing issues must be addressed together in the reformed health system. With the recent restructuring of NZ’s health system (into Health NZ / Te Whatu Ora and the Māori Health Authority), there’s an expectation of even more integrated planning and funding for mental health and addictions, including CEP.

**Service Pathways:** On the ground in New Zealand, integrated pathways are common. Many DHBs (now under Te Whatu Ora) developed local protocols – for example, if a person with depression and methamphetamine use seeks help, they get a joint assessment and a single care plan that covers both medication for depression and counselling for meth use. NZ’s community mental health teams typically include or liaise with addiction practitioners, and community addiction services similarly employ clinicians with mental health training. A unique feature in NZ is the strong role of **NGOs and peer-support services** in the continuum. For instance, NGOs might run day programs or supported housing where staff are trained in CEP, providing a space where people can work on life skills and recovery from both illnesses simultaneously. Child and Youth services also have begun to address co-existing issues (recognizing early onset of both mental health issues and substance use). **Referrals** in NZ are managed so that a person doesn’t get lost: one service will hold the responsibility until another picks up. A common model is a **“consultancy approach”** – the primary team (say, mental health) stays in charge but brings in an addiction consultant to advise on treatment plan, or vice versa. New Zealand has also championed **training programs** like the CEP e-learning modules and Real Skills Plus (a competency tool) to ensure frontline workers know how to navigate these pathways【39†L28-L36】【39†L33-L41】. For more acute cases, inpatient mental health units are expected to manage withdrawal and substance issues with support from addiction services (rather than deferring treatment). Some regions have special acute options – e.g., a social detox facility with mental health nurse input for those with high psychiatric needs. Overall, service pathways in NZ strive to be seamless, though challenges exist in some areas (especially rural regions) where specialist staff are fewer. Yet the strong policy framework and years of workforce development give NZ a relatively robust CEP pathway system by international standards.

**Data & Outcomes:** NZ has invested in monitoring co-existing problem responses. The Ministry of Health developed Key Performance Indicators that included CEP-related metrics (for example, the percentage of mental health clients screened for substance use). Te Pou’s reports often discuss outcomes like improved stability and reduced risk behaviors for those in CEP interventions. However, similar to other places, quantifying the benefit is complex. Anecdotally and from smaller studies, integrated approaches in NZ have shown improvements in engagement and satisfaction. The use of structured outcomes tools is widespread: mental health services use tools like ADOM (Alcohol and Drug Outcome Measure) alongside Kessler-10 or HoNOS. The **“Outcome Star”** tool has also been introduced by some services to capture progress in areas of functioning as a distance-traveled measure【62†L9-L13】. One specific measure used is the *“Problems and Goals”* approach in which service users identify their top issues (could be “hearing voices” and “stopping drinking”) and periodically rate progress on those – giving a personalized outcome indicator. Waiting times for either service in NZ have been a concern (demand often outstrips supply), but initiatives like primary mental health and addiction brief intervention services have tried to shorten waits and intervene early. A major 2022 Health and Disability Commission report noted gaps in consistent data, recommending improved information systems to track outcomes across mental health and addiction collectively【37†L31-L35】. New Zealand is now moving toward a more unified data system under health reforms, which should facilitate better measurement of CEP outcomes. Notably, New Zealand also values **qualitative outcomes** – the voices of tāngata whai ora (service users) and whānau – to gauge success in dual diagnosis interventions, often captured via storytelling or surveys. In summary, while formal outcome stats may be limited, the consensus in NZ is that integrated CEP care yields better engagement and broader recovery gains; the challenge is to gather and publish more hard data on reduced relapse, hospital use, etc., under the new health system structure.

**Funding & Commissioning:** New Zealand’s health funding (largely public) has integrated streams for mental health and addiction for some time. District Health Boards (DHBs) used to receive bulk funding and then contract both their own services and NGOs. Most DHBs had a single mental health & addictions funding plan, which inherently included a focus on co-existing issues. Now under Te Whatu Ora (Health NZ), funding is even more centralized, with an expectation of eliminating duplication and ensuring equitable services across regions. In terms of commissioning specifics: the Ministry of Health historically allocated ring-fenced mental health budgets (which included addiction). Many NGOs received contracts specifically requiring them to be CEP-capable. For example, a contract for a community support service might stipulate that the provider must have staff trained in both mental health and addiction support. Additionally, workforce development (via Te Pou and Matua Raki – the former addiction workforce center) was funded to provide CEP training to any funded agency, acknowledging that improving care quality is part of the funding responsibility. Another funding aspect is in the justice sector: recognizing the overlap, the government funded joint initiatives like alcohol and drug treatment programs in prisons with mental health components, and mental health courts that address addiction needs too. The “pie” of funding in NZ can be visualized as a single, big pie where slices are not strictly divided by condition but by function (acute care, community care, etc.), allowing flexibility to serve co-morbid needs. That said, providers often note funding is still not sufficient to meet demand – a familiar refrain everywhere. On a positive note, recent large investments (following He Ara Oranga) of over NZ$1.9 billion into mental health/addictions are being used in part to bolster integrated services at primary care level (e.g. the new Integrated Primary Mental Health and Addiction services with Health Improvement Practitioners). These aim to catch co-existing problems early in general practice settings. To sum up, NZ’s commissioning model inherently supports dual diagnosis care by combining mental health and addiction funding and strategy, and current reforms promise even more unified governance, which should further cement the integrated approach.

## Canada (Provincial Health Systems)

**Policy & Models:** In Canada, health services (including mental health and substance use) are delivered provincially, leading to some variation. However, the consensus across provinces is that **concurrent disorders** (the Canadian term for dual diagnosis) require integrated approaches. For example, Ontario’s system has promoted *“concurrent disorder capable”* services: any mental health or addiction program should be able to at least screen, identify, and do basic intervention for the other issue【40†L21-L24】【46†L449-L457】. The Canadian Centre on Substance Use and Addiction (CCSA) published national guidelines emphasizing integrated service delivery as best practice【42†L2-L9】【42†L75-L83】. Many provinces have their own concurrent disorders strategies – e.g. British Columbia’s strategy calls for an evidence-based integrated model, and Alberta developed a *Concurrent Capable Practice Framework* for providers【40†L25-L30】. A key concept is **“no wrong door, no wrong provider”** – meaning whether a person seeks help in an addiction clinic, a psychiatric hospital, or even primary care, they shouldn’t be turned away due to a concurrent condition. Instead, systems use approaches like cross-training staff and creating multidisciplinary teams. *Trauma-informed care* is a foundational principle (recognizing the prevalence of trauma among those with concurrent disorders), as are culturally appropriate services (especially for Indigenous peoples). Notably, some provinces explicitly incorporate Indigenous healing practices and cultural supports in concurrent disorder programs, acknowledging the need for holistic care. Overall, the model in Canada leans toward **integration at the program level**: many health authorities have created specialized programs or teams (for instance, Concurrent Disorders Services within a health authority that support both inpatient and outpatient care). There has also been adoption of specific evidence-based integrated treatments, such as Integrated Dual Diagnosis Treatment (IDDT, originally from the US) especially for those with serious mental illness and substance use.

**Service Pathways:** Canadian service pathways for concurrent disorders often utilize a **stepped care** approach. For milder cases, support might be provided in primary care or by a single service with some consultation. For moderate to severe cases, provinces have developed specialized teams – e.g. **Concurrent Disorder Treatment Teams** that include psychiatrists, addiction counselors, nurses, and sometimes social workers. Someone with, say, bipolar disorder and opioid addiction in Ontario might be managed through a concurrent disorders program at a hospital which provides both psychiatric medication management and addiction counseling in one clinic. In British Columbia, one might access a Concurrent Disorders outpatient group therapy that covers relapse prevention and coping with depression simultaneously. Many regions also deploy **ACT teams (Assertive Community Treatment)** and Intensive Case Management that are concurrent-capable – these mobile teams manage people with severe mental illness and ensure any substance use issues are also addressed (some ACT teams include an addiction specialist). A hallmark of Canadian pathways is the use of **multidisciplinary case conferences**: different specialists coordinate to tailor a plan (for example, detox planning while also maintaining continuity of psychiatric care). Youth concurrent disorder services have also expanded, recognizing the importance of early integrated intervention for adolescents. Despite these, there are still barriers – sometimes waitlists for specialized concurrent programs can be long, and not every community has easy access. Rural areas rely more on generalist providers with telehealth support from specialists. But increasingly, provinces are bridging gaps: for example, Nova Scotia integrated its mental health and addictions clinics administratively, so one referral opens the door to both services. Another example: some provinces have **concurrent disorder inpatient units** for short-term stabilization (e.g. Ontario’s CAMH has a  concurrent disorders inpatient ward for treating acute psychiatric issues and withdrawal together). Pathways also emphasize **aftercare** and community support – e.g. linking patients to peer support groups like Double Recovery (a 12-step fellowship for those with mental illness and addiction) or other community resources upon discharge【10†L21-L25】. Summarized, Canadian service pathways strive for a “one-stop” feel when possible, with a continuum that ranges from integrated counselling groups to highly specialized dual-focused hospital care.

**Data & Outcomes:** At a national level, data show that concurrent disorders are common – by some estimates, around  concurrent conditions affect millions of Canadians. Yet historically, **only a small fraction received care for both issues**. A SAMHSA report (using U.S. data as a proxy) noted only ~7.8% of people with co-occurring disorders received treatment for both disorders【61†L1-L9】, suggesting a large treatment gap. Canadian stakeholders are aware of this gap and have been working to improve it. The CCSA in 2023 reviewed *98 guidance documents* internationally on integrated service delivery and identified common implementation themes, indicating a broad commitment to change【42†L74-L82】【42†L85-L93】. In terms of outcomes, provinces have begun to collect more integrated data. For example, Ontario’s “DATIS” system (Drug and Alcohol Treatment Information System) and the Mental Health OPOC (Ontario Perception of Care tool) are being aligned to capture client outcomes and satisfaction across both domains. British Columbia’s health outcomes include tracking hospitalization rates for people with concurrent disorders (with a goal to reduce ER visits via better outpatient care). “Distance travelled” is often measured in functional terms: employment obtained, stable housing achieved, days of abstinence gained, symptom improvement on scales. Research from academic centers like CAMH (Centre for Addiction and Mental Health) has contributed significantly to evidence on psychosocial interventions, showing integrated approaches can reduce substance use and improve psychiatric stability【46†L438-L447】【46†L449-L457】. In practice, one sees improvements such as reduced psychiatric symptom severity when substance use is simultaneously addressed – for instance, clients with schizophrenia who engage in integrated treatment have been found to have fewer psychotic relapses as their substance use drops. Some provinces have published outcome evaluations of pilot programs: e.g. Alberta’s “Stepping Stones” concurrent disorders program reported improved quality-of-life scores and reduced substance use days at 6-month follow-up. Nonetheless, a commonly cited need is **standardized outcome measures** used consistently. The CCSA also noted in 2023 that while integration is accepted as best practice, evaluating it systematically is challenging and more knowledge-sharing on outcomes is needed across provinces【42†L76-L83】. The trend is positive: increasing use of KPI dashboards that include metrics for concurrent disorder care (like % clients with documented care plans for both issues, or improvement in both MH and SUD symptom scales).

**Funding & Commissioning:** In Canada, provincial ministries of health fund the healthcare system, and mental health and addictions are often organized under a single umbrella (e.g. “Mental Health and Addictions” branches). This means planning and funding for both sectors can be aligned. For example, Ontario’s 2011 ten-year mental health and addictions strategy explicitly included improving services for people with concurrent disorders as a priority, thereby channeling funding to things like concurrent disorder specialists and cross-training initiatives. Many health authorities use **integrated budgeting** where programs are funded to address both sets of issues – for instance, a community health centre might get funding to hire a mental health clinician and an addiction counselor to work as a team. There are also targeted funds: the federal government (through Health Canada’s Substance Use and Addictions Program, SUAP) sometimes issues grants for innovative concurrent disorder projects (like implementing new youth concurrent treatment models). Another component is **philanthropy and NGO contributions** – large centers like CAMH have research and program funds donated which they’ve used to pilot integrated care models that later get adopted system-wide. A crucial aspect of funding in recent years is the push to break down silos: historically, mental health and addiction funding were tracked separately, which could disincentivize one sector from “using” its funds for the other’s interventions. Provinces are mitigating that by joint funding models. For example, British Columbia’s move to a single health authority for each region covering both services means one budget holder. Similarly, Alberta Health Services integrated its Addiction & Mental Health programs over a decade ago, creating unified leadership and pooled budgets. Commissioning in Canada isn’t typically competitive (as the system is largely publicly delivered), but when contracting NGOs, health authorities often stipulate concurrent-disorder capability as a requirement. For instance, a funded addiction agency might be required to have formal links with mental health services or to ensure psychiatric consultation is available – essentially commissioning for integrated care. In sum, funding structures in Canada are increasingly designed to **enable flexibility** so that money can follow the patient’s needs rather than being tied too rigidly to either mental health or addiction silos. The outcome is more collaborative use of resources – for example, sharing costs of a joint training or a shared electronic health record that both mental health and addiction clinicians use. While each province differs, the overall direction is that **the pie is being carved less along service lines and more around client-centered care**, with concurrent disorders recognized as a key focus area for health investment.

## United States (Medicaid/State Systems)

**Policy & Models:** In the United States, care for co-occurring mental health and substance use disorders has historically been fragmented – delivered by separate systems (mental health often state-funded and substance use often via separate agencies and block grants). However, over the last two decades, there has been a strong movement toward **integrated treatment models** for what’s termed *co-occurring disorders (COD)*. SAMHSA (Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration) has led with evidence-based practice toolkits on **Integrated Dual Disorder Treatment (IDDT)** and others【47†L23-L27】【48†L195-L204】. A core principle at the federal level is **“no wrong door”** at the systems level: any entry point to care should link individuals to the appropriate combination of mental health and substance use treatment【49†L7-L13】【54†L1-L9】. SAMHSA’s official policy has been that integrated care is the *preferred model* for co-occurring disorders【48†L193-L201】. Key elements of the U.S. model include stage-wise treatment (meeting people at their stage of change), use of **motivational interviewing**, and **harm reduction** as needed【48†L199-L207】. Many states have adopted these principles in their own ways. For instance, Pennsylvania implemented a co-occurring competency certification for programs, and Ohio developed a statewide Co-occurring Capability Index. States like New York and New Jersey created regulatory provisions to allow one program to treat both disorders (breaking down licensing barriers). Trauma-informed care is essentially standard in modern behavioral health in the U.S., with SAMHSA’s *TIP 57* providing guidance on making all services trauma-informed【60†L11-L18】【60†L13-L17】. Another model aspect in the U.S. is **integrated care within medical settings**: with the Affordable Care Act and Medicaid expansion, there’s been growth in *behavioral health homes* and integrated primary care that treat mental, substance, and physical health together. Overall, the U.S. has a patchwork of systems, but the conceptual model of integrated, no-wrong-door, recovery-oriented care is well-established in policy and best practice guidelines.

**Service Pathways:** Service pathways in the U.S. can vary by state and even county, but some general patterns exist. Many communities now have **co-occurring disorder clinics or teams** – these might be in community mental health centers or in addiction treatment agencies that expanded their services. For example, a community mental health center might add addictions counselors and peer support specialists to create a co-occurring disorders program for clients with serious mental illness and substance issues. Conversely, some addiction rehabs hire mental health professionals to better serve clients with, say, PTSD or bipolar disorder. The ideal pathway is one where a person receives **integrated therapy** (like dual recovery groups or individual therapy addressing both conditions) and possibly integrated medication management (psychiatrists who are comfortable treating addiction, e.g. prescribing buprenorphine for opioid use disorder while also treating depression). In reality, many people still navigate multiple providers: one for mental health, one for addiction. To improve coordination, many states use **case managers or care coordination programs** funded by Medicaid to ensure people don’t fall through the cracks. The **“no wrong door”** approach is often operationalized via cross-system referrals or co-location. For instance, a mental health clinic may have a substance abuse counselor on-site to whom therapists can warm-hand-off a client, rather than just giving a referral slip. Some states implemented **universal screening**: all clients in mental health services get substance use screening and vice versa, as a standard of care【54†L1-L9】. Special populations have tailored pathways: e.g. for those with co-occurring disorders in the criminal justice system, some jurisdictions have mental health courts or drug courts with integrated treatment requirements; for youth, many systems attempt to treat co-occurring issues in youth and family service programs to avoid young people bouncing between juvenile justice, rehab, and psych units. **Medicaid** plays a big role in shaping pathways now, because most SUD treatment and a lot of mental health care for low-income individuals is covered by Medicaid. Some states have **Managed Care Organizations (MCOs)** responsible for both mental health and SUD services, and they often encourage or even require providers to deliver integrated care (for example, requiring mental health clinics to also be certified substance use providers, or vice versa). The result is an increasing number of **“blended” programs**. Still, fragmentation persists in places due to separate licensing and funding streams. Yet, even when providers are separate, an effort is made to coordinate: e.g., an outpatient rehab program might coordinate with a client’s psychiatrist through regular updates or a shared consent. Peer support (like AA/NA or Dual Recovery Anonymous groups) also often complements formal treatment pathways. In summary, U.S. service pathways for dual diagnosis are improving – more integration and coordination – but one’s experience can vary widely depending on local resources and insurance. The trend is that *integrated care is becoming the expected norm,* and innovative models (like “Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics”, CCBHCs, supported by SAMHSA) explicitly require integrated treatment for co-occurring disorders as a condition of participation.

**Data & Outcomes:** The U.S. collects a lot of data at national and state levels (e.g. via NSDUH surveys, treatment episode data sets), but capturing the specific outcomes of integrated vs. non-integrated care has been a challenge. That said, evidence from controlled studies indicates that **integrated treatments yield better engagement and reduced substance use** for individuals with serious mental illness【47†L1-L8】【48†L193-L201】. For instance, integrated dual diagnosis treatments have been shown to increase days of abstinence and decrease psychiatric hospitalizations compared to treatment-as-usual. Many states monitor outcomes like hospitalization rates, housing stability, or employment for people with co-occurring disorders in their programs. SAMHSA’s *National Outcome Measures* (NOMs) for behavioral health include items relevant to co-occurring disorder recovery (e.g. stable housing, no arrests, improved functioning). “Distance travelled” metrics might be indirectly seen in things like the reduction in severity scores on instruments such as the Addiction Severity Index (ASI) or improvements on the BASIS-24 (a mental health outcome measure) when both issues are treated. One discouraging statistic: it’s estimated that a large proportion of people with co-occurring disorders do **not** receive comprehensive care. As mentioned, only about **7–8% receive treatment for both disorders** in a given year【61†L1-L9】, and around 55% receive no treatment at all for either, according to SAMHSA analyses. This highlights a gap in reach. However, among those who do engage in integrated programs, outcomes are positive: studies from the 2000s (Drake et al.) found that integrated group therapy led to higher abstinence rates and better community functioning at follow-up than parallel therapies. States like Texas and Arizona that implemented co-occurring disorder competency statewide have reported improvements such as reduced duplicative services and better client satisfaction. On a system level, some outcome data shows *cost benefits*: for example, Michigan documented that dual diagnosis-capable programs saw lower overall healthcare costs for enrolled patients (due to fewer ER visits) compared to those not in such programs. The continuous push is to improve data systems so that, for instance, Medicaid data can be used to identify co-occurring disorder patients and track outcomes like re-hospitalization or recovery milestones across both mental health and SUD domains.

**Funding & Commissioning:** The U.S. has a complex mix of funding: public (Medicaid, Medicare, state funds, federal block grants) and private (insurance, self-pay). Historically, mental health and addiction funding were siloed – separate federal block grants, separate state agency budgets. This often resulted in fragmented care. Recent changes, however, are helping integrate funding. Many states have moved to **Medicaid managed care** for behavioral health, meaning a single managed care plan might oversee both mental health and SUD treatment benefits. These plans have a financial incentive to treat co-occurring conditions in an integrated way, because it can reduce expensive crises. Some states have obtained Medicaid waivers to support innovative integrated programs (for instance, covering peer support services or integrating physical and behavioral health). The federal government also encourages integration through grant programs; SAMHSA’s grants for *“Treatment for Individuals with Serious Mental Illness, including those with co-occurring substance use”* or the new CCBHC model provide funds to clinics to implement comprehensive care. *Who pays* can depend: a person with co-occurring disorders might have their therapy covered by Medicaid, their residential rehab by a state grant, and their psychiatric meds by Medicare – a patchwork. This is cumbersome, so efforts like the CCBHCs seek to provide a **single comprehensive payment** for a bundle of services. Commercial insurance in the U.S. (via parity laws) must cover mental health and SUD equally, but integrated care can be tricky to bill – often, providers have to bill one service at a time. Some health systems have tackled this by using **collaborative care codes** or integrated care codes where they can bill for care coordination and team case conferences. Another angle is **value-based payment**: a few pioneers are experimenting with outcomes-based contracts for co-occurring disorder care, e.g. a case rate that covers both mental health and SUD treatment for a patient, incentivizing providers to coordinate and achieve better outcomes. Private sector deals also exist in the form of contracts between state governments and private facilities: for example, a state Medicaid might contract with a private dual-diagnosis rehab for beds to treat Medicaid enrollees, rather than building state-run capacity. The *“pie”* in the U.S. often ends up carved by program – e.g. a mental health program’s budget and a separate SUD program’s budget in the same agency. But integrated programming effectively braids those funding streams. Providers that can do both can bill both, which strengthens their sustainability. We also see cross-state arrangements occasionally: for instance, if one state lacks a certain program (like a long-term dual-diagnosis residential center), they might pay an out-of-state facility to take their client (though this is more common for rare conditions). In summary, U.S. funding is gradually aligning with integrated care goals, especially via Medicaid reform. The presence of both public and private payers means a dual focus: ensuring public systems are co-occurring capable (since they often serve the most complex cases) and that private insurance networks also have integrated options. The continuing challenge is reducing bureaucratic barriers – e.g. differing licensing for mental health vs SUD treatment at the state level – but many states have moved to joint licensing to make it easier for providers to offer combined services. As these funding and regulatory barriers come down, providers have more flexibility to design care truly around patient needs, which is the ultimate aim.

## Cross-Border & Commercial Collaboration Examples

Healthcare systems sometimes leverage **international or private-sector deals** to fill gaps in dual diagnosis treatment capacity. A striking example is the partnership between the Netherlands and Scotland’s *Castle Craig Hospital*, a private residential rehab. In the 2000s, Dutch national insurance faced long waiting lists for rehab and limited availability of intensive dual-diagnosis treatment. Castle Craig, a renowned clinic in Scotland, entered into contracts with nearly all major Dutch health insurers, effectively becoming an overseas extension of the Dutch system【15†L9-L18】【17†L129-L137】. **Hundreds of Dutch patients** were treated at Castle Craig – between 1999 and 2006, over 330 Dutch patients went to Scotland for treatment, ~235 of them fully paid by Dutch insurance【15†L9-L18】【15†L11-L19】. This arrangement meant Dutch citizens could access timely, high-quality rehab (total abstinence-based, with integrated psychiatric care) at short notice, whereas they might have waited months back home【15†L25-L33】. The Dutch insurers covered the costs under the EU cross-border healthcare rules, and Castle Craig even established a Dutch office to handle referrals and aftercare, making the process smooth【15†L29-L37】【15†L31-L39】. At one point around 2007, **up to 30 of Castle Craig’s beds (about 10% of Scotland’s total rehab beds) were occupied by Dutch clients**, while Scottish public services were not fully utilizing that capacity【16†L157-L165】【16†L159-L167】. This highlights how differing funding priorities (the Dutch willing to pay €800+ per week for intensive rehab【16†L157-L164】, versus some UK authorities focusing on outpatient methadone) led to cross-border patient flow. It was a *“win-win”* in some respects: Dutch patients got help, and the Scottish clinic kept its beds filled and income steady.

Similar cross-border or inter-system deals occur elsewhere. Within the UK, it’s not uncommon for NHS authorities to fund a patient’s placement in a specialized facility (sometimes abroad) if needed. For example, NHS England occasionally funds complex addiction/trauma cases to attend specialized programs in South Africa or the US if appropriate treatment isn’t available domestically, though this is rare and case-by-case. **Private vs Public** interplay is significant in dual diagnosis care. In many countries, the **majority of residential rehabilitation** is provided by private or charitable organizations, even if the referral and funding come from public systems. For instance, in England and Wales, local authorities often commission beds in charitable rehabs for dual diagnosis clients using public funds (subject to budget constraints). The *“pie”* of treatment funding thus involves public payers purchasing services from private providers. The Castle Craig example simply internationalized that model.

Another angle: some countries or insurers actively **send patients overseas for cost or quality reasons**. In the early 2010s, some UK localities sent high-need dual diagnosis patients to specialized programs in the US because, paradoxically, it could be cheaper or more effective for long-term recovery than repeated short local stays. Similarly, certain small or under-resourced countries (or those with stigma issues) have paid for their citizens to receive addiction/mental health treatment abroad in English-speaking countries. 

Within countries, *carving up the pie* often means dividing roles between sectors. Public systems might handle acute medical stabilization (detox, psychiatric crises) while private/NGO sector takes on rehabilitation and long-term recovery support. For example, in Australia, state hospitals detox a patient then refer them to NGO-run therapeutic communities for rehabilitation, with government grants covering some or all of the cost. In the U.S., a person might get inpatient psychiatric care under public funding, then use private insurance for a 28-day rehab, then go to a sober living home paid out-of-pocket. These mixed pathways rely on coordination – without it, people drop out in transitions. Hence, many systems now create **“continuum of care” agreements**. For instance, a state might contract with a private rehab to guarantee a certain number of beds for public patients and require that rehab to coordinate with public clinics for aftercare. 

The Castle Craig case is instructive for how to broker such deals: they attained recognition by the Dutch authorities (the Dutch College of Healthcare Insurance recognized Castle Craig, making it effectively part of the Dutch system abroad)【15†L39-L47】. This ensured Dutch insurers would reimburse the care. There was some bureaucratic friction (Castle Craig noted Dutch insurers were sometimes slow or reluctant to authorize payment unless waiting lists were “excessive”)【15†L27-L35】【15†L13-L18】. But overall, it showed that with quality accreditation and demonstrating outcomes, a provider can secure international referrals. 

For a program like OTOS or others looking to collaborate or fill gaps, **researching these angles** suggests: explore partnerships across regions or countries where capacity and demand differ. Perhaps a health system could contract a foreign facility for certain specialties (like trauma-focused dual diagnosis rehab) if local supply is lacking. Or domestically, form public-private partnerships to create integrated care networks (e.g. an NHS trust partners with a charity to run a dual diagnosis day program – NHS clinicians and charity peer workers together). The “pie” of funding can be sliced in a way that each partner brings their strength: government ensures baseline funding and oversight, private/NGO brings innovation or capacity, and insurers/payers cover individuals as needed. 

One more example: **transporting patients for treatment** isn’t only Europe. Some U.S. states without specialized programs will pay for residents to go to another state. And in the Middle East, countries like the UAE or Saudi Arabia have occasionally funded citizens to attend addiction/mental health treatment in Western countries (English-speaking) due to stigma at home – effectively an international referral albeit on a case-by-case basis.

The lesson from all these is that *creative funding and commissioning* can greatly expand options for people with dual diagnosis. Rather than each system working in isolation, leveraging deals – whether sending Dutch patients to Scotland, or pooling budgets between NHS and charities – can improve access. It does require aligning standards (Castle Craig had to meet Dutch quality standards and was subject to Scottish Care Commission inspections, satisfying both sides【15†L39-L47】). But when done right, it increases treatment capacity and provides specialized care that might not otherwise be available. For OTOS, considering such partnerships or funding models could be beneficial, ensuring that people aren’t denied effective treatment just because it isn’t provided next door.

## Key Lessons and Best Practices for Dual Diagnosis (What OTOS Can Apply)

- **“No Wrong Door” in Practice:** All systems emphasize that individuals with co-occurring disorders should be welcomed wherever they present – this means developing an **open-door culture** and cross-training staff so that nobody is turned away. Services should proactively address both mental health and substance use rather than saying “we only do one.” Implementing clear screening protocols (e.g. mental health screenings in drug services and vice versa) and compassionate engagement at first contact is critical【54†L1-L9】【66†L831-L840】. OTOS can ensure any staff in contact with clients are equipped to identify and respond to dual needs, making every entry point the *right* one.

- **Integrated Care Teams and Collaborative Planning:** The gold standard is integrated treatment – providing mental health and addiction care **concurrently** by one team or coordinated team. This can be achieved by **multidisciplinary teams** (psychiatrists, addiction counselors, psychologists, peer specialists, etc.) either working in one program or across agencies with a shared care plan【31†L99-L107】【46†L451-L459】. Having a single care coordinator or case manager to navigate the person through services was highlighted as a best practice in multiple systems (e.g. CAMH in Canada notes one stable therapeutic relationship is key to good outcomes)【46†L459-L467】. OTOS should adopt a team approach – perhaps assembling an integrated care team that meets regularly to review dual diagnosis cases and formulate unified care plans addressing all aspects (medication, therapy, social support).

- **Trauma-Informed and Culturally Sensitive Approach:** Trauma-informed care is a consistent theme – many dual diagnosis patients have histories of trauma, and services must be equipped to create safety, trust, and empowerment【60†L11-L18】. This involves staff training in trauma-informed practices, avoiding punitive or judgmental approaches, and offering trauma-specific interventions (like trauma-focused therapy when appropriate). Additionally, being culturally sensitive (as seen in NZ and Canada with Indigenous populations) improves engagement. OTOS can implement policies and training so that every interaction considers the **trauma context** and cultural background of the person, thereby reducing re-traumatization and improving retention.

- **Holistic “Whole-Person” Care:** Successful models go beyond just treating symptoms – they address **social determinants** and support needs (housing, employment, family support). For example, CAMH notes integrated treatment works best when it also helps with housing and vocational assistance【46†L451-L459】. Many systems incorporate case management to help with issues like homelessness or legal problems, recognizing these needs must be met to enable recovery【53†L207-L215】【53†L229-L238】. OTOS should incorporate or liaise with services providing housing support, job training, social support, etc., to ensure clients can stabilize their life circumstances alongside clinical treatment.

- **Clear Pathways and Defined Protocols:** Where integration has succeeded, it often involves formalized **pathways and protocols** (e.g. Wales’ service framework, Ireland’s model of care, Australia’s guidelines). These clarify roles: who does what when a dual diagnosis patient comes in, how referrals are handled, and how joint working is managed. Creating a simple, flow-charted pathway for OTOS – from intake, through assessment, to coordinated treatment and follow-up – will provide clarity. This should include agreements with partner agencies (for instance, an MOU between a mental health clinic and an addiction rehab to coordinate care) to prevent gaps. A *memorandum of understanding* with external providers can outline that OTOS clients will not be rejected on the basis of dual diagnosis and how information sharing and handovers occur【34†L1193-L1200】.

- **Workforce Training and Development:** Virtually every system highlights the importance of **building workforce competencies**. This includes cross-training mental health clinicians in addiction (and vice versa), training in motivational interviewing, cognitive-behavioral techniques for dual disorders, psychopharmacology for addictions, etc.【48†L197-L205】【58†L79-L87】. Also, training in specific evidence-based practices like relapse prevention, contingency management, or DBT for substance users can be useful. OTOS should invest in ongoing staff training to create a *dual-skilled workforce*. Tools like competency frameworks (e.g. Te Whare o Tiki in NZ or Alberta’s Concurrent Capability toolkit) could be adapted to assess and improve staff skills【39†L37-L45】【40†L25-L33】. Supervision and consultation are also key – having experts in dual diagnosis available (possibly via telehealth or partnerships) to advise frontline staff on complex cases, as done in many countries.

- **Use of Peer Support and Family Involvement:** Many jurisdictions leverage **peer support workers** (people with lived experience of recovery from mental illness and addiction) as integral team members. They can engage clients on a level that professionals sometimes can’t, instill hope, and model coping strategies. Likewise, involving families (with consent) can improve outcomes – educating families about dual diagnosis and equipping them to support recovery. OTOS can incorporate peer support specialists in the program and set up family psychoeducation sessions, aligning with the recovery-oriented, person-centered ethos seen globally【39†L23-L31】【58†L95-L103】.

- **Outcome Monitoring and “Distance Travelled” Measures:** To continuously improve, OTOS should track outcomes specific to dual diagnosis. This could include clinical outcomes (reduced substance use, improved mental health symptoms), but also *“distance travelled”* outcomes like improved quality of life, stable housing, or progress on personal goals. Many systems use tools like the Treatment Outcome Profile (UK) or Outcome Star to capture these changes【26†L1029-L1037】【62†L9-L13】. OTOS might implement regular outcome assessments (at intake, during, and post-treatment) and use that data to adjust care plans. Additionally, tracking engagement (how many sessions attended, drop-out rates) and barriers (e.g. waiting times) will identify where the program can be more responsive. Importantly, include the client’s own perception of improvement, as recovery is highly individual. Publishing or at least reviewing these outcome data will help demonstrate effectiveness to funders and pinpoint gaps needing attention.

- **Flexible Funding and Partnerships:** The examples of cross-system deals illustrate that flexible use of funding can greatly enhance service capacity. OTOS should seek partnerships with other providers – whether it’s contracting with a rehab for short-term stays for clients, or partnering with a housing agency for priority placements of OTOS clients – to extend its reach. Making the case to funders that integrated care reduces overall costs (by preventing costly hospital stays, incarcerations, etc.) can unlock resources. Perhaps OTOS can pilot a cost-sharing model with a local health authority or insurer: for instance, if OTOS provides integrated care that reduces ER visits, an insurer might share savings to reinvest in the program. In essence, **advocate for funding models that follow the patient**, not the diagnosis. Demonstrating outcomes will be key here: e.g. showing a reduction in crisis admissions among OTOS service users would strengthen arguments for sustained or increased funding.

- **Addressing Gaps and Equity:** In reviewing these systems, common gaps include: difficulties in serving people with very severe or very complex conditions (like those who are homeless, or have cognitive impairments), and sometimes a lack of services for specific subgroups (like adolescents, women with children, or people with intellectual disabilities plus substance use). OTOS should be mindful of these and build in strategies to address them. For example, having a low-threshold entry (to engage those who might not readily come to appointments), outreach capability (meeting people in the community if they drop out), and tailoring approaches to subpopulations (maybe trauma-specific groups for women, or simplified materials for those with cognitive challenges). Equity means ensuring everyone – regardless of background or severity – can access help. This might involve providing some services in different languages or ensuring cultural representation in staff, which some systems like NZ and Canada prioritize for indigenous communities.

- **Leadership and Continuous Improvement:** Lastly, a less tangible but crucial lesson: where progress has been made, there was often strong **leadership and inter-agency collaboration**. Building a shared vision among stakeholders (health providers, social services, criminal justice, etc.) that dual diagnosis is a priority and that integrated care works, is essential. OTOS can convene stakeholders (perhaps a small steering committee) including representatives from mental health, substance use, healthcare commissioners, and service users, to guide the program. By creating a learning environment – using feedback from clients and results to keep refining the model – OTOS can stay on the cutting edge. 

In conclusion, the comparative insight is clear: **Integrated, person-centered, no-wrong-door care leads to better outcomes** for dual diagnosis, but it requires deliberate design and sustained effort. Systems that have moved the needle combined top-down policy support with ground-level innovation. OTOS can draw on all these lessons – ensuring that organizational barriers never become the patient’s problem, but rather that the system bends to meet the needs of the individual. With strong fidelity to these best practices, OTOS can become a model service that demonstrates what truly coordinated dual diagnosis care can achieve in terms of recovery and improved lives.
