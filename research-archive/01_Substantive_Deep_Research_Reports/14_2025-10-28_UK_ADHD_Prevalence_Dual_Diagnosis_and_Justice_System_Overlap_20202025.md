In England alone, an **estimated 2.5 million people** (around 4.5% of the population) are living with ADHD as of 2025【1†L68-L76】【31†L38-L46】. This includes roughly **5% of children** and **3–4% of adults**, reflecting the typical prevalence observed in high-income countries【31†L38-L46】【26†L163-L170】. Surveys reveal a large gap between those *potentially* affected and those formally diagnosed. According to the Adult Psychiatric Morbidity Survey (England, 2023/24), about **13.9% of adults screen positive** for ADHD symptoms, yet only **1.8% have a professional diagnosis**【3†L111-L119】. This under-diagnosis is especially pronounced in women (who actually showed a slightly *higher* positive-screen rate than men) and in older adults【34†L59-L67】. **Younger adults have the highest ADHD symptom prevalence**: about **1 in 4 of 16–24 year-olds** screened positive in 2023/4, compared to only ~3.7% of those aged 75+【34†L59-L67】. This suggests many older individuals missed diagnosis in youth. Meanwhile, clinical diagnosis rates have been rising – the share of adults screening positive increased from 8.2% in 2007 to 13.9% in 2023【34†L67-L75】, reflecting greater awareness and recognition of adult ADHD. Still, the **majority of people with ADHD remain undiagnosed or untreated**, creating a large reservoir of unmet need【3†L113-L121】. 🟨 *Insight:* The disparity between **~5.8%** of adults who think they have ADHD and only **1.8%** diagnosed highlights a critical gap in access to assessment【3†L111-L119】. **Reducing diagnostic backlogs is essential** to close this gap (see “Waiting Lists” below). 🟩

## ADHD and Substance Use Disorder (Dual Diagnosis)

There is a strong and well-documented link between ADHD and substance use disorders (SUD). **Individuals with ADHD are significantly more likely to develop problems with drugs or alcohol**, often as a result of impulsivity and attempts to self-medicate ADHD symptoms【8†L124-L132】【8†L138-L146】. A 2022 meta-analysis confirmed that **adults with ADHD have more than double the risk of developing a SUD** over their lifetime compared to those without ADHD【8†L104-L112】. Conversely, studies of people in addiction treatment find very high rates of undiagnosed ADHD: analyses indicate between **25% and 45% of patients in substance misuse treatment meet criteria for adult ADHD**【8†L104-L112】. This is dramatically higher than ADHD’s ~2.5–4% prevalence in the general adult population【5†L172-L180】【26†L163-L170】. In other words, *about one in four people receiving addiction treatment likely has ADHD*, as supported by UK clinical data in 2025【8†L101-L110】. (For comparison, only ~4% of UK adults have ADHD overall【26†L163-L170】, making ADHD **5–10 times more common in addiction settings** than in the general population.🟦)

These statistics underscore a critical dual-diagnosis issue: **ADHD and addiction frequently co-occur**, yet traditionally our treatment systems address them separately. Many people with ADHD turn to substances (e.g. cannabis, alcohol, stimulants) to cope with restlessness or impulsivity, often before they ever receive an ADHD diagnosis【8†L138-L146】. In fact, around **12% of substance misuse patients were found to have undiagnosed ADHD** in earlier clinical research【3†L169-L174】, and more recent UK data suggest the share could be even higher (closer to 1 in 4)【8†L104-L112】. Conversely, among individuals diagnosed with ADHD, roughly **21–23% have had a substance use disorder** at some point【7†L5-L8】, more than double the rate in the general population. 

Despite this overlap, **ADHD often goes unrecognized in addiction services** – clinicians report that traits like impulsivity or poor adherence are misattributed to “difficult” behavior rather than seen as flags for underlying ADHD【8†L124-L132】. This lack of integrated care means many patients cycle through rehab or relapse because the root neurodevelopmental issues are unaddressed【8†L155-L164】. 🟨 *Insight:* The high co-morbidity (e.g. **25% of addiction patients have ADHD**【8†L104-L112】) suggests that **routine ADHD screening in addiction services** could improve outcomes. Without recognizing neurodiversity, treatment programs may label ADHD-driven behaviors as non-compliance, leading to repeated relapse【8†L153-L162】. **Adjusting addiction treatment models to account for ADHD (e.g. providing more flexible, less routine-bound approaches) is *essential***【8†L155-L164】【8†L160-L168】. 🟩 

## ADHD in the Criminal Justice System

ADHD is **over-represented in criminal justice populations**, contributing to what has been called an “iceberg” of undiagnosed neurodiversity in offending populations. Estimates suggest roughly **one in four prisoners in the UK has ADHD**, far above the ~2–4% prevalence in the general population【5†L172-L180】【20†L710-L718】. A landmark evidence review by HM Inspectorate in 2021 cited that **around 25% of prisoners meet ADHD diagnostic criteria**【20†L710-L718】, and other research found **31% of ex-prisoners screened positive** for ADHD symptoms【3†L169-L174】. Many of these individuals were never diagnosed or treated while in the community – indeed an estimated **80% of incarcerated people with ADHD have no prior diagnosis**【5†L214-L222】. This means prisons contain a large “hidden” ADHD cohort, often manifesting as challenging behavior.

Untreated ADHD can contribute to criminal behavior or institutional misconduct through impulsivity, low frustration tolerance, and difficulties adhering to rules【5†L185-L193】【5†L200-L207】. Prisoners with ADHD are up to **8 times more likely to be involved in aggressive incidents** in custody, according to experts, making jails harder to manage if ADHD isn’t addressed【5†L185-L194】. Conversely, treating ADHD has significant benefits: evidence shows that providing medication or psychological interventions to offenders with ADHD can **reduce criminal reoffending by ~32% in men and 41% in women**【5†L213-L221】. In other words, properly supporting neurodivergent offenders is a crime reduction strategy. 🟦

Recognizing this, the justice system has started to respond. In 2022, a major report by the ADHD Foundation and partners sounded the alarm on ADHD in prisons【5†L180-L188】, prompting calls for universal ADHD screening at prison entry【5†L173-L181】. By 2024 the Ministry of Justice rolled out dedicated **Neurodiversity Support Managers in every public prison in England & Wales**【15†L104-L112】【15†L170-L178】. These specialists train staff to identify and support inmates with conditions like ADHD or autism, and early reports show positive impacts (e.g. specialized neurodiversity units like one at HMP Pentonville saw **reductions in violence** after implementation)【15†L147-L155】【15†L149-L157】. Additionally, over **180,000 neurodiversity screenings** have been conducted in prisons since 2021【15†L147-L155】. 

However, gaps remain in earlier stages of the justice pathway. In probation and community rehabilitation, neurodevelopmental conditions are still often missed. There is no routine ADHD screening for individuals serving community sentences or on probation, despite some estimates that **at least 15% of offenders under community supervision have ADHD traits** (data is limited, but likely significant). Many offenders with ADHD had histories of school exclusion or undiagnosed issues that contributed to offending【23†L99-L107】【23†L115-L123】. The **school-to-prison pipeline** for neurodivergent individuals is a real concern: for example, children with untreated ADHD may drift into antisocial behavior, especially if they also fall into substance misuse, leading to youth justice involvement. Indeed, a UK study found **43% of youth (14-year-old) offenders met criteria for childhood ADHD**【21†L15-L23】 – a staggering figure. 

Importantly, neurodivergent people in the justice system also have distinct needs for rehabilitation. Traditional programs may not suit ADHD individuals unless adjustments are made (e.g. shorter sessions, more engaging formats). The government’s **Neurodiversity Action Plan (2021)** acknowledges this and calls for “routine screening” and tailored support across policing, courts, and probation【17†L21-L30】. 🟨 *Insight:* Given that **up to half of UK prisoners are neurodivergent** (covering ADHD, learning disabilities, etc.)【15†L124-L132】, addressing neurodevelopmental needs is **key to reducing reoffending**. **Expanding ADHD screening and treatment into probation services and courts** *should* be a priority to catch people earlier in the cycle. This would require closer Justice–Health collaboration, as recommended by cross-government initiatives【30†L279-L288】. 🟦 

## Regional Variation and Service Capacity

Across the UK, the surge in ADHD awareness and referrals since 2020 has strained services, with significant regional variations in capacity and waiting times. **England** has begun systematically tracking ADHD waiting lists: as of June 2025, there were roughly **491,000 open referrals** for ADHD assessment recorded in mental health services (68% adults, 32% children)【31†L71-L79】, plus an additional ~177,000 potential cases in community health pathways【31†L73-L81】. In total, **up to 668,000 people in England may have been waiting for an ADHD assessment** by mid-2025【31†L79-L87】 – a staggering backlog that has grown rapidly. Over **65% of adults and 62% of children on these lists had been waiting more than a year** for an assessment, with only around 8–11% having waited under 13 weeks【31†L99-L107】. Many adults face waits of **4–8 years** for diagnosis, far exceeding NHS targets【30†L214-L223】【30†L225-L233】. Such delays can be devastating, especially for those whose life course (education, employment, relationships) may deteriorate while they await help【8†L159-L167】【8†L165-L173】.

Other parts of the UK report similar challenges. **Scotland**, for example, does not yet routinely publish ADHD-specific waiting times, but combined neurodevelopmental data show that as of March 2025, **over 42,000 children and 23,000 adults** were waiting for some form of neurodevelopmental assessment (which includes ADHD and autism) in Scotland【25†L25-L29】. This indicates tens of thousands of Scots in queue, and press reports suggest some adults have waited *5+ years*. **Wales** has acknowledged extraordinarily long waits as well – prior to new investments, waits of **4 years or more** for an ADHD assessment were not uncommon【27†L1-L9】. In one anecdote, an adult in Wales was told the waiting list was **12 years** long【27†L29-L33】 (though emergency measures aim to cut this). **Northern Ireland** faces its own backlog, as an NI Assembly review in 2024 noted rising referrals and under-resourced services【26†L164-L172】【26†L185-L193】.

Regional **service provision** also varies. Within England, some Integrated Care Boards (ICBs) have *no specialist adult ADHD clinic*, relying on out-of-area providers, while others have multiple options. For instance, Mid & South Essex ICB recently commissioned **five new ADHD providers** to expand adult diagnostic capacity【45†L197-L205】【45†L212-L221】, whereas other regions have fewer choices. Some areas make use of the NHS Right to Choose policy, allowing GPs to refer patients to private partners (like Psychiatry-UK) for faster assessments – but take-up differs across the country【13†L23-L32】. In Scotland and Wales, neurodevelopmental services are often integrated within general mental health or CAMHS, but pilot projects are underway for dedicated pathways【28†L75-L83】【11†L17-L25】. The **inequity** is clear: a patient’s waiting time and support can depend heavily on where they live and how proactive their local health board or ICB has been in bolstering ADHD services.

In England, by contrast, much responsibility has fallen to local ICBs – leading to patchy improvements. In the interim, many are left without support – often until crises emerge. **Policymakers should treat ADHD backlogs as an urgent clinical priority**, as recommended by the NHS ADHD Taskforce (to *“act quickly to address growing backlogs… to avoid wasted expenditure on the adverse outcomes of untreated ADHD”*【30†L309-L317】). 🟩 

## Commissioning and Funding Responsibilities

The organizational responsibility for ADHD services in the UK is **split across different sectors**, which has led to gaps in care for those who don’t neatly fit one remit. In the NHS (especially England’s ICS structure), ADHD assessment and treatment are generally commissioned as part of mental health services. For children and teens, this falls under CAMHS or pediatric services; for adults, under adult mental health/neurodevelopmental services (usually provided by NHS mental health trusts or authorized partners). These services are funded via the NHS (Integrated Care Boards in England, Health Boards in devolved nations) and thus ultimately by the Department of Health and Social Care (DHSC) or devolved health budgets. Historically, ADHD services, especially for adults, were **not well-prioritized** – many areas lacked dedicated clinics, and GPs often struggled to find where to refer adults. This is now changing with targeted initiatives (e.g. NHS England’s independent **ADHD Taskforce** in 2023–24 to review services【30†L214-L223】).

In **parallel**, **substance misuse (addiction) services** have a completely separate commissioning route – especially in England. Since 2013, local authority Public Health teams commission the bulk of community drug and alcohol treatment (often contracting charities like Change Grow Live (CGL), Turning Point, etc.). These services are funded from a ring-fenced public health grant (under DHSC in England, or via devolved governments). Their contracts historically focus on addiction counseling, detox/maintenance, and harm reduction. *They do not routinely cover neurodevelopmental diagnosis or psychiatric treatment like ADHD medication.* In practical terms, an adult with addiction in treatment under a local authority contract **cannot get their ADHD diagnosed or medicated through that service** – it’s outside the service scope and staff expertise. Instead, they would be referred to NHS mental health for ADHD, which – as noted – often has long waits or might require the patient to achieve abstinence first. This creates a **commissioning gap:** neither the NHS nor local councils “own” the full problem of dual ADHD-addiction patients. Each may view the other as responsible (the NHS saying the person needs to address substance use first; the addiction service saying mental health must handle the ADHD). The **case of Cambridgeshire & Peterborough** (CPFT NHS trust vs. CGL charity service) is emblematic: the two operate under different funding streams, making it challenging to provide *joined-up* care for co-morbid clients. 🟨

Similarly, in the **criminal justice system**, responsibilities are divided. The Ministry of Justice funds prisons and probation, but healthcare in prisons is commissioned and funded by the NHS (through NHS England’s Health & Justice division for England/Wales prisons). This means forensic mental health teams or contracted healthcare providers are supposed to address conditions like ADHD for inmates. In reality, prison healthcare has focused on severe mental illness and primary care, and only recently is ADHD being recognized (with support of the new Neurodiversity Support Managers)【15†L111-L119】. Probation services can flag neurodivergence, but they rely on community NHS services to actually diagnose or treat it. There is no dedicated ADHD budget in probation – if someone on probation needs ADHD assessment, they join the same NHS waiting list as anyone else. 

The **funding streams** for ADHD-related needs thus run on parallel tracks: one through NHS mental health budgets, another through local authority budgets for public health (addiction), and yet another through MoJ/HMPPS for custody support. There is no single accountable commissioner for “ADHD in addicted offenders,” for example – those individuals fall between stools. Recent policy reviews have noted this silo issue. The NHS ADHD Taskforce’s interim report explicitly called for **cross-departmental coordination**: *“government departments (DHSC, MoJ, DfE, DWP) need to work together on radical, holistic spending plans”* including invest-to-save models for ADHD across sectors【30†L279-L288】. In other words, saving money on reoffending or unemployment via ADHD treatment may require one department (e.g. health) to spend, while another (justice or welfare) reaps the savings. At present, such cross-sector incentives are weak. 🟦

One example of siloed funding is the issue of **dual diagnosis (mental health + substance misuse)** in England. General mental health policy often excludes those whose primary issue is substance misuse (“you need to address your addiction first”), while addiction services often exclude those with acute mental illness (“we’re not funded to treat that”). For severe mental illnesses like schizophrenia, there have been efforts to create integrated dual-diagnosis teams. But for ADHD – which has not traditionally been seen as “severe mental illness” – there is *no established dual-diagnosis pathway*. Neither the typical adult ADHD clinic nor the addiction service is resourced to take full responsibility. This is a glaring gap, given the high prevalence of co-morbidity noted above. 

Funding for **ADHD services within the NHS** is generally part of overall mental health allocations. There have been some targeted boosts: e.g. NHS England in 2023 announced an **Independent ADHD Review** and the aforementioned Taskforce, partly in response to public pressure about waiting times【33†L15-L23】. Some ICSs have used elective recovery funds or transformation funds to contract private providers to tackle waiting lists (as seen in Essex, Kent, etc., where companies like Psychiatry-UK are commissioned)【45†L197-L205】【45†L212-L221】. But these are interim measures. There is not yet a ring-fenced national ADHD budget. By contrast, **Wales**’ £13.7m investment in 2025【28†L27-L35】 and prior £12m over 3 years【28†L51-L59】 specifically target neurodivergence services, showing a strategic commitment. 

On the local authority side, funding for drug and alcohol treatment in England has seen recent boosts (the Government’s Drug Strategy in 2021 pledged an extra £780m over 3 years for treatment expansion). Within that, however, **little if any is earmarked for neurodiversity screening or treatment**. Commissioners could choose to spend some of the new money on training staff in ADHD or piloting joint clinics, but this has not been standard. 

In summary, the commissioning map is fragmented: **NHS vs Local Authority vs Justice each cover different pieces**, and people with overlapping needs frequently fall through. This fragmentation is illustrated by Figure 1 above, which shows how an individual with ADHD and addiction might navigate separate systems. The lack of an integrated funding model means innovative solutions (like co-located ADHD clinics in drug services) have relied on local champions or short-term grants. There is a clear need for more cohesive strategy and funding that bridges health, social care, and justice for neurodivergent populations. 🟨 *Insight:* **No single agency “owns” ADHD support for people in addiction or criminal justice.** This vacuum leads to people bouncing between services or getting lost entirely. Assigning *joint accountability* (for example, through Integrated Care Systems working with local councils and criminal justice partners) could ensure the “catchment gap” is closed. The forthcoming recommendations will address this. 🟦

## The “Catchment Gap”: Individuals Falling Between Systems

For people who have **both ADHD and substance use issues**, the current system can feel like a maze with no exit. They are often shuttled between mental health and addiction services, each requiring the other problem to be addressed first. This is the classic **catch-22** of dual diagnosis. Figure 2 below illustrates a typical scenario in the journey of such an individual:

【43†embed_image】 *Flow of an individual with ADHD and addiction seeking help.* **Figure 2:** The “Catchment Gap” flow diagram. A person with co-occurring ADHD and substance use may be bounced between an ADHD clinic (which asks for sobriety first) and an addiction service (which cannot treat ADHD). Without integrated intervention, the individual risks relapse or offending, until a crisis (e.g. arrest) forces belated identification of ADHD. 🟦

In practice, someone with unmanaged ADHD may self-medicate with drugs or alcohol, then seek help when their life becomes unmanageable. If they approach an **ADHD service or psychiatrist**, they might be told: *“We need your substance use to be stable before we can diagnose/treat ADHD.”* Active addiction can indeed complicate an ADHD assessment, but this stance leaves the person in limbo. So they then attend an **addiction service** for their substance problem. There, the staff might recognize signs of ADHD (difficulty sticking to programs, extreme impulsivity) but **they typically cannot prescribe ADHD medications or make a formal diagnosis** – their contract doesn’t cover it. The best they can do is refer the client back to mental health for an ADHD assessment, which (as we’ve seen) could be years away. In the meantime, the standard addiction program (with its structured routines and cognitive behavioral approach) may not “click” for someone whose ADHD is untreated【8†L155-L164】. The person may be labeled as non-compliant or chronically relapsing, when in fact their untreated neurodivergence is undermining their recovery【8†L157-L165】.

This gap can lead to tragic outcomes. Many such individuals **drop out of treatment and continue to misuse substances**, with escalating consequences. Some enter the **criminal justice system** due to drug-related offenses or impulsive crimes. It is often only **after incarceration** that a thorough assessment uncovers the underlying ADHD (e.g. through a court liaison & diversion team or in prison healthcare)【19†L61-L69】【19†L43-L51】. By then, opportunities for early intervention have been missed. One clinician described this as *“falling through the net that lies between mental health and addiction services”* – a net that currently has large holes.

Notably, Public Health England (PHE) admitted in a 2023 briefing that **neurodevelopmental conditions remain “an under-resourced component” of the national mental health strategy**【8†L165-L173】. In other words, the system hasn’t caught up with the reality that conditions like ADHD profoundly affect (and are affected by) other public health issues such as addiction. The Royal College of Psychiatrists warned in 2023 that without adjustments for ADHD in addiction treatment, *relapse rates will remain high*【8†L157-L165】. The **human cost** is huge: these are people who might have been successful in life if their ADHD was supported, but instead cycle through costly services (rehabs, A&Es, courts) ineffectively. 

Bridging this gap requires **integrated care models** – for example, some areas have trialed having an ADHD specialist work within a drug treatment service【11†L11-L19】, or fast-tracking ADHD assessments for those in rehab. There are also calls for **multi-agency case management**, where a single care plan addresses both ADHD and SUD concurrently rather than sequentially. Thus far, these remain exceptions rather than the rule.

🟨 *Insight:* The “catchment gap” is essentially a **policy gap** – no one agency is tasked (or funded) to treat co-occurring ADHD and addiction together. Individuals are often left to navigate two siloed systems on their own, which many cannot. **Establishing shared pathways and funding for dual diagnosis (including ADHD+SUD) could drastically improve engagement and outcomes** (e.g. reducing drop-out and reoffending). It would also be cost-effective in the long run: as the ADHD Taskforce noted, failing to treat ADHD early leads to “wasted expenditure” later on (in health crises, incarcerations, etc.)【30†L309-L317】. 🟩

## Strategic Recommendations and Insight Classification

To address the findings above, a multi-pronged strategy is required. The following actions are recommended, categorized by **🟩 DO (urgent actions)**, **🟨 SHOULD (strategic initiatives)**, and **🟦 COULD (longer-term or evidence-gathering measures)**. These correspond to control system tasks, master plan items, and evidence base building, respectively, as per Boardroom v7(2) insight routing:

- **🟩 DO:** **Rapidly expand ADHD diagnostic and treatment capacity** – The NHS should **immediately invest in backlog clearance** for ADHD assessments, using an *invest-to-save* approach. Waiting lists of 4+ years are unacceptable【30†L309-L317】. For example, commissioning extra providers (as some ICBs have done【45†L197-L205】) and deploying nurse-led or digital assessment models can bring waits down. This will prevent “wasted” costs on crises stemming from untreated ADHD (ER visits, mental health crises, criminal justice)【30†L309-L317】.

- **🟩 DO:** **Implement routine ADHD screening in addiction and justice settings** – All substance misuse services and all prison/young offender intakes should **screen for ADHD** as standard (using tools like the ASRS). Given the high prevalence (20–30% range) in these groups【8†L104-L112】【20†L710-L718】, this will identify hidden cases. Those who screen positive **must have expedited referral pathways** to an ADHD evaluation (e.g. a fast-track clinic for individuals in treatment or on probation). *This is a controllable operational step* that many services could adopt quickly with training. It aligns with the 2021 joint inspection recommendation for routine neurodiversity screening in the CJS【17†L21-L30】. 

- **🟩 DO:** **Pilot integrated dual-diagnosis clinics** – Establish demonstration projects where an ADHD specialist (psychiatrist or nurse prescriber) is embedded within an addiction service, or vice versa. These clinics would provide *one-stop* assessment and treatment for people with co-morbid ADHD and SUD, avoiding the ping-pong between services. Initial pilots could be funded through joint commissioning by ICSs and local authorities (using drug strategy funding). **Evidence from such pilots should be collected to inform broader rollout**. 🟦 (This builds the evidence base on integrated care effectiveness.)

- **🟨 SHOULD:** **Create joint commissioning frameworks** for neurodiversity in adults – ICSs, local authorities, and criminal justice agencies *should* develop shared protocols and pooled budgets for people who fall between systems. For instance, an ICS and council could share the cost of an ADHD nurse within the council’s drug service – recognizing that both will benefit (health outcomes and crime reduction). Similarly, NHS England and MoJ *should* collaborate on ensuring prisoners with ADHD receive continuity of care on release (so that those leaving custody on ADHD medication don’t lose treatment due to community wait lists)【5†L225-L231】. This may involve formal agreements or Integrated Care System plans that explicitly include adult ADHD and dual diagnosis.

- **🟨 SHOULD:** **Train and equip staff across sectors** – Frontline staff in addiction, criminal justice, and primary care *should* receive training to recognize ADHD symptoms and understand referral pathways. Many clinicians report that once they are aware of a patient’s neurodivergence, they can adjust their approach with better results【8†L138-L146】【8†L155-L164】. For example, probation officers who understand ADHD can better support compliance with orders. Training could be rolled out as part of the neurodiversity programs (e.g. the prison Neurodiversity Support Managers could extend training to probation and community partners). Ensuring every drug and alcohol service has at least one staff member with advanced training in ADHD *should* be a short-term goal. 🟦 (This recommendation is supported by evidence that knowledge gaps contribute to people being mislabeled “difficult” rather than being helped【8†L124-L132】.)

- **🟨 SHOULD:** **Adopt needs-based (not diagnosis-based) support** – Early support for individuals showing ADHD-like traits *should* not always wait for a formal diagnosis (which can be delayed)【30†L204-L212】【30†L298-L307】. Education, employment services, and social services can implement accommodations for those with attentional difficulties. For instance, job centers could offer coaching or neurodiversity screenings for clients with chaotic work histories. Schools *should* continue improving support for neurodivergent pupils to prevent exclusions (as high exclusion rates among ADHD youth often lead to later justice involvement【23†L115-L123】). This principle of *“no wrong door”* support aligns with the ADHD Taskforce’s call for **needs-led support uncoupled from diagnosis** to break the school-to-prison pipeline【30†L298-L307】. 

- **🟦 COULD:** **Enhance data and research** – Continue building the evidence base by linking datasets across health, justice, and education to track outcomes for people with ADHD【30†L279-L288】. ONS *could* routinely include ADHD status in its surveys【30†L279-L287】. Research is needed on regional prevalence differences, long-term outcomes of treated vs untreated ADHD in offender populations, and cost-benefit analyses of integrated care. This evidence will help target investments where they have most impact (for example, quantifying how much treating ADHD in a drug user reduces relapse or crime rates will strengthen the case for funding those interventions). 

- **🟦 COULD:** **Foster peer support and advocacy** – People with lived experience of ADHD and addiction or offending *could* be engaged as peer mentors or “recovery navigators.” Several charities and user groups (e.g. ADHD Foundation, ADDISS, local neurodiversity charities) exist and can help bridge trust gaps. This isn’t a substitute for professional treatment, but can improve engagement and adherence.

Finally, **strategic oversight** is needed. A cross-departmental taskforce (building on the current NHS ADHD Taskforce) should steer these changes, ensuring that **funding responsibility is clarified** and not perpetually kicked between services. The recommendations above collectively aim to close the prevalence-treatment gap, address the dual-diagnosis “blind spot,” and reduce the downstream impact on the justice system. With prevalence of ADHD in youth and adults now clearly documented and rising【34†L67-L75】, the period of neglect must end. The data and insights from 2020–2025 make a compelling case that investing in ADHD services (particularly for those at risk of addiction or offending) will yield dividends in public health, safety, and economic productivity. 🟩 **DO: It is time to move from acknowledging these issues to funding and implementing solutions on the ground.**
